8.1 Introduction

Several methods are available for estimating Value at Risk (VaR), each with distinct advantages and assumptions:

  1. Historical Simulation: This approach uses past data to simulate potential changes in portfolio value, calculating VaR based on the worst observed losses at a specified confidence level.

  2. Parametric (Analytical) Approach: Assuming that asset returns follow a normal distribution, this method employs the mean and standard deviation of returns to calculate VaR. It can be applied to both individual assets and portfolios, incorporating asset correlations.

  3. Monte Carlo Simulation: By generating random price paths for assets, this method simulates thousands of possible portfolio outcomes. VaR is then determined from the distribution of simulated losses.

  4. Extreme Value Theory (EVT): EVT models the tail of the loss distribution, making it particularly useful for calculating VaR at very high confidence levels where extreme losses are of primary concern.

8.2 Historical Simulation

The historical simulation method for VaR evaluates the current portfolio by revaluing it based on historical changes in key risk factors over a specified lookback period. The process involves:

  1. Decomposition and Data Collection: The portfolio is broken down into individual risk factors, and historical returns for each factor are collected over the lookback period.
  2. Repricing: The current portfolio is repriced using the returns for each day in the lookback period. This generates a range of simulated portfolio values.
  3. Sorting Results: These simulated outcomes are sorted from the largest loss to the greatest gain.
  4. VaR Calculation: To estimate a one-day VaR at a given confidence level (e.g., 5%), a cutoff point is selected where 5% of the outcomes result in larger losses, representing the VaR threshold.
  5. Expected Shortfall (ES): To calculate ES, the average of all losses that exceed the VaR threshold is taken, providing a view of the tail risk beyond VaR.

This approach leverages real historical data, providing a non-parametric view of potential losses, although it assumes that historical trends will adequately represent future risk scenarios.

Example

Consider a 500-day time window of daily returns for the SPY ETF to calculate the 1-day VaR and ES at a 99% confidence level. The table below lists the 10 worst daily returns from this period:

No. Date Log Returns
1. 2024-08-05 -0.029556
2. 2022-11-02 -0.025416
3. 2022-12-15 -0.024766
4. 2024-07-24 -0.022923
5. 2022-11-09 -0.020817
6. 2024-09-03 -0.020794
7. 2023-02-21 -0.020265
8. 2024-08-02 -0.018794
9. 2023-03-09 -0.018622
10. 2022-12-05 -0.018153

In this example:

  • 1% of 500 observations equals 5, meaning the 5th worst loss represents the 1-day 99% VaR.
  • VaR: The daily 99% VaR is the 5th worst return, which corresponds to -0.020817.
  • ES: The daily 99% ES is the average of the 4 worst returns, -0.025665, providing an estimate of the average loss beyond the VaR threshold.

This method leverages historical returns to identify potential extreme losses based on recent market behavior.

8.2.1 Stressed VaR and Stressed ES

In the historical simulation method, VaR and ES are usually based on recent data, known as current VaR and current ES. To account for risk under extreme conditions, regulators introduced stressed VaR and ES, which use data from a particularly adverse 251-day period for the current portfolio. This period yields 250 scenarios, each reflecting changes in market variables between consecutive days.

  • Stressed VaR: The one-day 99% stressed VaR is the midpoint loss between the second and third worst scenarios.
  • Stressed ES: The one-day 99% stressed ES is calculated as the average of the two worst losses.

8.2.2 Accuracy of VaR

The historical simulation method for Value at Risk (VaR) relies on a finite set of observations, introducing estimation error. The standard error for the \(q\)-percentile of the distribution, estimated as \(x\), is given by:

\[ \text{Standard Error} = \frac{1}{f(x)} \sqrt{\frac{(1 - q)q}{n}} \]

where \(n\) is the sample size and \(f(x)\) represents an estimate of the probability density at \(x\). Generally, the standard error for VaR estimates from historical simulation is high, decreasing as sample size grows or confidence level lowers. Loss distributions often have heavier tails than the normal distribution, so using a Pareto distribution for \(f(x)\) can yield more accurate standard error estimates.

Example
  1. We aim to estimate the 99th percentile of a loss distribution using 500 observations, so \(n = 500\) and \(q = 0.99\).
  2. Assuming a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of $10 million.
  3. In Excel, the 99th percentile is calculated as \(\text{NORMINV}(0.99,0,10) = 23.26\).
  4. The density \(f(x)\) at this point is \(\text{NORMDIST}(23.26,0,10,\text{FALSE}) = 0.0027\).
  5. The standard error of the estimate is:

\[ \frac{1}{0.0027} \times \sqrt{\frac{0.01 \times 0.99}{500}} = 1.67 \]

  1. If the historical simulation estimate of the 99th percentile is $25 million, a 95% confidence interval is calculated as:

\[ 25 \pm 1.96 \times 1.67 \]

This results in an interval from $21.7 million to $28.3 million, providing an estimate range for the 99th percentile at a 95% confidence level.

8.2.3 Extensions and Computational Issues

VaR and ES calculations rely on recent historical data under the assumption that this provides a reliable forecast of future behavior. Traditional historical simulation assigns equal weight to each past observation, but there are ways to refine this approach:

  • Weighting of Observations: Recent data can be weighted more heavily to reflect current volatility and macroeconomic conditions. Exponential weighting, where weights decay over time, is commonly used for this purpose.
  • Volatility Scaling for Market Variables: Incorporating volatility estimates into historical simulations provides additional accuracy. For instance, using the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) allows dynamic monitoring of the standard deviation of simulated losses.
  • Bootstrap Method: This involves creating multiple simulated datasets by sampling observations with replacement, providing a robust estimate of potential outcomes.

Calculating VaR and ES through historical simulation requires revaluing the entire portfolio, often through multiple rounds of simulation (e.g., 500 runs), which can be computationally demanding. For portfolios with complex instruments valued via Monte Carlo simulation, this can lead to a “simulation within a simulation” scenario, further escalating computational load.

To reduce processing time, financial institutions may employ an approximation technique using a Taylor series expansion, which simplifies the calculations while preserving accuracy.

8.2.4 Extreme Value Theory

Extreme Value Theory (EVT) is a method for estimating the tails of a distribution, based on the power law, which can enhance Value at Risk (VaR) or Expected Shortfall (ES) calculations, especially at high confidence levels. EVT works by smoothing and extrapolating the tails of an empirical distribution, yielding more accurate tail estimates that better reflect the distribution’s extreme values.

Applying EVT to the probability distribution of daily portfolio changes derived from historical data mitigates calculation uncertainties. Even with limited datasets (e.g., 500 days), EVT can provide reliable VaR or ES estimates at very high confidence levels, such as 99.9%.

8.2.5 Advantages of the Historical Simulation Method

  • Simplicity and Transparency: The method is intuitive and straightforward, relying on historical data to model potential future scenarios.
  • Non-Parametric Approach: Unlike parametric methods, historical simulation does not assume a specific distribution (e.g., normal) for returns, making it resilient to non-normal returns.
  • Captures Market Volatility: By using actual historical data, this method naturally incorporates periods of high volatility, producing realistic VaR estimates during turbulent times.
  • Empirical Foundation: Historical simulation relies on observed data rather than theoretical assumptions, offering a more grounded view of extreme event risk.
  • Flexibility: The approach can easily be adjusted to accommodate various time horizons, confidence levels, and other parameters as needed.
  • Adaptability to New Data: As new data becomes available, it can be seamlessly integrated into the model, allowing continuous updates to reflect evolving market conditions.
  • Capability with Options: Unlike parametric methods, historical simulation can account for options and other non-linear instruments, enhancing its applicability to a broader range of portfolios.

8.3 Parametric Approach

The parametric method, also known as the analytical or variance-covariance method, is an alternative to historical simulation for estimating risk measures like VaR and ES. It assumes that the return distributions of portfolio risk factors follow a normal distribution, requiring only the expected value and standard deviation as parameters, which are estimated using historical data and adjusted for consistency with long-term values.

This approach is efficient when portfolio value changes are linearly dependent on market variable changes, and when these changes can be assumed to be multivariate normal, making it computationally faster than historical simulation. This method builds on Harry Markowitz’s portfolio theory but is not suitable for portfolios with non-linear derivatives. While other distributions can be used to capture more complex behaviors, they add computational complexity. It’s also possible to calculate stressed measures by choosing appropriate historical data to estimate volatilities and correlations.

8.3.1 The Basic Methodology

Assuming a normal distribution of returns, VaR and ES can be calculated with the following equations:

\[ \text{VaR} = \mu + \sigma \times \sqrt{T} \times N^{-1}(X) \]

\[ \text{ES} = \mu + \sigma \times \sqrt{T} \times \frac{e^{-(Y^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} (1 - X)} \]

where:

  • \(\mu\) is the mean of losses, typically assumed to be zero,
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of losses,
  • \(X\) is the confidence level,
  • \(N^{-1}(\cdot)\) is the inverse cumulative normal distribution,
  • \(Y\) is the \(X\) th percentile of the standard normal distribution,
  • \(T\) is the time horizon in days, where for extended horizons, using an appropriate return frequency is recommended.

The standard deviation of a multi-asset portfolio is calculated by:

\[ \sigma_P = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \rho_{ij} \delta_i \delta_j \sigma_i \sigma_j} \]

or equivalently:

\[ \sigma_P = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i^2 \sigma_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} \rho_{ij} \delta_i \delta_j \sigma_i \sigma_j} \]

where:

  • \(\rho_{ij}\) is the correlation between assets \(i\) and \(j\),
  • \(\delta_i\) is the weight of asset \(i\) in the portfolio,
  • \(\sigma_i\) is the standard deviation of asset \(i\)’s returns.

This approach efficiently computes portfolio risk in linear and normally distributed portfolios but is less accurate for portfolios with non-linear payoffs, such as those involving options.

8.3.2 Parametric Approach Example

Asset A

Consider a portfolio consisting of one share of Asset A valued at $10 million, with a 10-day time horizon and a 99% confidence level for VaR. The assumptions are as follows:

  • Daily volatility: 2%, or $200,000.
  • Expected change: Assumed to be zero.
  • Distribution: Changes in value are assumed to be normally distributed with a mean of zero and a standard deviation of $200,000.

Calculations:

  • One-day 99% VaR: \(2.326 \times \$200,000 = \$465,300\).
  • 10-day 99% VaR: \(2.326 \times \$200,000 \times \sqrt{10} = \$1,471,300\).
  • 10-day 99% ES:

\[ \text{ES} = \$200,000 \times \sqrt{10} \times \frac{e^{-(2.326^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} \times (1 - 0.99)} = \$1,686,994 \]

Asset B

Now consider a portfolio with one share of Asset B valued at $5 million, using the same 10-day horizon and 99% confidence level. The parameters are:

  • Daily volatility: 1%, or $50,000.
  • Expected change: Assumed to be zero.
  • Distribution: Changes in value are normally distributed, with a mean of zero and a standard deviation of $50,000.

Calculations:

  • One-day 99% VaR: \(2.326 \times \$50,000 = \$116,300\).
  • 10-day 99% VaR: \(2.326 \times \$50,000 \times \sqrt{10} = \$367,800\).
  • 10-day 99% ES:

\[ \text{ES} = \$50,000 \times \sqrt{10} \times \frac{e^{-(2.326^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} \times (1 - 0.99)} = \$421,400 \]

These examples illustrate the application of the parametric method for estimating VaR and ES, where the assumption of normality enables direct calculation based on volatility and confidence level.

Two-Asset Case

Assume a portfolio consisting of Asset A and Asset B with a correlation of 0.3. The combined standard deviation (\(\sigma_{X+Y}\)) of two variables \(X\) and \(Y\), given their individual standard deviations (\(\sigma_X\) and \(\sigma_Y\)) and the correlation coefficient \(\rho\), is calculated as:

\[ \sigma_{X+Y} = \sqrt{\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2 + 2 \rho \sigma_X \sigma_Y} \]

Using the values from the previous example for Asset A ($200,000) and Asset B ($50,000):

\[ \sigma_{A+B} = \sqrt{200,000^2 + 50,000^2 + 2 \times 0.3 \times 200,000 \times 50,000} = \$220,227 \]

Calculations:

  • One-day 99% VaR: \(2.326 \times 220,227 = 512,300\)
  • 10-day 99% VaR: \(512,300 \times \sqrt{10} = 1,620,100\)
  • 10-day 99% ES:

\[ \text{ES} = 220,227 \times \sqrt{10} \times \frac{e^{-(2.326^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} \times (1 - 0.99)} = 1,856,100 \]

Benefits of Diversification

  • 10-day 99% VaR for Asset A: $1,471,300
  • 10-day 99% VaR for Asset B: $367,800
  • 10-day 99% VaR for both assets combined: $1,620,100

The difference between the sum of individual VaRs and the combined VaR represents the diversification benefit:

\[ (1,471,300 + 367,800) - 1,620,100 = 219,000 \]

Thus, $219,000 is the amount of risk diversified away due to the less-than-perfect correlation between the assets. If both assets were perfectly correlated, the combined VaR would equal the sum of the individual VaRs, with no diversification benefit. This benefit applies to both VaR and ES measures.

Note: For non-normal distributions, the VaR of a combined portfolio can sometimes exceed the sum of the individual VaRs. Expected Shortfall (ES) does not exhibit this limitation, making it a more robust measure under such conditions.

8.3.3 Parametric Approach vs. Historical Simulation

Advantages of Parametric Approach

  • Allows for rapid calculation of VaR and ES.
  • Integrates easily with procedures for updating volatility and correlation, adapting quickly to changing market conditions.
  • While volatility adjustments can be applied in historical simulation, they are more naturally incorporated in the parametric approach.

Disadvantages of Parametric Approach

  • Assumes that changes in portfolio value are linearly related to proportional or actual changes in risk factors, which may not hold in practice.
  • Assumes that daily changes in risk factors follow a multivariate normal distribution, whereas real market distributions often deviate from normality, exhibiting heavier tails.
  • Highly sensitive to parameter estimates, especially covariances in portfolios, which can lead to inaccuracies.
  • Less suitable for portfolios containing options due to their non-linear payoff structures, which are challenging to model with a parametric approach.

8.4 Monte Carlo Simulation

The Monte Carlo simulation method allows users to define custom assumptions about distribution statistics, generating random hypothetical returns to estimate a portfolio’s risk. It is widely used for pricing complex options and handling investments with multiple uncertain variables due to its flexibility in accommodating non-normal distributions.

  • Applicability to Complex Portfolios: Ideal for portfolios with multiple assets and complex risk interactions, Monte Carlo simulation does not rely on the normal distribution assumption.
  • Customizable Reliability: Users select the number of random values to simulate; a larger number increases the accuracy of results but requires more computational time.
  • Correlation Considerations: Simulations must account for correlations among variables to avoid independent, unrealistic scenarios.
  • Process: The method involves simulating risk factor returns, revaluing portfolio exposures under these returns, and then sorting the results to identify a percentile for VaR estimation.
  • Accuracy for Non-linear Instruments: The flexibility of Monte Carlo simulation allows it to handle any distribution, making it especially effective for portfolios containing options or bonds with embedded options, where parametric methods fall short.

Monte Carlo’s adaptability makes it a powerful tool for accurate risk estimation, though it demands significant computational resources, particularly for highly detailed simulations.

8.4.1 Procedure for Monte Carlo Simulation to Calculate VaR and ES

  1. Define Assumptions: Specify assumptions about the statistical distribution of returns for each risk factor in the portfolio (e.g., mean, volatility, skewness). Monte Carlo simulation allows for any distribution, making it suitable for complex portfolios and non-linear instruments like options.

  2. Generate Random Scenarios: Decide on the number of random scenarios to simulate. Each scenario represents a possible outcome for the portfolio’s value. A higher number of scenarios improves accuracy but increases computation time.

  3. Simulate Risk Factor Returns: For each scenario, generate random returns for all risk factors in the portfolio, ensuring the correct distribution characteristics and incorporating the correlations between risk factors.

  4. Reprice Portfolio: For each simulated scenario, revalue the portfolio based on the simulated returns. This step may involve complex calculations, particularly for non-linear instruments, as it requires calculating how each risk factor affects portfolio value.

  5. Calculate Portfolio Losses: Determine the portfolio’s loss or gain for each scenario by comparing its revalued amount to its original value. Sort these losses from largest to smallest.

  6. Estimate VaR: Identify the loss at the desired percentile to estimate VaR. For example, for a 99% confidence level, VaR is the loss at the 1st percentile of sorted losses, meaning only 1% of simulated losses are expected to exceed this amount.

  7. Calculate ES: Expected Shortfall is calculated by averaging the losses that exceed the VaR threshold. For a 99% confidence level, ES is the average of the worst 1% of simulated losses.

This procedure allows for robust risk estimation by accommodating any return distribution and accounting for complex interactions within the portfolio, making it particularly suited for portfolios with non-linear or path-dependent instruments.
