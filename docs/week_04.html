Key Insight

VaR tells you the frequency of bad events and the threshold but nothing about the severity when that threshold is breached.

In crises, what kills firms isn’t the frequency of bad days—it’s the magnitude of losses on those days.

Theory: Conditional Expectation in the Tail

Expected Shortfall Definition

Expected Shortfall (ES) measures the average loss during bad days when things go worse than your VaR threshold.

Also called Conditional VaR (CVaR) or Expected Tail Loss (ETL), ES answers a critical question: When we have a really bad day that exceeds our VaR limit, how bad should we expect it to be?

ES is always worse than (or equal to) VaR because it’s the average of all the losses in the tail, including the very worst outcomes.

Mathematically, for confidence level \(\alpha\): \[ES_\alpha = E[L \mid L \geq VaR_\alpha]\]

This is the average of all losses in the worst \((1-\alpha)\) portion of the distribution, including the VaR observation itself.

More precisely for continuous distributions: \[ES_\alpha = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \int_{VaR_\alpha}^{\infty} L \cdot f(L) \, dL\]

For discrete historical data with \(n\) observations: \[ES_\alpha = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} L_i\] where:

  • \(k = \lceil n(1-\alpha) \rceil\) is the number of worst losses to average
  • \(L_1 \geq L_2 \geq ... \geq L_k\) are losses sorted from worst to best
  • \(L_k = VaR_\alpha\) (the k-th worst loss is the VaR)

Block B: Expected Shortfall - Measuring Tail Risk

Theory: Calculating Expected Shortfall

Historical Simulation Method for ES

Step 1: Sort all daily returns from worst to best

Step 2: Determine how many tail observations to include:

\[k = \lceil n \times (1-\alpha) \rceil\]

Step 3: Calculate the average of these \(k\) worst observations: \[ES_\alpha = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} L_i\]

Step 4: Convert to dollar loss for your portfolio value

Parametric Approach for ES (Normal Distribution)

For normally distributed returns:

Step 1: Calculate distribution parameters from your data:

  • Sample mean: \(\mu\)
  • Sample standard deviation: \(\sigma\)

Step 2: Apply the ES formula for normal distribution: \[ES_\alpha = -(\mu - \sigma \times \frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1-\alpha})\]

where:

  • \(z_\alpha\) is the standard normal quantile at confidence \(\alpha\)
  • \(\phi(z_\alpha)\) is the standard normal PDF evaluated at \(z_\alpha\)

The PDF can be calculated as: \[\phi(z_\alpha) = \frac{e^{-z_\alpha^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\]

Excel implementation:

  • For PDF: NORM.DIST(z_α, 0, 1, FALSE)
  • For z-score: NORM.INV(1-α, 0, 1)
  • Complete ES formula: =-1*(mean - stdev*NORM.DIST(NORM.INV(1-α,0,1),0,1,FALSE)/(1-α))

Note: The negative sign converts P/L returns (where losses are negative) to positive loss values, matching our VaR convention.

Step 3: Convert to dollar loss for your portfolio value

Real-world Implications

The subadditivity property matters because:

  • It ensures diversification is always rewarded
  • Risk limits can be allocated consistently across desks
  • Portfolio risk is never understated when aggregating positions
  • Capital requirements properly reflect risk reduction from diversification

Theory: Regulatory Context - From VaR to ES

Basel III’s Shift to Expected Shortfall

The regulatory evolution:

  • Basel II (until 2016): Market Risk Capital based on VaR(99%, 10-day) with multiplier of 3
  • Basel III (2016-present): Market Risk Capital based on ES(97.5%, stressed) with multiplier of 1.5

Key change: After the 2008 crisis, regulators switched from VaR to ES because:

  • VaR missed tail severity (banks failed despite “acceptable” VaR)
  • ES is mathematically coherent (rewards diversification)
  • ES is harder to manipulate

Stressed calibration requirement:

Basel III requires banks to calculate ES using both:

  • Recent period: Last 250 trading days
  • Stressed period: The historical 250-day window that produces highest ES for current portfolio

\[\text{Capital} = 1.5 \times \max(ES_{recent}, ES_{stressed})\]

The stressed period is found by testing rolling 250-day windows across historical data (typically capturing crisis periods like 2008 or March 2020).

Note: We’ll explore the full regulatory framework in detail in a later seminar. For now, these exercises demonstrate why regulators made this shift.

Block C: Monte Carlo Simulation for Risk Measurement

Theory: From Historical Data to Simulated Futures

Monte Carlo Methods in Risk Management

Why simulate?

  • Historical data is limited (only one path of history)
  • Need to stress test scenarios that haven’t happened yet
  • Can incorporate complex dependencies and dynamics

Basic Monte Carlo for VaR/ES:

  1. Specify a distribution for returns (normal, student-t, etc.)
  2. Generate thousands of possible scenarios
  3. Calculate portfolio value for each scenario
  4. Extract VaR and ES from simulated distribution

Industry practice uses more sophisticated models:

  • Stochastic Volatility: Volatility itself is random (GARCH, Heston)
  • Jump Diffusion: Sudden jumps in addition to normal movements
  • Regime Switching: Different distributions in calm vs stressed periods
  • Copulas: Complex dependency structures between assets

Real-World Implementation

Banks typically use:

  • 10,000+ scenarios minimum
  • Multiple risk factors (not just single asset)
  • Historical simulation with bootstrapping
  • Filtered Historical Simulation (combining historical data with volatility models)

The trade-off: More scenarios = more accuracy but slower calculation. For intraday risk, banks often use fewer scenarios for speed.

Block D: Case Study - UBS and the 2007-2008 Financial Crisis

UBS, Switzerland’s largest bank, suffered the biggest losses of any European bank during the 2007-2008 financial crisis. Despite sophisticated risk models and AAA-rated assets, the bank lost over $37 billion on mortgage-related securities. This case illustrates how VaR models can catastrophically fail and why Expected Shortfall matters.

Before the Crisis (2005-2007):

  • UBS held $50+ billion in “Super Senior” CDO tranches (the safest part of mortgage securities)
  • These were rated AAA - supposedly safer than US government bonds
  • Risk models (like VaR) showed minimal danger because:
    • Historical data (2003-2006) showed tiny default rates
    • Mortgages across different regions were assumed independent
    • The bank bought insurance from monoline insurers as protection
    • Assumed normal distributions and stable correlations

During the Crisis (2007-2008):

  • October 2007: UBS announces $3.4 billion loss (first major bank to report)
  • Full year 2007: $18.7 billion in writedowns
  • Q1 2008: Another $19 billion in losses
  • Stock price fell 70%, CEO and entire senior management resigned

Why Everything Failed:

  • US housing collapsed nationwide (not regionally as models assumed)
  • AAA-rated securities became worthless
  • Insurance companies failed (they insured the same mortgages)
  • Market liquidity vanished - couldn’t sell at any price
  • Losses exceeded any historical scenario by 10-20x

The Key Lesson

VaR’s blind spot: It tells you the threshold you won’t exceed 99% of the time, but nothing about what happens in that 1%.

UBS’s fate: The 1% tail event wasn’t just slightly worse than VaR - it was catastrophically worse. This is why regulators switched to Expected Shortfall after the crisis.

Remember: Risk models work well in normal times. They fail precisely when you need them most - during crises when correlations spike, liquidity vanishes, and “impossible” events happen.

Key Takeaways

  • VaR’s fatal flaw: It tells you nothing about severity when breached—ES measures what actually happens in the tail

  • Coherence matters: ES always rewards diversification (subadditive), while VaR can punish it, leading to perverse incentives

  • Distribution choice is critical: Normal distributions underestimate tail risk; fat-tailed distributions (Student-t) better capture extreme events

  • Monte Carlo extends our toolkit: When historical data is limited, simulation helps explore scenarios that haven’t happened yet

  • Regulatory evolution has a reason: Basel III’s shift to ES wasn’t arbitrary—it directly addressed failures revealed in 2008

  • Models fail when you need them most: UBS lost $37B despite “safe” VaR—risk models work in normal times but break in crises when correlations spike and liquidity vanishes

  • The practical bottom line: Always ask “If things go bad, HOW bad?”—that’s ES, and it might save your firm
