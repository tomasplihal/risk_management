Block A: Economic Capital and RAROC

Theory: Beyond Regulatory Minimum

Economic Capital

  • Economic capital is the bank’s own, model-based estimate of how much capital it needs to cover unexpected losses over one year at a chosen confidence level.
  • Regulatory capital is the minimum capital required by supervisors under Basel rules, based on standardized or approved internal models.

Banks often link the confidence level for economic capital to a target external rating:

Rating Default Probability Implication for economic capital
AAA 0.01% Very high capital: losses covered in almost all scenarios
AA 0.02% High capital: can absorb very severe losses
A 0.05% Solid capital: can absorb severe but less extreme losses
BBB 0.20% Moderate capital: can absorb typical stress scenarios

Choosing a higher target rating (e.g. AAA instead of BBB) means the bank wants default to be more unlikely, so it must hold more economic capital to make sure the modeled 1‑year loss exceeds capital only in very rare states of the world.

In practice you compare available capital with two benchmarks:

  • Regulatory capital requirement (Basel): external minimum you must satisfy
  • Economic capital requirement (internal): capital needed to support the target rating

The binding constraint is the larger of the two. If available capital just meets the regulatory minimum but is below the economic capital requirement, the bank is economically undercapitalized even though it is formally compliant with regulation.

Economic capital aggregates all main risk types from previous weeks:

  • Market risk (Weeks 5–6): one-year VaR/ES based on trading and ALM positions
  • Liquidity risk (Week 8): cost of funding or forced liquidation in stress
  • Credit risk (Week 9): default and migration risk on loans and derivatives
  • Operational risk (Week 10): loss distribution for fraud, systems, legal, etc.
  • Business / strategic risk: model, reputational and business-model risk

Mathematical View: Economic Capital as a Risk Measure

Let \(L\) denote the random loss of the bank over one year (positive = loss). Denote by \(\mathbb{E}[L]\) the expected loss (EL), which should be covered by pricing and provisions, and by \(\rho(L)\) a risk measure of the loss distribution capturing unexpected losses.

A general way to define economic capital is

\[ \text{EC} = \rho(L) - \mathbb{E}[L], \]

so that EC is the extra capital buffer, on top of EL, needed to protect the bank against rare but severe losses. Two common choices for \(\rho(\cdot)\) are Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall.

Link to target rating: if the bank targets a one-year default probability \(p^*\) (e.g. \(p^* = 0.03\%\) for an A rating), it typically chooses \(\alpha = 1 - p^*\). Then, using either VaR or ES as \(\rho(\cdot)\), the economic capital level \(\text{EC}\) is calibrated so that the modeled probability that losses exceed EL + EC is roughly \(p^*\), matching the desired external rating.

Aggregating Economic Capital

If we simply add economic capital across risks

\[E_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E_i,\]

we implicitly assume perfect correlation across risk types and business units. This is usually too conservative, so banks use a simple correlation-based aggregation:

\[ E_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} E_i E_j \rho_{ij}}, \]

where \(\rho_{ij}\) is the (low to moderate) correlation between risks \(i\) and \(j\). This produces a lower, diversified total EC.

The simple sum therefore overstates required capital, while the fixed correlation-matrix approach can understate it, because correlations between risks tend to jump toward 1 in crises and losses become more tightly linked in the tails. In practice, banks try to correct for this by using stress scenarios and portfolio models (sometimes with copulas or stressed correlations) to capture higher dependence in bad states of the world when sizing economic capital.

RAROC: Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital

RAROC measures economic profitability per unit of economic capital:

\[ \text{RAROC} = \frac{\text{Revenues} - \text{Costs} - \text{Expected Losses}}{\text{Economic Capital}} \]

  • Numerator: risk-adjusted profit (after expected losses)
  • Denominator: economic capital allocated to the business unit

Interpretation:

  • RAROC > cost of equity → value creation
  • RAROC < cost of equity → value destruction

RAROC lets you compare very different businesses (retail, trading, asset management) on a common, risk-adjusted basis and decide where to grow or shrink.

Block B: The Evolution of Bank Regulation

Big Picture: Why Regulation Keeps Changing

Bank regulation tries to balance two goals that are in tension:

  • Keep banks safe enough to avoid costly crises and taxpayer bailouts
  • Allow banks to provide credit, liquidity, and risk transfer to the real economy

Before 1988, each country had its own simple capital-to-asset rules. These were easy to apply but did a poor job of capturing the risks from international lending, derivatives, and securitization. Large banks could also engage in “regulatory shopping” by booking business in countries with weaker rules.

The Basel Committee’s frameworks (Basel I, II, II.5, III and now FRTB) can be read as a sequence of patches to visible weaknesses:

  • Basel I introduces a simple, global standard but is too crude.
  • Basel II makes capital more risk-sensitive but relies heavily on banks’ own models.
  • The Global Financial Crisis reveals that both capital levels and risk measures were inadequate, especially in trading books and off-balance-sheet exposures.
  • Basel II.5 and Basel III push for more and better-quality capital, add liquidity and leverage constraints, and try to address model risk and procyclicality.
  • FRTB focuses specifically on tail risk and liquidity risk in the trading book.

Basel I–III and FRTB in One Narrative

  • Basel I (1988) – “simple buckets”

    • Introduces risk-weighted assets (RWA) with a small number of risk weights (0%, 20%, 50%, 100%) and an 8% minimum capital ratio.
    • Strength: first common international standard; recognizes that not all assets are equally risky.
    • Weakness: the buckets are very crude (AAA = B-rated corporate), and initially only credit risk is covered.
    • Unintended consequence: encourages regulatory arbitrage through securitization and balance-sheet engineering (move “good” assets off balance sheet, keep risky exposures, but report lower RWA).

  • Basel II (around 2007) – “more risk-sensitive, more complex”

    • Introduces internal ratings-based (IRB) approaches and an explicit Three Pillar structure:
      • Pillar 1: minimum capital
      • Pillar 2: supervisory review
      • Pillar 3: market discipline via disclosure
    • Strength: better differentiation of credit risk, inclusion of operational risk, more alignment with banks’ own models.
    • Weakness: heavy reliance on model assumptions and historical data; capital tends to be lowest in good times and highest in bad times (procyclicality).
    • Unintended consequence: complex rules and model freedom create scope for underestimating tail risk and for “optimizing” models to reduce capital.

  • Global Financial Crisis (2007–2009) – “stress test of the system”

    • Trading books, securitizations, and off-balance-sheet vehicles produce losses far beyond what models predicted.
    • Market liquidity evaporates; positions that looked “safe” under normal assumptions become impossible to unwind.
    • Many banks meet formal capital requirements right before the crisis, but are clearly undercapitalized once stress hits.

  • Basel II.5 and Basel III (2010–2023) – “more and better-quality capital, plus new constraints”

    • Basel II.5: adds stressed VaR and an incremental risk charge to force trading book models to reflect crisis conditions.
    • Basel III:
      • Raises the level and quality of capital (CET1, capital conservation and countercyclical buffers).
      • Introduces a non-risk-weighted leverage ratio as a backstop.
      • Adds liquidity ratios (LCR, NSFR) to address funding and market liquidity risk.
    • Strength: banks enter the next crisis with more and better-quality capital, less reliance on short-term wholesale funding.
    • Weakness: still complex, still model-based; some risks (e.g. cyber, conduct, climate, business-model risk) remain only partially captured.
    • Unintended consequences:
      • Some activities move into the less regulated “shadow banking” sector.
      • Banks change their asset mix in response to risk weights and leverage constraints.

  • FRTB (Fundamental Review of the Trading Book) – “tail risk and liquidity”

    • Replaces VaR with Expected Shortfall (ES) at 97.5% for regulatory capital.
    • Introduces liquidity horizons: capital charges are higher for risk factors that are slow to hedge or unwind in stress.
    • Tightens the boundary between trading book and banking book to reduce arbitrage between the two.
    • Goal: make capital more responsive to extreme moves and illiquidity, not just day-to-day volatility.

Block C: Too Big to Fail: G-SIBs, D-SIBs, TLAC and CoCos

Even with better rules, some institutions are so large and interconnected that their failure threatens the system as a whole. This is the “too big to fail” problem.

Systemically important banks

  • Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs) are very large, internationally active banks whose failure would have global repercussions.
  • Domestic Systemically Important Banks (D-SIBs) are critical for a particular national banking system, even if they are not global players.
  • More broadly, regulators talk about Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFIs), which can include large insurers and other non-banks.

These institutions face capital surcharges on top of Basel III minimums (for G-SIBs, roughly +1–3.5% CET1, depending on their systemic importance bucket). The idea is:

  • They benefit from an implicit “too big to fail” funding advantage.
  • In return, they must hold more loss-absorbing capacity and prepare credible resolution plans.

For G-SIBs, regulators also require Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity (TLAC):

  • TLAC consists of equity plus certain long-term debt that can be written down or converted into equity in resolution.
  • The goal is to enable a “bail-in”: shareholders and eligible creditors absorb losses, rather than taxpayers, while the essential parts of the bank continue to operate.

Contingent convertible bonds (CoCos) are a prominent example of bail-in style instruments:

  • They are bonds that convert into equity or are written down when a trigger is hit (for example, when the bank’s CET1 ratio falls below a threshold, or when regulators decide the bank is no longer viable).
  • Investors receive higher coupons to compensate for the risk of conversion or write-down.
  • If they meet certain criteria, they count as Additional Tier 1 (AT1) capital.

From different perspectives:

  • Banks like CoCos because they are cheaper than equity in normal times but help strengthen capital in stress.
  • Regulators like them because they can provide automatic recapitalization and reduce the need for sudden dilutive equity issues or taxpayer support.
  • Investors bear the downside: in a severe stress, they can lose some or all of their investment.

Key Takeaways

  • Economic capital and RAROC provide an internal view of risk and performance: they aggregate market, credit, liquidity, operational, and business risks and allow banks to compare business units on a common, risk‑adjusted basis.
  • Regulatory capital frameworks (Basel I → Basel II → Basel III/FRTB) are best seen as a series of “patches” to visible weaknesses and arbitrage, improving risk sensitivity and resilience but also increasing complexity and creating new incentives.
  • Systemically important institutions (G‑SIBs, D‑SIBs, SIFIs) and tools like TLAC and CoCos are designed to make large bank failures more orderly by shifting losses from taxpayers to shareholders and creditors, raising questions about who ultimately bears risk.
  • For both supervisors and bank management, the central challenge is to align regulation, internal capital models, and incentives ex ante so that banks remain resilient in stress without unduly restricting their ability to provide useful financial intermediation.
