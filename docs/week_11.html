Block A: The Evolution of Bank Capital Regulation

Why Bank Regulation Evolved

Bank regulation ensures financial institutions maintain adequate capital to manage risks and safeguard economic stability. Before 1988, countries applied inconsistent capital-to-asset ratios, creating competitive imbalances and failing to address risks from international lending and OTC derivatives. The 1988 Basel Accord (Basel I) introduced the first international risk-based standards, but its oversimplification led to regulatory arbitrage and inadequate risk capture.

The 2007-2009 Global Financial Crisis exposed critical weaknesses in Basel II, which coincidentally was implemented just as the crisis began. Banks using internal models had underestimated risks, particularly in trading books. The crisis prompted a comprehensive regulatory overhaul: Basel II.5 (2011), Basel III (2010-2023), and the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB).

Key timeline:

  • 1988: Basel I - First international capital standards (8% minimum, risk-weighted assets)
  • 2007: Basel II - Three pillars (minimum capital, supervisory review, market discipline)
  • 2008: Global Financial Crisis - Revealed Basel II weaknesses
  • 2011: Basel II.5 - Stressed VaR, incremental risk charge
  • 2010-2023: Basel III - Higher capital quality, liquidity requirements, leverage ratio
  • 2019-TBD: FRTB - Expected Shortfall, liquidity horizons, stricter book boundaries

Basel I: The Foundation and Its Flaws

The 1988 BIS Accord introduced risk-weighted assets (RWA) with five risk weight categories (0%, 10%, 20%, 50%, 100%). While groundbreaking, it had significant limitations:

Risk-Weighted Assets Formula:

\[ \text{RWA} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i L_i \]

where \(w_i\) is the risk weight and \(L_i\) is the principal amount.

Capital requirement: Banks must maintain capital ≥ 8% of total RWA, with at least 4% as Tier 1 (core) capital.

Critical flaws:

  1. Crude risk buckets: All corporate loans = 100% risk weight regardless of credit quality (AAA same as B-rated)
  2. No market risk: Only credit risk covered initially (1996 Amendment added market risk)
  3. Regulatory arbitrage: Banks could structure transactions to minimize capital while maintaining risk exposure
  4. No operational risk: Completely ignored

Example of regulatory arbitrage: A bank could securitize high-quality mortgages (50% risk weight) and retain low-quality ones, reducing regulatory capital while increasing actual risk.

Block B: Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB)

From VaR to Expected Shortfall

You’ve already studied Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES) as risk measures. The FRTB represents the Basel Committee’s shift from VaR to ES for regulatory capital calculations, recognizing that VaR failed to capture tail risk during the 2007-2009 crisis.

Evolution of market risk capital:

  1. Basel I (1996 Amendment): 10-day VaR at 99% confidence
  2. Basel II.5 (2011): Added stressed VaR (VaR + sVaR) to capture crisis conditions
  3. FRTB (2019): Replaced VaR with Expected Shortfall at 97.5% confidence

Why Expected Shortfall?

  • VaR answers: “What is the maximum loss in 99% of cases?”
  • ES answers: “What is the average loss in the worst 2.5% of cases?”
  • ES is coherent (subadditive), captures tail risk, and provides information about extreme losses

Liquidity Horizons: The Key FRTB Innovation

The FRTB’s most significant innovation is liquidity horizons - the time required to exit or hedge a risk position in a stressed market. Different risk factors have different liquidity horizons, reflecting how quickly positions can be unwound without significant price impact.

FRTB liquidity horizon structure:

Risk Factor Liquidity Horizon
Large-cap equity, major FX, government bonds 10 days
Small-cap equity, corporate bonds (IG) 20 days
Volatility (equity, FX) 20 days
Corporate bonds (high-yield) 40 days
Structured credit products 60 days
Exotic products 120 days

Capital calculation under FRTB:

\[ \text{ES}_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\text{ES}_{10d}^2 + \text{ES}_{20d}^2 + \text{ES}_{40d}^2 + \text{ES}_{60d}^2 + \text{ES}_{120d}^2} \]

where each \(\text{ES}\) term represents risk factors within that liquidity horizon.

Practical implication: Illiquid positions (exotic derivatives, structured credit) now require significantly more capital than liquid positions (government bonds, major FX pairs), properly reflecting the higher risk of being unable to exit in stressed markets.

Trading Book vs. Banking Book

FRTB introduced stricter criteria to prevent regulatory arbitrage between books:

  • Trading book: Instruments held for short-term profit, marked-to-market daily, subject to market risk capital (ES-based)
  • Banking book: Instruments held to maturity, marked-to-model or amortized cost, subject to credit risk capital (probability of default-based)

Why this matters: Pre-FRTB, banks could shift positions between books to minimize capital. For example, moving illiquid credit instruments to the trading book (lower capital under Basel II.5) even though they couldn’t actually be traded.

FRTB restrictions:

  • Clear intent required for trading book classification
  • Presumptive lists of instruments for each book
  • Once assigned, transfers between books severely restricted
  • Any capital benefit from transfer is disallowed

Block C: Basel III Capital Framework

Capital Quality and Quantity

Basel III, finalized in 2017 and fully implemented in January 2023, dramatically increased both the quality and quantity of required capital. The framework eliminated Tier 3 capital and redefined capital tiers to ensure loss-absorbing capacity.

Capital tier definitions:

  1. Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) - Highest quality:
    • Share capital and retained earnings
    • Minus deductions: goodwill, intangible assets, deferred tax assets, pension deficits
    • Minimum: 4.5% of RWA
  2. Additional Tier 1 (AT1):
    • Non-cumulative perpetual preferred stock
    • Contingent convertible bonds (CoCos) meeting strict criteria
    • Total Tier 1 minimum: CET1 + AT1 ≥ 6% of RWA
  3. Tier 2:
    • Subordinated debt (minimum 5-year maturity)
    • Total capital minimum: Tier 1 + Tier 2 ≥ 8% of RWA

Capital Buffers

Beyond minimum requirements, Basel III introduced buffers to ensure banks can absorb losses during stress:

1. Capital Conservation Buffer (CCB):

  • Additional 2.5% of RWA in CET1 capital
  • Brings effective CET1 minimum to 7% (4.5% + 2.5%)
  • If breached, automatic restrictions on dividends, bonuses, and share buybacks

2. Countercyclical Buffer (CCyB):

  • 0% to 2.5% of RWA, set by national regulators
  • Activated during credit booms to build extra capital
  • Released during downturns to allow lending
  • Must be met with CET1 capital

Total CET1 requirement: 4.5% (minimum) + 2.5% (CCB) + 0-2.5% (CCyB) = 7% to 9.5%

Leverage Ratio

The leverage ratio provides a non-risk-weighted backstop to prevent excessive leverage:

\[ \text{Leverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Tier 1 Capital}}{\text{Total Exposure}} \]

  • Minimum: 3% (some jurisdictions require higher)
  • Total Exposure includes: on-balance sheet assets, derivatives, securities financing, off-balance sheet commitments
  • Purpose: Prevents underestimation of risk-weighted assets and limits absolute leverage

Why both ratios matter: A bank can be compliant on RWA-based ratios but fail leverage ratio if it holds many low-risk-weight assets (e.g., government bonds). The binding constraint determines actual lending capacity.

Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs)

G-SIBs are banks whose failure could destabilize the global financial system. Designation is based on size, interconnectedness, substitutability, complexity, and cross-jurisdictional activity.

G-SIB capital surcharges:

  • Bucket 1: +1.0% CET1
  • Bucket 2: +1.5% CET1
  • Bucket 3: +2.0% CET1
  • Bucket 4: +2.5% CET1
  • Bucket 5: +3.5% CET1

Current G-SIBs: The Financial Stability Board (FSB) publishes an annual list at www.fsb.org. As of 2023, approximately 30 banks are designated as G-SIBs, including JPMorgan Chase, HSBC, Bank of America, Citigroup, and others.

Contingent Convertible Bonds (CoCos)

CoCos are hybrid debt instruments that convert to equity or are written down when a bank’s capital falls below a trigger level. They provide automatic recapitalization during stress.

Key features:

  • Higher interest rates than regular debt (compensates for conversion risk)
  • Trigger event: Typically CET1 ratio falling below 5.125% or 7%
  • Conversion mechanism: Convert to equity (diluting shareholders) or write-down (investors lose principal)
  • Can count as AT1 capital if meeting Basel III criteria

Benefits:

  • For banks: Enhances equity during stress while offering cheaper funding during normal times
  • For regulators: Automatic recapitalization reduces need for taxpayer bailouts
  • For investors: Higher yield compensates for tail risk

Notable example: Credit Suisse CoCos were completely written down to zero during its 2023 rescue by UBS, while equity holders received some value - a controversial inversion of typical capital hierarchy.

Block D: Economic Capital and RAROC

Beyond Regulatory Minimum: Economic Capital

Economic capital represents an internal estimate of capital needed to absorb unexpected losses over a one-year horizon at a confidence level aligned with the institution’s target credit rating. Unlike regulatory capital (compliance-driven), economic capital reflects the bank’s actual risk profile.

Key distinction:

  • Regulatory capital: “What regulators require us to hold” (Basel III rules)
  • Economic capital: “What we actually need to hold” (internal risk models)

A well-managed bank should ensure economic capital ≥ regulatory capital. If economic capital < regulatory capital, the firm is taking less risk than regulators assume. If economic capital > regulatory capital, the firm may be undercapitalized despite regulatory compliance.

Confidence level and credit rating:

Target Rating Default Probability Confidence Level
AAA 0.01% 99.99%
AA 0.02% 99.98%
A 0.05% 99.95%
BBB 0.20% 99.80%

Components of Economic Capital

Economic capital must cover all risk types, integrating the concepts from previous weeks:

1. Market Risk (Weeks 5-6): - Assessed using 1-year horizon VaR or ES at target confidence level - Derived from daily VaR scaled: \(\text{VaR}_{1\text{year}} = \text{VaR}_{1\text{day}} \times \sqrt{250}\)

2. Credit Risk (Week 9): - Models: CreditMetrics, Credit Risk+, KMV - Captures default risk and credit migration risk - Can be cycle-specific (conditional) or cycle-neutral (unconditional)

3. Operational Risk (Week 10): - Loss distribution approach - Captures frequency and severity of operational losses - Highly skewed distribution (many small losses, few catastrophic)

4. Liquidity Risk (Week 8): - Cost of emergency funding during stress - Often embedded in other risk types or handled separately

5. Business Risk: - Strategic risk, reputational risk, competitive pressures - Difficult to quantify but important for strategic initiatives

Loss distribution characteristics:

Risk Type Standard Deviation Skewness Kurtosis Implication
Market Risk High Zero (normal) Low VaR ≈ ES
Credit Risk Moderate Moderate Moderate Some tail risk
Operational Risk Low High High Fat tails matter

Aggregating Economic Capital

Simply summing economic capital across risk types assumes perfect correlation (overly conservative):

\[E_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E_i\]

This overestimates by ~40% in practice.

Correlation-based aggregation (hybrid approach):

\[ E_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} E_i E_j \rho_{ij}} \]

where \(\rho_{ij}\) is the correlation between risk types \(i\) and \(j\).

Typical correlations:

Market Credit Operational
Market 1.00 0.30 0.10
Credit 0.30 1.00 0.10
Operational 0.10 0.10 1.00

Advanced approach: Copulas

  • Maps each risk type’s loss distribution to a standard distribution
  • Applies correlation structure using copula function
  • Gaussian copula is simplest; t-copula captures tail dependence

RAROC: Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital

RAROC measures risk-adjusted profitability, enabling capital allocation and performance evaluation:

\[ \text{RAROC} = \frac{\text{Revenues} - \text{Costs} - \text{Expected Losses}}{\text{Economic Capital}} \]

Components:

  • Revenues: Interest income, fees, trading profits
  • Costs: Operating expenses, funding costs
  • Expected Losses (EL): Average losses over time (e.g., credit loss provisions)
  • Economic Capital (EC): Capital required for unexpected losses

Interpretation:

  • RAROC > Cost of Equity → Business creates value
  • RAROC < Cost of Equity → Business destroys value
  • Compare RAROC across business units to allocate capital efficiently

Applications:

  1. Ex-ante (forward-looking): Should we expand this business? Enter this market?
  2. Ex-post (backward-looking): How did each business unit perform? Bonus allocation?
  3. Pricing: What interest rate should we charge on this loan to achieve target RAROC?

Example: A trading desk generates $200M revenue, $80M costs, $10M expected losses, and requires $800M economic capital:

\[ \text{RAROC} = \frac{200 - 80 - 10}{800} = \frac{110}{800} = 13.75\% \]

If the bank’s cost of equity is 12%, this desk creates value.

Key Takeaways

Regulatory Evolution

  • Basel I (1988) introduced risk-weighted assets but was overly simplistic and enabled regulatory arbitrage
  • Basel II (2007) improved risk sensitivity but was implemented just as the crisis revealed its weaknesses
  • Basel II.5 (2011) added stressed VaR and incremental risk charge for trading books
  • Basel III (2023) increased capital quality (CET1) and quantity (buffers), added leverage ratio and liquidity requirements
  • FRTB (ongoing) replaces VaR with Expected Shortfall and introduces liquidity horizons

FRTB Key Innovations

  • Expected Shortfall at 97.5% captures tail risk better than VaR at 99%
  • Liquidity horizons (10 to 120 days) reflect true time to exit positions in stress
  • Stricter trading vs. banking book boundaries prevent regulatory arbitrage
  • Capital requirements for illiquid products increased significantly

Basel III Capital Framework

  • CET1 (4.5%) + Capital Conservation Buffer (2.5%) + Countercyclical Buffer (0-2.5%) = 7-9.5% total CET1 requirement
  • Leverage ratio (3%) provides non-risk-weighted backstop
  • G-SIBs face additional 1-3.5% CET1 surcharge (FSB publishes annual list)
  • CoCo bonds automatically recapitalize banks when CET1 falls below trigger

Economic Capital and RAROC

  • Economic capital is internal estimate based on target rating; regulatory capital is compliance minimum
  • Aggregate risks using correlations to capture diversification benefits
  • RAROC measures risk-adjusted performance: \(\text{RAROC} = \frac{\text{Rev} - \text{Cost} - \text{EL}}{\text{EC}}\)
  • Use RAROC for capital allocation, pricing, performance evaluation, and strategic decisions
  • Integrate market, credit, operational, and liquidity risk into unified framework

Critical Connections

  • FRTB liquidity horizons reflect lessons from Week 8 (liquidity risk freezing in 2008)
  • Economic capital aggregates Week 5-6 (market), Week 8 (liquidity), Week 9 (credit), and Week 10 (operational) risks
  • Binding constraint (RWA vs. leverage ratio) determines actual lending capacity
  • Economic capital > Regulatory capital → Bank may be undercapitalized despite compliance

Practical Implications

  • Banks must satisfy both RWA-based ratios AND leverage ratio (dual constraint)
  • FRTB makes illiquid trading strategies much more expensive (liquidity horizons)
  • RAROC enables comparing business units with different risk profiles
  • G-SIB designation adds 1-3.5% capital requirement but also provides funding advantages (“too big to fail” perception)
