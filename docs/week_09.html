Credit VaR vs Market VaR

You’ve calculated Market VaR in previous weeks. Credit VaR follows similar logic but with key differences:

  • Market VaR: Continuous price changes, typically 1-10 day horizon, observable daily
  • Credit VaR: Discrete default events, typically 1-year horizon, highly skewed distribution

Both measure potential losses at a confidence level, but credit losses are asymmetric (small probability of large loss) while market losses are more symmetric.

The calculation above (UL = 4×EL) is a simplified Credit VaR approach. More sophisticated models (CreditMetrics, CreditRisk+) explicitly model default correlations and rating migrations.

Theory Block A: Credit Risk Fundamentals

In your calculations, you discovered how three distinct components combine multiplicatively to create expected loss. Let’s formalize this framework.

The Credit Loss Distribution

Credit risk differs fundamentally from market risk in its asymmetric and discrete nature:

  • Market Risk: Continuous distribution, symmetric, observable in real-time (mark-to-market)
  • Credit Risk: Discrete jumps (default or no default), asymmetric (maximum gain = coupon, maximum loss = principal), latent (deterioration not observable until default)
Credit Risk Components

Probability of Default (PD):

  • Likelihood of default within a time horizon (typically 1 year), expressed as percentage
  • Estimated from external ratings, internal models, or market signals (CDS spreads)

Loss Given Default (LGD):

  • Percentage of exposure lost if default occurs: LGD = 1 - Recovery Rate
  • Depends on collateral quality, seniority, legal framework, and economic conditions

Exposure at Default (EAD):

  • Dollar amount outstanding when default occurs
  • For credit lines: \(\text{EAD} = \text{Drawn} + (\text{Undrawn} \times \text{CCF})\)
  • Credit Conversion Factor (CCF) calibrated empirically (higher for committed lines)

Expected Loss Formula

\[ \text{EL} = \text{PD} \times \text{LGD} \times \text{EAD} \]

This multiplicative formula creates important effects:

  • Improving any component reduces expected loss
  • Components interact: Low PD can offset high LGD
  • Diversification helps: Different exposures have different component drivers

Expected Loss vs. Unexpected Loss

Your Activity 3 revealed a critical distinction that drives all credit risk management:

Expected Loss (EL):

  • The mean of the loss distribution—predictable and statistically certain
  • Should be priced into the loan via interest rate spread
  • Covered by loan loss provisions and reserves
  • Not surprising when it materializes; part of the business model

Unexpected Loss (UL):

  • The volatility around the mean (standard deviation of losses)
  • Unpredictable credit losses that exceed EL—the true risk
  • Covered by bank capital (equity)
  • Key driver of regulatory capital requirements

\[ \text{Credit VaR}_{95\%} \approx \text{EL} + 1.645 \times \text{UL} \]

Key Insight: Banks earn profits by pricing expected loss into loans and using capital to absorb unexpected losses. If EL is mispriced, the bank loses money even without defaults occurring.

This distinction explains why Block D will examine Basel III capital requirements: regulatory capital must cover unexpected losses at 99.9% confidence, not just expected losses. The separation between pricing (covers EL) and capital (covers UL) is fundamental to banking.

Theory Block B: Credit Risk Beyond Default

Block A focused on default risk—the probability that borrowers fail to repay. But credit risk is not binary. You can lose money even without defaults occurring. This is crucial for understanding trading book risks and market-based credit exposures.

Credit Losses

Credit losses come from three distinct sources:

  • Default Risk: Borrower fails to repay (binary event: default or no default)
  • Migration Risk: Credit rating changes affect bond values
  • Spread Risk: Market-wide credit spread movements independent of ratings

For trading books (mark-to-market accounting), migration and spread risk dominate daily P&L. For banking books (held-to-maturity accounting), only default risk matters if the bank holds to maturity.

The relationship between bond yields, spreads, and prices:

\[ \text{Bond Yield} = \text{Risk-Free Rate} + \text{Credit Spread} \]

When credit spreads widen (e.g., due to downgrade), bond prices fall to bring yields up to the new market level. The approximate price change for small spread movements:

\[ \Delta \text{Price} \% \approx -\text{Modified Duration} \times \Delta \text{Spread} \]

The Fallen Angel Effect: When Investment Grade Disappears

When a bond falls from investment grade (BBB- or better) to high yield (BB+ or worse), it becomes a fallen angel. This triggers:

  • Forced selling by investment-grade-only mandates (mutual funds, pension funds, insurance companies)
  • Price decline beyond spread widening due to selling pressure
  • HQLA reclassification: Level 2A eligible → Not eligible for LCR (Basel III)
  • Liquidity evaporates: Market makers widen bid-ask spreads
  • Wrong-way risk: Credit downgrades trigger liquidity needs precisely when liquidity is scarce

Key Insight: Credit quality is sticky but asymmetric—downgrades are more common than upgrades. Banks price this migration risk into spreads and capital requirements.

Block C: Historical Default Probabilities

Theory: Three Ways to Measure Default Risk

When analyzing credit risk, we use three interconnected measures:

1. Cumulative (Unconditional) Default Probability - \(Q(t)\)

  • Probability that a company defaults within time period \([0,t]\) (from now until time \(t\))
  • Answers: “What’s the chance they default sometime in the next \(t\) years?”
  • Always increases with time
  • Example: \(Q(5) = 1.54\%\) means 1.54% of BBB companies default within 5 years

2. Marginal (Conditional) Default Probability - \(q(t)\)

  • Probability of default in period \(t\), given survival through period \(t-1\)
  • Answers: “If they survived this long, what’s the chance they default next period?”
  • Can increase or decrease over time
  • Formula: \(q(t) = \frac{Q(t) - Q(t-1)}{1 - Q(t-1)}\)

3. Hazard Rate - \(h(t)\)

  • Instantaneous default intensity at time \(t\)
  • Continuous-time version of marginal PD
  • For constant hazard rate: \(Q(t) = 1 - e^{-h \cdot t}\)
  • Therefore: \(h = -\frac{1}{t} \ln(1 - Q(t))\)

Key relationship: All three measures are mathematically linked and provide equivalent information about default risk from different perspectives.

Block D: Market-Implied Default Probabilities

Theory: From Historical to Market-Based Measures

While historical default probabilities tell us what happened in the past, market prices tell us what investors expect to happen in the future.

Credit Default Swaps (CDS) and Default Probabilities

A CDS is insurance against default. The CDS spread reflects the market’s assessment of default risk:

  • CDS Spread: Annual premium (in basis points) paid for default protection
  • Key relationship: Higher default probability → Higher CDS spread

Risk-Neutral vs. Historical Probabilities

  • Historical (Real-World) PD: Actual default frequencies from past data
  • Risk-Neutral (Market-Implied) PD: Default probabilities implied by market prices
  • Why they differ: Risk-neutral PDs include risk premiums. Investors demand extra compensation for bearing default risk.

Simple Approximation

For a constant hazard rate \(h\) and recovery rate \(R\):

\[\text{CDS Spread} \approx h \times (1 - R)\]

Therefore:

\[h \approx \frac{\text{CDS Spread}}{1 - R}\]

The risk-neutral cumulative default probability is:

\[Q^{RN}(t) = 1 - e^{-h \cdot t}\]

More Precise Formula (when spreads are high):

\[Q^{RN}(t) = 1 - e^{-\frac{s \cdot t}{1 - R}}\]

where \(s\) is the CDS spread expressed as a decimal (e.g., 180 bps = 0.0180).

Key Takeaways

  • Expected loss (PD × LGD × EAD) measures average credit losses, while Credit VaR captures unexpected losses that determine capital requirements
  • Recovery rates vary significantly by seniority and collateral, with senior secured bonds recovering substantially more than subordinated debt
  • Credit migration risk creates mark-to-market losses even without default, as rating downgrades widen spreads and reduce bond values
  • Default probabilities can be measured three ways (cumulative, marginal, hazard rate) and allow calculation of default risk for any time horizon
  • Historical default rates increase sharply with lower credit ratings, demonstrating the nonlinear relationship between rating quality and default risk
  • Market-implied probabilities from CDS spreads exceed historical default rates because they include risk premiums and reflect forward-looking investor expectations
