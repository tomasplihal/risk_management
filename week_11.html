Learning outcomes:

  • Understand the growing importance of environmental risks in risk management practices.
  • Recognize the terms ESG, sustainability, greenwashing.
  • Understand the holistic approach of Enterprise Risk Management (ERM).
  • Recognize the importance of integrating business units into an enterprise-wide risk management system.
  • Distinguish between quantitative and qualitative aspects of risk appetite.
  • Emphasize the significance of a strong risk culture, especially in medium- to long-term risk assessment.
  • Be aware of the potential pitfalls in focusing solely on short-term profits, as evident from historical crises.

11.1 Climate Risk, ESG, and Sustainability

  • Environmental risks pose a threat to Earth’s habitability for future generations.
  • Companies and financial institutions are under increasing societal pressure to address these risks.
  • The prominence of environmental risks is expected to rise in the coming years.
  • Climate risk is identified as the most significant environmental risk.

11.1.1 Climate Risk

  • Climate risk is one of the most pressing environmental concern.
  • Human activities emit tens of billions of tons of greenhouse gases annually.
  • Greenhouse gases, primarily CO2, persist for hundreds of years, exacerbating global warming.
  • Paris Agreement (2015) and Glasgow Climate Pact (2021) aim for net-zero emissions by
  • Uncertainties exist in achieving these goals due to varying global policy responses.
  • Difficulty in balancing current emissions with future environmental damage (trade-off between emissions today and damage in the future).
  • Major sectors contributing to greenhouse gas emissions in the US are transportation, electricity, and industry.
  • China, U.S., and India as major emitters, with per capita disparities. https://www.worldometers.info/co2-emissions/co2-emissions-by-country/
  • Climate change causes rising sea levels due to ice melt and water expansion.
  • Increase in extreme weather events like storms, heatwaves, and hurricanes.
  • Extreme events already cost billions; e.g., Hurricane Ida cost $75 billion in 2021.

11.1.1.1 Physical and Transition Risks

  • Analysts differentiate between physical and transition climate risks.
  • Physical risks involve costs from extreme climate events like floods and hurricanes.
  • Transition risks are linked to policies for lower emissions, such as carbon taxes.
  • Companies may need to shut down fossil fuel plants earlier due to transition risks.
  • Lower emissions also present new business opportunities in green technologies.
  • Estimated $100–150 trillion needed for economic transition over next 30 years.
  • Carbon tax and cap-and-trade systems as tools to regulate emissions, varying adoption globally.

11.1.1.2 Insurance Companies

  • Climate change significantly impacts the insurance industry by affecting risk correlations.
  • Traditional “law of large numbers” less applicable due to correlated climate-related disasters.
  • Short-term contracts allow pricing adjustments.
  • Life insurance less adaptable to climate risks.
  • Resource allocation needed for better climate risk understanding and regulatory compliance.
  • Companies increasingly reluctant to insure high-risk areas for natural disasters.

11.1.1.3 Banks

  • Banks actively quantify climate change effects through forums like PCAF (Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials).
  • Regulatory bodies launch climate stress tests for banks.
  • Climate risks impact multiple traditional risk categories:
    • Credit Risk: Climate risk can impair debt service and collateral value.
    • Market Risk: Asset prices may decline due to climate effects.
    • Liquidity Risk: Market changes may limit access to stable funding.
    • Operational Risk: Legal and compliance risks may rise.
    • Reputational Risk: Public sentiment can heighten reputational risks.
  • Mortgages vulnerable to long-term climate change impacts on real estate values.
  • Severe climate events can reduce corporate profitability, raising credit risk.
  • Mass migrations and agricultural changes expected, affecting economic stability.
  • Sovereign debt increasingly risky, particularly in less-developed countries.
  • Transition to low-carbon economy creates winners and losers, affecting lending.
  • Reputational risks make banks cautious in lending to carbon emitters.

11.1.1.4 Institutional Investors

  • Increasing focus on incorporating climate risk in portfolios.
  • Companies incentivized to align with emission targets to attract institutional investments.
  • Green Bonds: Innovative financial instruments designated for climate-friendly investments, underwritten by investment banks.
  • Multiple organizations aim to standardize climate-related financial reporting, but efforts are still in progress.
  • Asset managers like BlackRock are proactive, while others like Berkshire Hathaway are resistant.
  • Political Counterpressures: Legislation proposals and requests to ignore environmental factors in state pension investments.
  • Despite political setbacks, focus on emissions and climate risk disclosure is growing.

11.1.2 ESG

  • Shift from profit-centric approach to incorporating environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors.
  • Social Aspect: Addresses diversity, equity, inclusion, and worker well-being. Metrics still under development.
  • Governance Aspect: Focuses on transparent accounting and accountability to shareholders. Avoids conflicts of interest and unethical behavior.
  • Multiple ESG data providers exist, often yielding conflicting reports.
  • Absence of regulated ESG reporting standards allows companies to shop for favorable scores, creating risk for investors.

11.1.3 Sustainability

  • Sustainability Defined: Meets current needs without jeopardizing future generations.
  • Examples of Nonsustainability: Greenhouse gas emissions, ocean pollution, soil and water contamination.
  • Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB) and Global Reporting Initiative provide guidelines.
  • Three Pillars:
    • Environmental Sustainability: Resource conservation.
    • Economic Sustainability: Long-term stability with social and environmental care.
    • Social Sustainability: Supports healthy, livable communities for current and future generations.
  • Sustainable investment funds aim to invest in companies with good ESG or sustainability practices.
  • No significant outperformance compared to traditional funds, despite increased demand. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jofi.12841
  • Some reports overstate benefits, suggest high returns with low risks contrary to finance theory.

11.1.4 Greenwashing

  • Greenwashing Concept: Companies misleadingly claim ecological responsibility to attract consumers.
  • Examples:
    • Innocent drinks: False claims about recyclable plastics.
    • Keurig: Misleading recycling claims led to a $3 million fine.
    • IKEA: Used illegally sourced wood despite good sustainability credentials.
    • Ryanair: Unsubstantiated claim of being Europe’s “lowest emissions airline.”
  • Financial Institutions: Deutsche Bank’s DWS exaggerated green credentials, leading to CEO’s resignation.
  • Implications: Considered a serious offense, impacting consumer trust and corporate credibility.

11.2 Enterprise Risk Management

  • Risk managers must evaluate risks and adopt a holistic view through Enterprise Risk Management (ERM).
  • Top-down and bottom-up approaches essential for effective risk management.
    • Top-down sets overall risk appetite.
    • Bottom-up assesses risks at the business unit level.
  • Shift from compliance orientation to strategic orientation in risk management is noted.

11.2.1 Risk Appetite

  • How much risk is the organization prepared to take in order to achieve its strategic objectives and business plans?

  • Risk appetite is an essential part of Enterprise Risk Management (ERM).

  • For fund managers, risk appetite is more straightforward to quantify using beta, expected return, and risk.

  • For fund managers it is possible to use \(\beta\) that is consistent with a particular VaR objective.

\[ \beta = \frac{R - R_F}{R_M - R_F + N^{-1} (1-p) \sigma_M} \]

  • \(R\) is the return that has a probability \(p\) of being exceeded in one year.

  • \(R_M\) is the expected market return and \(R_F\) is the risk-free rate.

  • Unfortunately, the available risk-return alternatives for a financial institution such as a bank or an insurance company cannot usually be quantified as easily as for a fund manager.

  • VaR or expected shortfall often specify worst-case loss aspect of risk appetite.

  • Other elements like reputational risk, credit rating risk, legal risks, etc., may be qualitative.

  • Risk quantification can be:

    1. Bottom-Up: Calculated by business units, then aggregated.
    2. Top-Down: Set by the board, guiding risk limits for individual business lines.

  • Risk appetite should address risk Concentration: Limit exposure to sectors, market variables, companies.

  • Monitoring and adherence to set limits crucial for effectiveness.

11.2.2 Risk Culture

  • It is all about how decisions are made.
  • If the risk culture is good, all decisions are made in a disciplined way with a careful consideration of possible outcomes, weighing risks and rewards.
  • A strong risk culture should align with an institution’s risk appetite and long-term objectives.
  • Short-term profits should not outweigh long-term risks.
  • The role of compensation in influencing decisions is crucial and is being revised to mitigate short-term focus.
  • E.g., spreading bonuses over years, clawback provisions for adverse outcomes.
  • Regulatory guidelines (Financial Stability Board) can assist in establishing a robust risk culture.

11.2.3 Identifying Major Risks

  • Core to ERM, similar to stress testing; consider historical and emerging trends.
  • Regulatory Changes: Banks face tighter regulations; shift of activities to less-regulated shadow banks.
  • Cognitive Biases:
    • Wishful Thinking: Confusing desires with likely outcomes.
    • Anchoring: Fixation on initial estimates, neglecting full range of outcomes.
    • Availability: Overemphasis on recent information.
    • Representativeness: Stereotyping situations based on past experiences.
    • Sunk Costs Bias: Ignoring sunk costs critical for rational decision-making.
  • Underestimating tail risks due to cognitive biases; requires methods to improve estimations.
  • Inclusion of diverse views mitigates bias, improves risk identification.

11.2.4 Strategic Risk Management

  • Focuses on assessing assumptions and potential failures in a company’s strategy.
    • Key Questions: Impact of competitors, customer reactions, and external events.
    • Risk is tied to the poor performance under ‘wrong’ scenarios.
  • Courses of Action Once Risk Identified:
    • Avoid: Terminate the business or investment.
    • Reduce: Mitigate likelihood or impact of adverse events.
    • Adjust: Modify plans to lower risks.
    • Share/Insure: Transfer or share some risk.
    • Accept: No action taken.
  • Choice of action varies by context but requires thorough consideration.

11.3 Practice Questions and Problems

11.3.1 Climate Risk, ESG, and Sustainability

  1. Why does the burning of fossil fuels lead to temperature rises?
  2. What is the goal that was agreed to in Paris in 2015?
  3. What is the difference between a carbon tax and a cap and trade system?
  4. What is the difference between physical risks and transition risks?
  5. What are the risks of climate change to insurance companies?
  6. What does ESG stand for?
  7. What is greenwashing?
  8. How can pension plans and mutual funds help solve the global warming problem?
  9. Give examples of activities covered by ESG and sustainability that are not climate risk issues.

11.3.2 Enterprise Risk Management

  1. Explain the difference between top-down and bottom-up approaches to risk management. Why are both necessary in ERM?
  2. “The risk culture at a bank should lead it to consider the longer-term implications of decisions as well as short-term profits.” Discuss.
  3. What steps can a bank take to encourage employees to consider more than short-term profits?
  4. What distinguishes enterprise risk management from more traditional approaches to risk management?
  5. A fund’s risk appetite is such that it wants to be 90% certain that it will not lose more than 20% in any one year. Using the performance of the S&P 500 between 1970 and 2016 (mean return 11.74%, standard deviation 18.86%), determine the beta the fund should have. Assume a risk-free rate of 3% per annum.
  6. Give three examples of cognitive biases in finance.
