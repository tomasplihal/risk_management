4.1 Introduction

Definition of options

Options are contracts that give the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy (in the case of a call option) or sell (in the case of a put option) an underlying asset at a predetermined price, known as the strike price, within a specified time frame. The seller of the option, in turn, has the obligation to fulfill the transaction if the buyer decides to exercise the option.

  • Options have non-linear payoffs and require upfront payment.
  • Options allow benefiting from favorable movements and minimizing harm from unfavorable movements.
  • Sellers of options face potential large losses and must be adequately compensated.

4.1.1 Notation and Assumptions

  • Assumptions:
    • Option user has a view on underlying movements or volatility.
    • Options are customized and European-style.
    • No early termination of strategies.
  • Time notation:
    • Time \(0\) is the time at which the strategy is initiated.
    • Time \(t\) is the time the option expires, stated as a fraction of a year.
  • Time until expiration: \(t - 0 = t\), which is \(\frac{\text{Days to expiration}}{365}\).
  • Symbol definitions:
    • \(c_0, c_t\) \(\dots\) Price of the call option at time \(0\) and time \(t\).
    • \(p_0, p_t\) \(\dots\) Price of the put option at time \(0\) and time \(t\).
    • \(X\) \(\dots\) Exercise price.
    • \(S_0, S_t\) \(\dots\) Price of the underlying at time \(0\) and time \(t\).
    • \(V_0, V_t\) \(\dots\) Value of the position at time \(0\) and time \(t\).
    • \(\Pi\) \(\dots\) Profit from the transaction, calculated as \(V_t - V_0\).
    • \(r\) \(\dots\) Risk-free rate.

4.2 Option Strategies for Equity Portfolios

  • The following strategies can be applied to variety of asset classes.
  • However, they are primarily explained and understood within the context of investing in equities or equity indices.

4.2.1 Standard Long and Short Positions

  • Based on the course “Financial Derivatives” you should be able to define the following:
    • Long position in a stock and short position in a stock.
    • Long call and short call options.
    • Long put and short put options.
    • Calculate profit and value of the option.
    • Determine the maximum profit or loss for each position.

4.2.2 Risk Management Strategies with Options and the Underlying

4.2.2.1 Covered Call

  • Covered call strategy involves owning the underlying asset (like stocks) and then selling call options on that same asset.
  • The goal is to generate additional income from the option premium, which the investor gets to keep regardless of whether the option is exercised or not.
  • Covered calls reduce the risk of a position, contradicting beliefs that options inherently increase risk.
  • While they decrease risk, covered calls also reduce expected returns compared to only holding the stock.
  • This strategy may limit gains in bull markets but provides a cushion against losses in bear markets through the option premium.
  • To summarize the covered call, we have the following: \[ V_t = S_t - \max(0, S_t - X) \]

\[ \Pi = V_t - S_0 + c_0 \]

\[ \text{Maximum profit} = X - S_0 + c_0 \]

\[ \text{Maximum loss} = S_0 - c_0 \]

\[ \text{Breakeven} = S_t = S_0 - c_0 \]

4.2.2.2 Covered Call Example

  • This example illustrates how a covered call strategy can generate income and potentially increase profits, but it also shows that the strategy can limit upside potential if the underlying asset’s price increases significantly.
  • Suppose you own 100 shares of Company XYZ, which are currently trading at $50 per share.
  • You decide to implement a covered call strategy by selling one call option (equivalent to 100 shares) with a strike price of $55 for a premium of $2 per share.
  • If the price of the shares rises to $60, the option will be exercised:

\[ \text{Profit} = \text{Profit from shares} + \text{Profit from option} \]

\[ \text{Profit} = (\$60 - \$50) \times 100 + (-\$300) = \$1000 - \$300 = \$700 \]

  • If the price of the shares stays below $55, the option will not be exercised:

\[ \text{Profit} = (\$2 \times 100) + ((\$55 - \$50) \times 100) = \$200 + \$0 = \$200 \]

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Variables
stock_price = 50
strike_price = 55
premium = 2
shares = 100

# Stock price range for the graph
stock_price_range = np.arange(40, 70, 1)

# Underlying stock payoff calculation
stock_payoff = stock_price_range - stock_price

# Call option payoff calculation (for short position)
call_payoff = np.where(
    stock_price_range <= strike_price,
    premium,
    premium - (stock_price_range - strike_price),
)

# Covered call payoff calculation
covered_call_payoff = stock_payoff + call_payoff

# Plotting
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(stock_price_range, stock_payoff, label="Underlying Stock", color="blue")
plt.plot(stock_price_range, call_payoff, label="Short Call Option", color="red")
plt.plot(
    stock_price_range,
    covered_call_payoff,
    label="Covered Call Strategy",
    color="green",
    linewidth=3,
)
plt.axhline(y=0, color="black", linewidth=1.2)
plt.xlabel("Stock Price at Expiration")
plt.ylabel("Profit/Loss")
plt.title("Covered Call Strategy Payoff")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

4.2.2.3 Protective Put

  • A protective put strategy involves buying an asset (like stocks) and buying a put option for the same asset.
  • The goal of this strategy is to limit the downside risk of owning the asset.
  • If the asset’s price falls, the put option increases in value, offsetting the loss from the asset.
  • A protective put strategy offers downside protection with upside potential, but it’s important to consider the costs and trade-offs.
  • Protection against downside loss is expensive and the buyer of a protective put must give up considerable upside potential.
  • The protective put strategy is often viewed as a form of insurance.
  • To summarize the protective put, we have the following:

\[ V_t = S_t + \max(0, X - S_t) \]

\[ \Pi = V_t - S_0 - p_0 \]

\[ \text{Maximum profit} = \infty \]

\[ \text{Maximum loss} = S_0 + p_0 - X \]

\[ \text{Breakeven} = S_t = S_0 + p_0 \]

4.2.2.4 Protective Put Example

  • Suppose you own 100 shares of Company XYZ, which are currently trading at $50 per share.
  • You decide to implement a protective put strategy by buying one put option (equivalent to 100 shares) with a strike price of $45 for a premium of $2 per share.
  • If the price of the shares rises to $60, the option will not be exercised:

\[ \text{Profit} = \text{Profit from shares} + \text{Profit from option} \]

\[ \text{Profit} = (\$60 - \$50) \times 100 + (-\$2) \times 100 = \$1000 - \$200 = \$800 \]

  • If the price of the shares drops to $40, the option will be exercised:

\[ \text{Profit} = (\$40 - \$50) \times 100 + (-\$2) \times 100 + (\$45 - \$40) \times 100 \]

\[ \text{Profit} = -\$1000 - \$200 + \$500 = -\$700 \]

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Variables
stock_price = 50
strike_price = 45
premium = 2
shares = 100

# Stock price range for the graph
stock_price_range = np.arange(40, 60, 1)

# Underlying stock payoff calculation
stock_payoff = stock_price_range - stock_price

# Put option payoff calculation (for long position)
put_payoff = (
    np.where(stock_price_range <= strike_price, strike_price - stock_price_range, 0)
    - premium
)

# Protective put payoff calculation
protective_put_payoff = stock_payoff + put_payoff

# Plotting
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(stock_price_range, stock_payoff, label="Underlying Stock", color="blue")
plt.plot(stock_price_range, put_payoff, label="Long Put Option", color="red")
plt.plot(
    stock_price_range,
    protective_put_payoff,
    label="Protective Put Strategy",
    color="green",
    linewidth=3,
)
plt.axhline(y=0, color="black", linewidth=1.2)
plt.xlabel("Stock Price at Expiration")
plt.ylabel("Profit/Loss")
plt.title("Protective Put Strategy Payoff")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

4.2.3 Money Spreads

  • In options trading, “money spreads” or “spreads” refer to strategies that involve simultaneously buying and selling options of the same class (e.g., calls or puts) on the same underlying asset but with different strike prices and/or expiration dates.
  • These strategies are often used to reduce risk and can also be a way to reduce the initial cost of entering a position.
  • Spreads are categorized based on how they make or lose money, and below are some common types:
  1. Vertical Spreads:
    • Bull Call Spread: Buying a call option with a lower strike price and selling another call option with a higher strike price, both with the same expiration date.
    • Bull Put Spread: Selling a put option with a higher strike price and buying a put option with a lower strike price.
    • Bear Put Spread: Buying a put option with a higher strike price and selling
    • Bear Call Spread: Selling a call option with a lower strike price and buying a call option with a higher strike price.
  2. Horizontal Spreads (also known as Calendar Spreads):
    • Involves options with the same strike price but different expiration dates.
  3. Diagonal Spreads:
    • Combines elements of both vertical and horizontal spreads, involving different strike prices and different expiration dates.
  4. Iron Condor:
    • A combination of two vertical spreads (one using calls, the other using puts) with the same expiration date but different strike prices.
  5. Butterfly Spread:
    • A combination of three strike prices, usually involving both calls and puts. It can be constructed using all calls (call butterfly) or all puts (put butterfly).
  • The profit and loss of these strategies depend on the underlying asset’s price at expiration, relative to the strike prices of the options used in the spread.
  • Money spreads help traders target a specific range of potential price movement, and they often involve less risk than outright buying or selling single options.
  • The tradeoff, generally speaking, is that they also offer more limited profit potential.

4.2.4 Combinations of Calls and Puts

4.2.4.1 Collars

  • A collar option strategy is a defensive strategy that investors use when they have a long position in the underlying asset and want to protect against potential losses.
  • It consists of three components:
    1. Owning the Underlying Asset: The investor holds shares of a stock or another underlying asset.
    2. Buying a Put Option: The investor buys a put option with a strike price below the current price of the underlying asset. This put option serves as insurance, providing the right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price if the asset’s price falls significantly. It defines a floor on potential losses.
    3. Selling a Call Option: At the same time, the investor sells (or “writes”) a call option with a strike price above the current price of the underlying asset. This generates premium income, which can offset the cost of buying the put option. However, it also caps the potential profit if the underlying asset’s price rises above the call’s strike price, as the call buyer will have the right to buy the asset at that price.
  • The combination of these three elements forms a “collar” around the potential profit and loss from the underlying asset:
    • Protection from Loss: If the underlying asset’s price falls, the put option compensates for the loss, limiting the downside risk.
    • Limitation on Profit: If the underlying asset’s price rises above the strike price of the sold call, the profit is capped, as the asset may be called away at that price.
    • Cost Mitigation: The premium received from selling the call option can offset the cost of buying the put, potentially creating a cost-neutral or low-cost hedge.
  • The collar strategy is often used by investors who have significant gains in the underlying asset and want to protect those gains without selling the asset.
  • It can be tailored to the investor’s outlook by choosing different strike prices and expiration dates for the options, balancing the level of protection, profit potential, and cost.

4.2.4.2 Straddle

  • A straddle option strategy is a neutral options strategy that involves simultaneously buying or selling a call option and a put option with the same strike price and expiration date on the same underlying asset.
  • It’s used when an investor expects a significant price movement but is uncertain about the direction of the movement (up or down). There are two main variations of the straddle strategy:
  • Long Straddle: Suitable for investors who anticipate significant price volatility but are uncertain about the direction. The cost is the premium paid for both options, and the profit potential is unlimited.
  • Short Straddle: Suitable for investors who anticipate little to no price movement in the underlying asset. The profit is limited to the premiums received, and the risk can be substantial if the asset price moves sharply.
  • Strangle: Similar to a straddle but uses different strike prices for the call and put, usually with the call’s strike price above and the put’s strike price below the current asset price. It can be implemented as a long or short strategy.
  • The straddle strategy is commonly used around significant news events, earnings announcements, or other situations where substantial price swings are possible. It allows investors to potentially profit from volatility itself, rather than having to correctly predict price direction.

4.3 Interest Rate Option Strategies

  • Underlying asset is an interest rate, usually on a 90- or 180-day underlying instrument.

  • The purpose is a protection against changes in interest rates.

  • Call option is exercised if the underlying interest rate is above the exercise rate.

  • Put option is exercised if the underlying interest rate is below the exercise rate.

  • Notional principal determines the payoff when the option is exercised.

  • Payoff is delayed by a period corresponding to the life of the underlying instrument.

  • The rate is determined on one day, the option expiration, and payment is made \(m\) days later.

  • This practice is standard in floating-rate loans and thus is used with interest rate options, which are designed to manage the risk of floating-rate loans.

  • Payoff for Interest Rate Call Option:

\[ \text{Notional principal} \times \max(0, \text{Underlying rate at expiration} - \text{Exercise rate}) \times \frac{\text{Days in underlying rate}}{360} \]

  • Payoff for Interest Rate Put Option:

\[ \text{Notional principal} \times \max(0, \text{Exercise rate} - \text{Underlying rate at expiration}) \times \frac{\text{Days in underlying rate}}{360} \]

  • \(\text{Days in underlying}\) refers to the maturity of the instrument from which the underlying rate is taken.

4.3.1 Using Interest Rate Calls with Borrowing - Example

Loan Details Value
Loan Amount $40,000,000
Underlying 180-day LIBOR
Spread 200 basis points over LIBOR
Current LIBOR 5.5%
Expiration 128 days later
Exercise Rate 5%
Call Premium $100,000
LIBOR at expiration 8%
  • Call Payoff at Expiration (received 180 days later):

\[ \$40,000,000 \times \max(0, 0.08 - 0.05) \times \frac{180}{360} = \$600,000 \]

  • Premium Compounded from option initiation to option expiration (128 days) at original LIBOR of 5.5% plus 200 basis points:

\[ \$100,000 \times \left[1 + (0.055 + 0.02) \times \frac{128}{360}\right] = \$102,667 \]

  • Effective Loan Proceeds:

\[ \$40,000,000 - \$102,667 = \$39,897,333 \]

  • Loan Interest:

\[ \$40,000,000 \times (0.08 + 0.02) \times \frac{180}{360} = \$2,000,000 \]

  • Effective Interest: Loan interest minus the call payoff
  • Effective Rate on the Loan:

\[ \left(\frac{\$40,000,000 + \$2,000,000 - \$600,000}{\$39,897,333}\right)^{365/180} - 1 = 7.79\% \]

  • For various LIBOR rates at option expiration, the highest possible effective rate is 7.79% (if the option is exercised). If the option is not exercised, the effective rate decreases.
  • The similar analysis can be used for lending by using interest rate put options.
  • A lender can buy a put that pays off if the interest rate falls below a chosen level.
  • The put payoff then compensates the lender for the lower interest rate on the loan.
  • The equations have to be slightly adjusted for put options, everything else is similar.

4.3.2 Floating-Rate Cap, Floor, and Collar

  • Many corporate loans are floating-rate loans that have periodic interest payments with regularly scheduled rate resets, causing multiple distinct risks.

  • Interest Rate Calls (Caplets):

    • Borrowers might use multiple call options (caplets) to set a ceiling on borrowing rates.
    • Each caplet has its own expiration date; exercise rates are typically the same.
    • A combination of these options is called a cap.

  • Interest Rate Puts (Floorlets):

    • Lenders like may be concerned about falling rates and can buy puts expiring on rate reset dates.
    • This combination is known as a floor, with individual options called floorlets.

  • Interest Rate Collars:

    • Combines a long position in a cap with a short position in a floor.
    • Zero-Cost collar means that exercise rate on the floor matches the cap premium, seeming to have no “cost,” but the borrower gives up gains from potential falling rates.
    • Borrowers often buy caps and sell floors, while lenders might buy floors and sell caps.
    • Most interest rate collars are initiated by borrowers, to protect against varying interest rates.

4.4 Option Portfolio Risk Management Strategies

  • Dealers provide liquidity in options, taking on risk and hedging positions to earn the bid-ask spread without taking on the risk themselves.
  • When a customer wants to buy a call option, the dealer quotes a price and sells it, but this creates a dangerous short call position.
  • Ideally, the dealer would find someone to take the opposite position or use put-call parity to hedge the risk.
  • However, this often isn’t practical, so dealers often resort to delta hedging.

4.4.1 Delta Hedging

  • Delta is a ratio expressing how the option price changes relative to the underlying price, typically between 0.0 and 1.0.

  • A long position in the underlying asset can offset a short call position; the size of the long position depends on the option’s delta.

  • If the dealer sells a given number of calls, they need to own a number of shares equal to the number of underlying shares in the options times the delta.

  • Challenges with Delta Hedging:

    • Delta as an Approximation: Delta is only an approximate measure, and it changes as the underlying price or time changes, meaning the hedged position can quickly become unhedged.
    • Rounding Off: The number of underlying units per option has to be rounded, leading to imprecision in balancing the two positions.
    • Continuous Monitoring Required: Constant adjustments are needed to maintain the delta-hedged position, especially when the underlying changes significantly.

4.4.2 Gamma Hedging

  • Gamma measures the rate of change in the delta with respect to the change in the underlying asset’s price.
  • It reflects the deviation between the exact option price change and the price change approximated by delta, making it a measure of the delta’s sensitivity.
  • Role in Delta-Hedging:
    • If a delta-hedged position were risk-free, its gamma would be zero.
    • A large gamma indicates a greater deviation from being risk-free.
    • The larger the gamma, the more the position is affected by rapid movements in the delta.
  • Gamma is the largest for at-the-money and near-expiration options.
  • Deep in-the-money or out-of-the-money options have slow delta movements and pose fewer problems for hedgers.
  • Gamma hedging requires adding another option and combining it with the underlying and existing options to make both delta and gamma zero.

4.4.3 Vega and Volatility Risk

  • Vega measures the sensitivity of an option’s price to changes in the volatility of the underlying asset.
  • Volatility is the only unobservable variable required to value an option, making it a critical factor.
  • An option’s price is highly sensitive to changes in the underlying asset’s volatility.
  • Changes in volatility can have a significant impact on a dealer’s position, even if it’s delta-hedged with zero or insignificant gamma.
  • An increase in volatility can raise the value of options, potentially leading to substantial losses for a dealer holding the underlying and selling options to delta hedge.
  • An option price is more sensitive to volatility when it is at-the-money.
  • Dealers attempt to measure and monitor vega, and in some cases, hedge it by taking a position in another option.
  • The dealer must jointly monitor and manage the risks associated with delta, gamma, and vega.
