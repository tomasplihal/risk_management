5.1 Volatility

Monitoring the volatility of market variables like exchange rates, equity prices, and commodity prices is essential for financial institutions to manage portfolio value effectively. Common models for capturing volatility include the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA), Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity (ARCH), and Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity (GARCH). These models recognize that volatility is dynamic, varying across time, with periods of low and high fluctuation.

Definition

Realized volatility, \(\sigma\), measures how much an asset’s price has moved over a certain time period. It is calculated using squared returns or the standard deviation of returns over shorter intervals, giving a clear picture of past price fluctuations.

  • Volatility is typically measured annually for option pricing and daily for risk management.
  • Volatility is higher when the market is open, so time is measured in trading days (252 per year) instead of calendar days.

\[ \sigma_{year} = \sigma_{day} \times \sqrt{252} \]

\[ \sigma_{day} = \sigma_{year} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{252}} \]

5.1.1 Implied Volatility

Definition

Implied volatility is the market’s forecast of a security’s future price fluctuations, derived from the price of an option. It reflects the expected volatility over the life of the option, based on the assumption that the option’s current price reflects market expectations.

  • Implied volatility is not directly observable and must be derived from market prices using an option pricing model.
  • It tends to rise during periods of market uncertainty or stress, reflecting increased expectations of future price swings.
  • Unlike historical volatility, which looks at past price movements, implied volatility is forward-looking and reflects the market’s consensus on future price movements.
  • Different options on the same asset may have different implied volatilities, a phenomenon known as the “volatility smile” or “volatility skew.”
  • The VIX S&P 500 Volatility Index.

5.1.3 Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) Volatility Model

Definition

The Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) model is a time-series model used to estimate volatility. It assigns exponentially decreasing weights to historical observations, meaning that more recent returns have a greater influence on the volatility estimate than older returns.

The EWMA model is frequently used in Value at Risk (VaR) calculations and risk management due to its simplicity and ability to reflect changing market conditions in real time. It is part of the RiskMetrics methodology developed by J.P. Morgan. The model is a good balance between responsiveness to new information and smoothness, making it a commonly used approach for tracking market volatility.

The EWMA volatility at time \(t\), denoted as \(\sigma_t\), is calculated using the following recursive equation:

\[ \sigma_t^2 = \lambda \sigma_{t-1}^2 + (1 - \lambda) r_{t-1}^2 \]

Where:

  • \(\sigma_t^2 \dots\) is the variance (or squared volatility) at time \(t\).
  • \(\lambda \dots\) the decay factor, which determines the weighting of past data, typically close to 1 (e.g., 0.94 for daily returns as recommended by RiskMetrics).
  • \(r_{t-1} \dots\) the return at time \(t-1\). The more extreme the return (e.g., a large price change), the greater its impact on the volatility estimate.
  • \(\sigma_{t-1}^2 \dots\) the variance at time \(t-1\).

Properties of EWMA

  • Recency Bias: EWMA gives more weight to recent data, which allows it to adapt more quickly to changing market conditions.
  • No Mean Reversion: Unlike other models such as GARCH, EWMA does not assume that volatility will revert to a long-term mean.
  • Simple to Implement: It only requires one parameter (the decay factor) and is computationally straightforward.
  • Stationarity: EWMA does not assume stationarity of returns, allowing for changing volatility over time.
  • Adaptability: The model adjusts more quickly to large market events (e.g., shocks), making it well-suited for capturing periods of volatility clustering.

5.1.4 ARCH Model

The ARCH model was developed by Robert Engle in 1982 to model time-varying volatility by relating the current variance of a time series to the squared errors of previous periods. In an ARCH(1) model (the simplest form):

\[ \sigma_t^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 r_{t-1}^2 \]

Where:

  • \(\sigma_t^2 \dots\) the variance at time \(t\).
  • \(r_{t-1} \dots\) the return at time \(t-1\).
  • \(\alpha_0 \dots\) a constant (long-term average variance).
  • \(\alpha_1 \dots\) the coefficient applied to the lagged squared return.

However, the ARCH model has a limitation in that it only relies on past squared returns, making it less flexible when volatility persistence needs to be captured over long periods.

5.1.5 GARCH Model

The Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH) model, developed by Tim Bollerslev in 1986, extends the ARCH model by incorporating both lagged squared returns and past variances. The most commonly used version is GARCH(1,1), which takes the following form:

\[ \sigma_t^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 r_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 \sigma_{t-1}^2 \]

Where:

  • \(\sigma_t^2 \dots\) the variance at time \(t\).
  • \(\alpha_0 \dots\) is a constant (long-term average level of volatility).
  • \(\alpha_1 \dots\) is the coefficient applied to the lagged squared return (\(r_{t-1}^2\), controls the impact of recent returns on volatility).
  • \(\beta_1 \dots\) is the coefficient applied to the lagged variance (\(\sigma_{t-1}^2\), representing volatility persistence).

The GARCH model is widely used for risk management, option pricing, and forecasting financial market volatility, making it a key tool for market participants who need to understand volatility dynamics in real time.

Properties of GARCH

  • Volatility Clustering: GARCH captures the tendency for volatility to cluster, as seen in financial markets where volatility tends to remain high after market shocks.
  • Mean Reversion: The model assumes that volatility will eventually revert to a long-term mean, though it can remain elevated for some time following a market event.
  • Leverage Effects: GARCH can be extended to asymmetric models (e.g., GJR-GARCH, EGARCH) to capture the fact that negative returns often have a larger impact on volatility than positive returns.

5.1.6 Why Financial Institutions Still Use Traditional Models?

Volatility is central to financial risk management as it measures the uncertainty of asset price movements. Financial institutions rely on volatility estimates for tasks like Value at Risk (VaR), capital allocation, and option pricing. Accurate volatility modeling helps manage exposures, comply with regulations, and allocate capital efficiently.

Despite the advancements in machine learning and AI, traditional models like EWMA and GARCH still dominate because:

  • Simplicity: These models are easy to implement and interpret, making them suitable for real-time risk management.
  • Regulatory Acceptance: Widely used and recognized by regulators, these models align with frameworks like Basel III.
  • Efficiency: They provide reliable volatility estimates with limited data and computational power.
  • Stability: EWMA and GARCH are less prone to overfitting and work well with limited historical data.

While machine learning offers potential for improved volatility modeling, financial institutions continue to favor EWMA and GARCH due to their simplicity, regulatory compliance, and interpretability. For now, traditional models remain the standard, with AI techniques gradually integrating into risk management as regulatory acceptance grows.

5.2 Correlations

Risk managers must estimate both volatilities and correlations between changes in market variables to accurately assess risk exposures. Monitoring correlations is crucial, as it helps identify relationships between variables that can impact portfolio risk. Similar techniques used for monitoring volatilities, such as historical data analysis or time series models (EWMA, GARCH), can be applied to correlations.

Definition

The correlation between two variables, \(V_1\) and \(V_2\), is quantified by the correlation coefficient \(\rho\), which measures the strength and direction of their linear relationship.

It is calculated as follows:

\[ \rho = \frac{E(V_1 V_2) - E(V_1) E(V_2)}{SD(V_1)SD(V_2)} \]

  • Here, \(E(.)\) represents the expected value, and \(SD(.)\) stands for standard deviation.
  • If there is no linear relationship between \(V_1\) and \(V_2\), then \(E(V_1 V_2) = E(V_1)E(V_2)\), resulting in \(\rho = 0\).
  • A perfect positive correlation occurs when \(V_1 = V_2\), yielding \(\rho = 1\).

It is important to note that zero correlation does not imply statistical independence. Variables may still be dependent through non-linear relationships, which correlation does not capture. Correlation strictly measures linear dependence, but variables can exhibit other forms of dependence.

5.2.1 Covariance and Its Role in Correlation

Covariance between two variables \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) is defined as:

\[ \text{cov}(V_1, V_2) = E(V_1 V_2) - E(V_1)E(V_2) \]

This provides a direct measure of the joint variability of \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) and can be used to express correlation:

\[ \rho = \frac{\text{cov}(V_1, V_2)}{SD(V_1)SD(V_2)} \]

While correlation is a normalized measure (bounded between -1 and 1) and often more intuitive, covariance remains fundamental for deeper risk analysis. Covariance is crucial in risk management for understanding how assets co-vary and contribute to overall portfolio volatility. For example, covariance is used to compute portfolio variance, where each asset’s contribution depends on its covariance with other assets.

5.3 Copulas

Definition

A copula is a way to link different events (or variables) and understand how they are connected—even if they don’t follow the same kind of distribution.

  • A copula links univariate marginal distributions to form a joint multivariate distribution.
  • By using copulas, risk managers can separately analyze the behavior of individual risk factors (via their marginal distributions) and their dependencies (via the copula function).
  • This separation is especially useful because it allows for the independent fitting of marginal distributions and dependency structures.
  • Limits and Criticisms: Despite their advantages, copulas have limitations. The use of Gaussian copulas in the 2007-2008 financial crisis was criticized because they failed to capture extreme tail dependence, leading to an underestimation of risk in mortgage-backed securities.
Example

Let’s say a bank holds both stocks and bonds. Normally, these two assets might not be closely related, meaning if stocks go down, bonds might stay steady. However, in a financial crisis, both assets could drop sharply at the same time. A copula model helps risk managers figure out how likely it is for extreme events to happen together, helping them prepare for scenarios where the usual diversification strategies might fail.

5.3.1 Applications in Risk Management

  1. Portfolio Risk Management: Copulas model the dependence between assets, improving risk measures like Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES) by capturing extreme joint movements.
  2. Credit Risk Management: Widely used in pricing credit derivatives like CDOs, copulas model joint default probabilities (default correlations).
  3. Operational Risk Management: Copulas model dependencies among low-frequency, high-impact events (e.g., legal risks, fraud), enhancing the accuracy of capital requirements.
  4. Insurance Risk Management: Copulas model dependencies between claims across sectors or regions, helping insurers manage and diversify risk.
  5. Stress Testing: Copulas improve stress testing by capturing dependencies between risk factors, leading to more realistic extreme scenario modeling.

5.3.2 Gaussian Copula

Definition

A Gaussian copula is a type of copula that uses the multivariate normal distribution to model the dependence structure between random variables. It captures how different variables move together, assuming their joint relationship follows a normal distribution. However, it doesn’t make any assumptions about the individual (marginal) distributions of the variables, which can be non-normal.

  • Mathematically, a Gaussian copula links the cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of individual variables to form a joint distribution using the CDF of a multivariate normal distribution.
  • Linear Dependence: It works well for modeling linear relationships between variables but does not capture tail dependence (extreme events happening together), meaning it may underestimate joint extreme events.

5.3.3 Formula for the Gaussian Copula:

Let \(F_1, F_2, ..., F_n\) be the marginal cumulative distribution functions of \(n\) random variables \(X_1, X_2, ..., X_n\). The Gaussian copula \(C\) is defined as:

\[ C(u_1, u_2, ..., u_n) = \Phi_{\Sigma} \left( \Phi^{-1}(u_1), \Phi^{-1}(u_2), ..., \Phi^{-1}(u_n) \right) \]

Where: - \(u_i = F_i(X_i)\) are the transformed values of the variables using their marginal distributions. - \(\Phi^{-1}\) is the inverse of the univariate standard normal CDF. - \(\Phi_{\Sigma}\) is the multivariate normal CDF with a correlation matrix \(\Sigma\) describing the dependence between the variables.

5.3.4 Why is the Gaussian Copula Important in Risk Management?

The Gaussian copula became widely used in risk management, particularly in modeling the joint default probabilities of financial assets (like in credit derivatives).

5.3.5 Advantages:

  • Simplicity: It’s relatively easy to use and implement.
  • Flexibility in Margins: Allows for different marginal distributions for each variable, while capturing the dependence between them.

5.3.6 Limitations:

  • Failure to Capture Tail Dependence: The Gaussian copula assumes dependence near the center of the distribution, but it struggles to capture tail dependence—the likelihood of extreme events happening together. This was a key issue during the 2007-2008 financial crisis, where extreme events (e.g., widespread defaults) were more common than the Gaussian copula suggested.
  • Overreliance on Correlation: It relies heavily on the assumption that relationships between variables are primarily driven by linear correlation, which may not hold true in financial markets, especially during crises.

5.3.7 Example in Risk Management:

The Gaussian copula was famously used in pricing Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs), where it was applied to model the joint likelihood of mortgage defaults. However, it underestimated the probability of multiple defaults happening simultaneously because it didn’t account for tail risk, contributing to the mispricing of these instruments leading up to the 2007-2008 financial crisis.

5.3.8 Summary:

The Gaussian copula is a powerful tool for modeling dependence between variables, assuming their relationship is primarily linear. While it offers flexibility in terms of the marginal distributions of the variables, its major limitation is the inability to account for extreme co-movements, making it less suitable for capturing tail risk in financial crises.

5.3.9 Types of Copulas in Risk Management:

  • Gaussian Copula: Assumes variables have a normal (bell-curve) type of dependence. This copula was widely used before the 2008 financial crisis but turned out to underestimate the risk of extreme events happening together.
  • t-Copula: Similar to the Gaussian copula, but it has heavier tails, meaning it is better at capturing the risk of extreme co-movements (important for crisis situations).

5.4 Copulas

5.4.1 Gaussian Copula

  • A Gaussian copula is a specific type of copula used to model the dependency structure among various dimensions or variables.
  • It assumes that these dependencies follow a multivariate normal distribution.
  • To understand the Gaussian copula, consider a collection of random variables.
  • Each of these variables has its own individual (marginal) distribution, which describes its behavior.
  • The copula then describes how these variables interact or are correlated with each other.

Simplified description of how a Gaussian copula is constructed:

  1. Start with random variables: Let’s say we have a set of random variables, each following its own arbitrary distribution.
  2. Transform to uniform: Apply the cumulative distribution function (CDF) to each random variable. This transforms each variable into a uniform distribution on the interval [0, 1].
  3. Apply inverse Gaussian CDF: Take these uniform variables and apply the inverse of the cumulative distribution function of the multivariate normal distribution. This step ensures that the new variables have a multivariate normal distribution.
  4. Correlation Matrix: The Gaussian copula is parameterized by a correlation matrix, which captures the dependencies among the variables.
  • The Gaussian copula is popular due to its simplicity and the tractability of the Gaussian distribution.
  • However, it has limitations, particularly in modeling tail dependencies.
  • It was famously used in the pricing of collateralized debt obligations (CDOs) and was implicated in the mispricing of risk that contributed to the 2008 financial crisis.
Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import norm

# Set the random seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# Generate independent data
data_independent = np.random.normal(size=(1000, 2))

# Generate correlated data
correlated_mean = [0, 0]
correlated_cov = [[1, 0.9], [0.9, 1]]
data_correlated = np.random.multivariate_normal(
    correlated_mean, correlated_cov, size=1000
)

# Gaussian Copula for independent data
u_independent = norm.cdf(data_independent)

# Gaussian Copula for correlated data
u_correlated = norm.cdf(data_correlated)

# Plotting both scatter plots side by side
fig, axs = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 5))

# Scatter plot for independent data
axs[0].scatter(u_independent[:, 0], u_independent[:, 1], marker=".", alpha=0.5)
axs[0].set_xlabel("U1")
axs[0].set_ylabel("U2")
axs[0].set_title("Gaussian Copula with Independent Variables")

# Scatter plot for correlated data
axs[1].scatter(u_correlated[:, 0], u_correlated[:, 1], marker=".", alpha=0.5)
axs[1].set_xlabel("U1")
axs[1].set_ylabel("U2")
axs[1].set_title("Gaussian Copula with Correlated Variables")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

5.4.3 Clayton Copula Example

  • Tail Dependence: The main difference between the Clayton and Gaussian copulas is how they model tail dependence. The Clayton copula is suitable for modeling lower tail dependence (correlation between extreme negative values), while the Gaussian copula can model both lower and upper tail dependence, but not as effectively for extreme values.

  • Parameterization: The Clayton copula uses a single parameter (the Clayton parameter) to determine the strength and direction of the dependence. In contrast, the Gaussian copula uses a correlation matrix to model the linear dependence between variables.

  • Origins: The Clayton copula is an Archimedean copula, while the Gaussian copula is derived from the multivariate normal distribution.

5.5 Valuation and Scenario Analysis

Valuation estimates the current value of financial assets or liabilities, essential for assessing risk exposure. It is typically calculated as the present value of future cash flows, adjusted for risk factors.

Scenario analysis assesses how hypothetical or historical events impact asset values *and risk metrics, focusing on adverse outcomes to answer, “How bad can things get?”

Types of Scenarios:

  • Historical: Based on real past events (e.g., the 2008 financial crisis).
  • Hypothetical: Extreme market shocks or stress events.
  • Regulatory: Scenarios required by regulators for stress testing.

Real-World vs. Risk-Neutral Projections:

  • Real-World Projections: Used in scenario analysis to reflect actual risk and investor behavior.
  • Risk-Neutral Projections: Applied in valuation, especially for time-evolving variables or events like company defaults.
