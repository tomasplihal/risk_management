Learning Outcomes:

  • Define risk and risk management.
  • Describe features of a risk management framework.
  • Identify financial and non-financial sources of risk and describe how they may interact.
  • Describe methods for measuring and modifying risk exposures and factors to consider in choosing among the methods.

1.1 Introduction

  • Investment involves inherent risk, and risk management is crucial in the investment process.
  • Risk identification, measurement, and control are essential for proper investing and achieving objectives.
  • Portfolio managers need to understand risk management both for portfolios and the enterprises they represent.
  • Risks should be evaluated to determine which are worth taking regularly, occasionally, or should be avoided.

1.2 The Risk Management Process

Definition of risk
  • Risk \(\rightarrow\) exposure to uncertainty.
  • Risk is the chance of a loss or adverse outcome as a result of an action, inaction, or external event.
  • It is not about avoiding risk, it is about managing risk.
  • Risk and return are the interconnected forces of the financial universe.
  • Risk can be managed or controlled as opposed to return.
  • Risk exposure \(\rightarrow\) the state of being exposed or vulnerable to a risk.
Example

Suppose there is an important announcement in Japan that will result in the yen either appreciating or depreciating by 1%. The risk can be described as the range of resulting outcomes and is often thought of in terms of a probability distribution of future returns.

Suppose that the underlying amount is ¥1,000,000 which result in ¥10,000 of risk exposure (1% of ¥1,000,000). The risk management process would inform the decision of whether to operate or invest in this risky currency.

  • The term risk has three related but different meanings:
    1. Risk driver for the underlying risk. The underlying uncertainty (e.g., the uncertain +1% or –1% movement of the currency).
    2. Risk position to describe or quantify the risky action taken (e.g., the ¥1,000,000 worth of risky currency).
    3. Risk exposure for the potential valuation change that may occurs (e.g., +¥10,000 or –¥10,000 risky outcome that might accrue to the business).
Definition of risk management

Risk management is the process by which an organization or individual defines the level of risk to be taken, measures the level of risk being taken, and adjusts the latter toward the former, with the goal of maximizing the company’s or portfolio’s value or the individual’s overall satisfaction, or utility.

Alternative definition: Risk management comprises all the decisions and actions needed to best achieve organizational or personal objectives while bearing a tolerable level of risk.

  • Actively understand and embrace risks that balance goal achievement with an acceptable failure chance, then quantify, monitor, and adjust them.
  • Good risk management offers a comprehensive framework that guides decisions before, during, and after a risk event, rather than preventing losses.
  • The best risk management might not prevent losses in a market crash or reduced profits during an economic downturn.
  • Risks and exposures are dynamic, risk management is a continuous process.

1.3 The Risk Management Framework

  • A risk management framework involves the infrastructure, process, and analytics needed to support effective risk management in an organization.
  • It integrates the “risk” and “return” aspects of the enterprise into decisions in support of best achieving its goals within its tolerance for risk.
  • Although there are a very high number of risks faced by every organization, most organizations are primarily affected by a small number of key risk drivers, or primary underlying factors that create risk.

1.3.1 Key Factors

  1. Risk governance:
    • The top-down process and guidance that directs risk management activities.
    • Enterprise risk management aligns risk management with the objectives, health, and value of the entire organization
  2. Risk identification and measurement:
    • Analyzing the environment for relevant risk drivers.
    • Analyzing and tracking the business or portfolio to ascertain risk exposures.
    • Calculating risk metrics to size these risks under various scenarios and stresses.
  3. Risk infrastructure:
    • Refers to essential personnel and systems for tracking risk and conducting quantitative analyses (e.g., databases, analytic models).
  4. Defined policies and processes:
    • Extends governance into daily operations, setting guidelines and limits aligned with risk tolerance and regulations.
  5. Risk monitoring, mitigation, and management:
    • A complex facet that integrates all aspects of the framework.
    • Involves ongoing review and action to align risk exposure with tolerance.
  6. Communications:
    • A continuous process across the organization.
    • Ensures clarity in governance, risk metrics, and exposure changes to enable appropriate action.
  7. Strategic analysis or integration:
    • Utilizes risk management proactively to enhance performance and overall value.

1.4 Risk Tolerance and Risk Budgeting

  • Risk tolerance defines how much risk an organization is willing to accept, including potential losses, opportunity costs, or failure in meeting objectives.
  • It identifies critical shortfalls or failures in achieving goals and exposure to uncertain forces or risk drivers.
  • It must be formally determined and communicated prior to any crisis, guiding management in the strategic selection of risks.
  • Risk budgeting focuses on the implementation of the risk tolerance decisions approved by the management at the strategic or governance level.
  • The risk budget will quantify risk by specific metrics and allocate risk across the organization.
  • Single-dimension measures: standard deviation, beta, value at risk (VaR), and scenario loss.
  • Multi-dimensional approaches for example evaluate risks by their underlying risk classes, such as equity, fixed income, commodity risk, and so on, and then allocates investments by their risk class (factor analysis).
  • Even basic risk budgeting promotes intentional risk trade-offs and a culture where risk is integrated into major decisions.
  • It aims to invest where return per risk unit is highest, affecting choices like opting for a market hedge or a less risky investment.

1.5 Identification of Risk

1.5.1 Financial Risks

Definition
  • Financial risks originate from the financial markets (e.g., changes in prices or interest rates).
  • Market risk arises from movements in interest rates, stock prices, exchange rates, and commodity prices.
  • Credit risk, also known as default risk or counterparty risk, is the risk of loss if one party fails to pay an amount owed on an obligation, such as a bond, loan, or derivative, to another party.
  • Liquidity risk is the risk of a significant downward valuation adjustment when selling a financial asset.
    • It is related to transaction costs in the market (bid-ask spread).
    • Depends on time-varying asset liquidity and size of a position.

1.5.2 Non-Financial Risks

Definition
  • Non-financial risks are risks that are not primarily related to financial markets but arise from various sources (e.g., actions within the organization or from external origins such as the environment, relationship with counterparties, regulators, governments, suppliers, customers, etc.).
  • Settlement risk is the risk of payment failure just before a default (e.g., you send money to your counterparty and it defaults).
  • Legal risk includes the risk of being sued and the risk that contract terms won’t be upheld.
  • Regulatory, accounting, and tax risks are related and collectively referred to as compliance risk.
  • Model risk is the risk of valuation error from using the wrong model or using the right model incorrectly.
  • Tail risk refers to events in the distribution tail that are more frequent than expected.
  • Operational risk arises from inadequate people, systems, internal policies, and external events (e.g., theft, accounting fraud, rogue traders, natural disasters, cyber risk, etc.).
  • Solvency risk (sometimes called liquidity risk) is the risk of running out of cash, even if an organization is otherwise solvent.

1.6 Interactions Between Risks

  • Risks in different categories can often be classified into multiple categories.
    • Market risk leads to credit risk, which in turn leads to operational risk.
    • Credit risk is closely linked to settlement risk.
    • Legal risk can arise from market or credit risk, as parties search for loopholes in contracts.
  • Risks can interact and compound, leading to higher costs and lower expected payoffs, particularly under stressed market conditions.
  • Such adverse risk interactions were notable during the 2008 financial crisis.
  • Combined risk is usually worse than the sum of individual risks (non-linear pattern).
  • Up-front scenario planning is valuable in understanding risk interactions.
  • Risk models and systems overlook these interactions, making the consequences worse.
  • Governance bodies, company management, and analysts must recognize the potential dangers of risk interactions and avoid treating risks as isolated and unrelated.

1.7 Measuring and Modifying Risk

1.7.1 Drivers

  • All risks arise from the fact that the future is unknown.
  • Financial markets reflect global and domestic economic conditions, industry factors, and company-specific characteristics.
  • Government policies, regulations, laws, and economic factors influence the economic environment and impact the activities of companies.
  • Systematic risks affect economies and industries, while idiosyncratic (unsystematic) risks are specific to individual companies.
  • Diversified portfolios aim to eliminate unsystematic risks, but unsystematic risks still matter to company management, poorly diversified investors, and financial analysts.
  • Unsystematic risks can sometimes become systematic, such as when poor credit risk management in a major bank triggers a global financial crisis.

1.7.2 Metrics

  • Risk metrics quantify risk exposure.
  • Common measures include:
    • Probability: Relative frequency of outcomes.
    • Standard Deviation: Outcome range; limited for non-normal distributions.
    • Beta: Sensitivity to market returns.
    • Greeks: Specialized measures for derivatives (e.g., delta, gamma).
    • Duration: Specific to fixed-income assets.
    • Value at Risk (VaR): Extreme loss size; subject to model risk and limitations.
      • Variants include Conditional VaR, extreme value theory, scenario analysis, and stress testing.
  • Credit risk assessment includes credit ratings, liquidity, solvency, profitability, leverage, and macroeconomic factors.
  • Measuring credit risk is challenging due to the rarity of credit events, but default probabilities can be estimated by aggregating companies with similar characteristics or analyzing market prices of derivative instruments (e.g., credit default swaps).
  • Operational risk is difficult to measure and involves assessing the likelihood and potential losses of rare events such as cyberattacks.
  • Some risks like changes in accounting or regulation may require subjective evaluation.

1.8 Methods of Risk Modification

  • Risk modification aligns actual risk with acceptable levels and strategic objectives.
  • It includes risk reduction (or hedging) and may vary by organizational goals.
  • Categories of risk modification: prevention and avoidance, acceptance, transfer, and shifting.

1.8.1 Risk Prevention and Avoidance

  • Risk avoidance may not always be possible or desirable, as there is often a trade-off between cost and benefits.
  • Strategic decisions made at the governance level.
  • Involves fine-tuning exposure to achieve desired risk levels.
  • If risk exceeds acceptable levels, solutions include self-insuring, risk transfer, and shifting.

1.8.2 Risk Acceptance: Self-Insurance and Diversification

  • Self-insurance involves bearing a risk that is considered costly to eliminate by external means.
  • Self-insuring can mean accepting the risk as is or establishing a reserve to cover potential losses.
  • It must align with an enterprise’s risk tolerance.
  • Diversification is a risk-mitigation technique that involves spreading investments across different assets or sectors.
  • Diversification is central to modern portfolio analysis and is often combined with other risk modification strategies.

1.8.3 Risk Transfer

  • Risk transfer involves passing on a risk to another party through insurance policies.
  • Insurers diversify risks, charge premiums, and manage correlated risks, offering specialized coverage for unique risks.

1.8.4 Risk Shifting

  • Risk shifting changes the distribution of risk outcomes and is associated with derivatives.
  • Derivatives, like forward commitments and contingent claims, allow efficient shifting of risk across probability distributions and from one party to another.
  • Derivatives can be created on exchanges or privately between parties.

1.8.5 Choosing a Method for Modifying Risk

  • Organizations often use multiple methods, and the choice depends on costs, benefits, and risk tolerance.
  • Companies with foreign operations often hedge exchange rate risk using derivatives.
  • Risk prevention and avoidance are preferred for risks with little benefit.
  • Self-insurance can be an option for organizations with ample cash flow, but it may not cover all risks.
  • Risk transfer through insurance may not be suitable for all risks, especially those that can affect many parties simultaneously.
  • Risk shifting using derivatives is common for financial risks that exceed risk appetite.
  • Different risk management methods have varying impacts on risk reduction and remaining risk profiles.
