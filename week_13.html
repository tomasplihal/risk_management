13.1 Financial Innovation

  • Technological changes have led to disintermediation in finance.
  • New tech-based intermediaries emerge (reintermediation).
  • Financial firms, like banks, face disruption from FinTech.
  • Future FinTech will impact digitization, customer behavior, and firm adaptation.
  • These changes are of particular relevance to risk management professionals.

13.1.1 Technological Advances

  • Dramatic increase in computer processing power since the 1960s.
  • Introduction of Robotics Process Automation (RPA) for tasks such as customer account creation and employee hiring.

13.1.1.1 Machine Learning

  • Differentiates from third industrial revolution through data-driven decision-making.
  • Applied in areas like credit decision automation, improving upon traditional human-led methods.
  • Types of Machine Learning:
    1. Unsupervised learning: Identifies patterns and clusters, e.g., customer segmentation.
    2. Supervised learning: Prediction and classification, e.g., house valuation or loan default prediction.
    3. Reinforcement learning: Sequential decision-making, e.g., hedging strategies in derivatives trading.
  • Challenges:
    • Overfitting: Model may not generalize to new data.
    • Solution: Use training, validation, and test datasets to estimate model accuracy.

13.1.1.2 Blockchain Summary

  • Challenges with Traditional Ledgers: Risks include fraud, lack of trust, hacking, and inconsistencies across multiple record-keepers.
  • Blockchain as a Solution: A distributed ledger technology allowing multiple entries controlled by a community, aiming to negate the aforementioned risks.
  • Each block is sealed using a cryptographic hash, ensuring data integrity and tamper-proofing.
  • In blockchain systems like Bitcoin, miners are rewarded for validating and adding new blocks, thereby ensuring ongoing, decentralized maintenance.
  • Types of Blockchains:
    • Permissionless: Open to all, like Bitcoin.
    • Permissioned: Restricted access; often used by financial institutions for efficient transaction settlements.
  • Potential in Financial Sector: Blockchain is increasingly being adopted for trading securities, settlements, and back-office functions, aiming for near real-time operations.
  • Blockchain poses both threats and opportunities—potentially lowering bank fees while opening new service avenues.
  • May also lower operational risks and regulatory capital requirements.
  • Adoption and utility in various sectors are still under evaluation; however, it holds significant potential for operational improvements.

13.1.2 Payment Systems

  • Evolution from cash to digital payments, with rapid adoption in developing countries.
  • Key attributes: speed, convenience, security, simplicity, and cost.
  • Innovations: Instant fund transfer (PayPal), digital wallets (Apple Pay), and use of cryptocurrencies.
  • Security: Focus on biometric authentication to reduce fraud and costs.
  • Privacy concerns: Data collection and biometric information.
  • Credit facilities: High-interest credit cards versus FinTech solutions like PayPal’s free 14-day credit.
  • Future services: Easier foreign exchange and data analytics.
  • Data-driven credit decisions: Risks around privacy and data selling.
  • Future trends: Decline of cash, political resistance to full digitization.
  • Cryptocurrencies: Volatile but secure, growing acceptance.
  • Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs): Emergence and implications for financial control.

13.1.3 Lending

13.1.3.1 P2P Lending

  • P2P lending disintermediates traditional banks but adds new intermediaries for risk assessment and transaction facilitation.
  • P2P often serves borrowers rejected by traditional banks, potentially charging higher interest rates than conventional loans but lower than credit cards.
  • Diversification and secondary markets are crucial for lenders in P2P platforms to manage risks.

13.1.3.2 Crowdfunding

  • Crowdfunding democratizes equity investment but faces valuation complexities and regulatory hindrances.
  • JOBS Act in the U.S. provides some regulatory leeway, but limitations on funding exist.
  • ICOs represent a speculative, risky, and largely unregulated form of crowdfunding with potential for both fraud and success.

13.1.4 Wealth Management

  • Traditional Wealth Management:
    • Highly profitable for banks, with fees ranging from 1% to 1.5% per annum.
    • Involves client risk assessment and investment recommendations based on risk profiles.
  • Emergence of index funds in 1975 by John Bogle, offering low fees (0.15%) and no need for human managers.
  • Robo-Advisers:
    • Appeared around 2010, subject to regulatory oversight.
    • Utilize digital platforms for risk assessment and automated portfolio rebalancing.
    • Lower fees, typically around 0.25% per annum.
    • Can address cognitive biases.
    • Popularity may be cyclical, depending on market performance.

13.1.5 InsurTech

  • Nature of Insurance Industry:
    • Resistant to disruption due to need for stability and strict regulations.
    • Difficult for newcomers to enter without significant capital.
    • Brokers and agents facilitate customer-insurer interactions.
    • Trend towards disintermediation; websites for direct customer dealings.
  • Technological Innovations:
    • Online quote comparison made easy.
    • Data collection for risk assessment.
    • Enables precise risk profiling for premium adjustments.
    • Potentially simplifies claims in case of accidents.
  • InsurTech unlikely to disrupt, but adoption of technology is advantageous.

13.1.6 Regulation and Compliance

  • Financial institutions face a high regulatory barrier, affecting small FinTech startups.
  • Regulators aim to balance innovation with client safety.
  • “Sandbox” programs provide a testing environment for new financial products.
  • Challenges include jurisdiction issues and evaluating FinTech’s primarily code-based assets.
  • Technology simplifies and streamlines regulatory compliance.
  • Real-time monitoring helps avoid fines and ensures international compliance.

13.1.7 How Should Financial Institutions Respond?

  • FinTech poses strategic risk to established financial entities.
  • Institutions should adapt business models to changing consumer behavior.
  • Various strategies include in-house development, acquisitions, and partnerships with startups.
  • Flexibility and Adaptability: Key for surviving in the changing landscape.

13.2 Risk Management Mistakes to Avoid

  • Clear and unambiguous risk limits essential for both financial and non-financial companies.
  • Limits should be established at the board level and trickled down to individual risk managers.
  • Daily reports must align with actual gains and losses for accurate risk assessment.
  • Special Attention to Derivatives:
    • Close monitoring of derivatives required to differentiate between hedging, speculation, or arbitrage.
  • Issue of Exceeding Risk Limits for Profit:
    • Profiting from exceeding risk limits creates a risky culture.
    • Penalties should apply equally for both profitable and unprofitable violations.
  • Misconception of Market Predictability:
    • Success in trading often attributed to luck rather than skill.
    • Risk limits should not be increased just because a trader shows consistent profits.
  • Importance of Diversification:
    • Risks can be halved by diversifying across 20 stocks, making it a poor choice to limit to one market variable.
  • Necessity of Scenario Analyses and Stress Tests:
    • Risk measures like VaR should be complemented by stress tests.
    • Results of stress testing should inform strategic decision-making, often ignored during good times.

13.2.1 Managing the Trading Room

  • All traders should be scrutinized, even high-performing ones, to ensure they are not taking undue risks or manipulating systems.
  • Separation of Front, Middle, and Back Office to prevent internal mishandling of trades.
  • Financial models are not foolproof and should not be blindly trusted.
  • Model failures have led to significant losses for financial institutions.
  • Profits from selling exotic instruments should be recognized conservatively over the life of the deal to mitigate the risk of aggressive trading behavior.
  • Financial institutions should avoid selling inappropriate high-risk products to clients, as it undermines long-term trust and can lead to legal repercussions.
  • Situations offering seemingly easy profits should be scrutinized for potential hidden risks.

13.2.2 Liquidity Risk

  • Traders use actively traded instruments to price infrequently traded ones.
  • Risk arises when assuming these can be traded close to theoretical prices, especially in market shocks.
  • The need for scenario analyses and stress testing.
  • Dangers escalate when multiple participants follow the same trading strategy, causing market volatility.
  • Excessive use of short-term funding for long-term needs increases liquidity risks.
  • Basel III introduced liquidity requirements.
  • Lack of transparency in structured products exacerbated the 2007 credit crisis.
  • Advocates for more information about underlying assets to minimize market disruptions (flight to quality during crisis).

13.2.3 Lessons for Nonfinancial Corporations

13.2.3.1 Understand Your Trades

  • Corporations should fully understand the trade or trading strategy before execution.
  • If senior management doesn’t understand a trade, it shouldn’t be approved.
  • Valuing the financial instrument is a way to ensure understanding.
  • Relying solely on investment bankers for valuation can be risky.

13.2.3.2 Hedgers vs Speculators

  • Initially, a trader may focus on hedging but might gradually transition to speculation.
  • A loss in speculation can lead to risky attempts to recover, potentially causing disaster.
  • Senior management should set clear risk limits and controls.

13.2.3.3 Treasury as Profit Center: Be Cautious

  • Making the treasury department a profit center can be tempting but is fraught with risks.
  • The treasurer might take additional risks to improve the bottom line.
  • The primary goal of a hedging program is to reduce risks, not to increase profits.
  • Treating the treasury as a profit center could lead to speculation and financial disasters.

13.2.4 A Final Point

  • Known Risks: Quantifiable risks like market and credit risks.
  • Unknown Risks: Known events with indeterminable probability, e.g., operational risks.
  • Unknowable Risks: Risks where even the event is unknown; often termed as “black swans.”
  • Known risks are called “risk,” while unknown risks are termed “uncertainty” (Knight, 1921).
  • Understanding and preparing for various kinds of risks can significantly mitigate the potential for catastrophic losses.

13.2.4.1 Managing Unknown Risks

  • Subjective probabilities often used.
  • Flexibility is key; avoid excessive leverage and aim for variable costs.
  • Insurance products may be developed to cover some unknown risks.

13.2.4.2 Managing Unknowable Risks

  • Most challenging to handle due to their unpredictability.
  • Diversification across products and markets can add a layer of protection.
  • Insurance products for unknowable risks are an area of future development.
