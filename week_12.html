12.1 Basel I, Basel II, and Solvency II

  • The Basel Accord of 1988 initiated international standards for bank regulation.
  • Regulatory evolution has seen new rules modify old ones while retaining certain approaches.
  • Understanding history is crucial for grasping the current regulatory landscape.
  • The chapter outlines Basel I, netting provisions, and the 1996 Amendment.
  • Basel II, introduced around 2007, significantly reformed the regulatory framework.
  • Solvency II, akin to Basel II, was adopted by the European Union in 2016 for insurance companies.

12.1.1 The Reasons for Regulating Banks

  • The primary aim of bank regulation is to ensure sufficient capital against risks.
  • Governments aim to minimize the probability of bank defaults for economic stability.
  • Self-regulation by banks is not historically supported; formal regulation enhances capital and risk awareness.
  • Deposit insurance programs, while protecting depositors, can inadvertently encourage risk-taking (“moral hazard”).
  • Systemic risk is a major concern, as a default by one large bank can trigger a financial system collapse.
  • Governments face a dilemma between bailing out struggling institutions and risking systemic collapse.
  • The 2008 market turmoil involved both bailouts and allowed failures, exemplified by Lehman Brothers, to send market signals.

12.1.2 Bank Regulation Pre-1988

  • Pre-1988 bank regulation focused on setting minimum capital-to-asset ratios within individual countries.
  • Regulatory stringency and definitions varied globally, leading to competitive imbalances.
  • Major international loans and complex transactions, like OTC derivatives, exposed the inadequacy of existing capital requirements.
  • Basel Committee formed in 1974 to standardize regulations.
  • 1988 BIS Accord, later Basel I, was the Committee’s first major output.

12.1.3 The 1988 BIS Accord

  • First international risk-based standards for capital adequacy.
  • Signed by all 12 Basel Committee members.
  • Increased resources for risk measurement and management in banks.
  • The Accord set the groundwork for modern risk management but faced criticisms for being too simplistic.
  • Cooke Ratio: Focused on total risk-weighted assets (credit risk exposures).
  • Categories of Credit Risk Exposures:
    1. On-Balance Sheet Assets: Excludes derivatives.
    2. Off-Balance Sheet Items: Excludes derivatives.
    3. Over-the-Counter Derivatives: Includes swaps, forwards, etc.

12.1.3.1 Key Equations:

  • Risk-Weighted Assets (On-Balance): \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i L_i \]

  • where \(L_i\) is the principal amount and \(w_i\) is its risk weight.

  • Credit Equivalent Amount (Off-Balance):

  • Conversion factors applied to principal amounts of instruments like bankers’ acceptances, guarantees, and loan commitments.

  • Credit Equivalent Amount (Derivatives): \[ \text{max}(V, 0) + aL \]

  • where \(V\) is current value, \(a\) is an add-on factor, and \(L\) is the principal amount.

  • Total Risk-Weighted Assets: \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i L_i + \sum_{j=1}^{M} w^*_j C_j \]

  • where \(C_j\) is the credit equivalent amount for off-balance-sheet items, and \(w^*_j\) is the risk weight of the counterparty.

12.1.3.2 Capital Requirements

  • Required banks to maintain capital equal to at least 8% of total risk-weighted assets.
  • Tier 1 Capital:
    • Includes equity and noncumulative perpetual preferred stock.
    • Goodwill is subtracted from equity.
    • Must constitute at least 50% of required capital (i.e., 4% of risk-weighted assets).
    • Must include 2% of risk-weighted assets as common equity.
  • Tier 2 Capital:
    • Also known as Supplementary Capital.
    • Includes cumulative perpetual preferred stock, certain 99-year debentures, and subordinated debt with original life over five years.
    • Provides cushion for depositors in case of insolvency.
  • Some countries and banks maintain capital ratios above the Basel Committee’s minimum requirements.

12.1.4 Netting

  • Standard in over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives markets.
  • Netting clause stating that all transactions are a single transaction in case of default.
  • Netting effectively reduces credit risk and modifies regulatory capital requirements.
  • Basel Accords: Modified in 1995 to include netting.
  • Without Netting: Loss to the bank = Sum of positive value transactions.
    • Example: Bank’s transactions worth +$24M, -$17M, +$8M. Loss = $24M + $8M = $32M.
  • With Netting: Loss to the bank = Sum of all transactions.
    • Example: Loss = $24M - $17M + $8M = $15M.
  • General Formula:
    • Without Netting: \(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \max(V_i, 0)\)
    • With Netting: \(\max(\sum_{i=1}^{N} V_i, 0)\)

12.1.5 The 1996 Amendment

  • The 1996 Amendment to the 1988 Basel Accord added capital charges for market risks in trading activities.
  • It mandated fair value accounting, also known as marking to market, for a bank’s trading book.
  • Two approaches for calculating market risk: standardized and internal model-based.
  • Value-at-risk (VaR) metrics and Specific Risk Charge (SRC) introduced.
  • Total capital combines credit and market risk, with Tier options.
  • Back-testing required; multiplier adjusted based on model performance.

12.1.6 Basel II

  • Follow-up to the 1988 Basel Accord to address its weaknesses.
  • Overall Goal: More sophisticated and comprehensive regulatory framework to manage risks in banking.
  • Initial proposal in June 1999, revised in January 2001 and April 2003.
  • Final rules published in June 2004, updated in November 2005.
  • Implementation started in 2007.
  • U.S.: Applies to internationally active banks; small banks follow Basel IA.
  • EU: Applies to all banks and securities companies.

12.1.6.1 Three Pillars of Basel II

  1. Minimum Capital Requirements:
  • More risk-sensitive approach to credit risk.
  • Operational risk included.
  • Market risk remains unchanged.
  • 8% of risk-weighted assets (RWA) still the base requirement.
  1. Supervisory Review:
  • Emphasizes qualitative and quantitative risk management.
  • Regulatory flexibility but seeks global consistency.
  • Focuses on early intervention.
  1. Market Discipline:
  • Greater disclosure requirements.
  • Aims to improve risk management through transparency.

\[ \text{Total Capital} = 0.08 \times (\text{credit risk RWA} + \text{market risk RWA} + \text{operational risk RWA}) \]

12.1.7 Solvency II

  • Solvency II is the regulatory framework for insurance companies in the European Union, replacing Solvency I in 2016.
  • Unlike Solvency I, which focused solely on underwriting risks, Solvency II also considers investment and operational risks.
  • Structure resembles Basel II with three pillars:
    1. Pillar 1: Minimum Capital Requirements (MCR) and Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR) calculated. Breach triggers supervisory intervention.
    2. Pillar 2: Supervisory review process for internal capital adequacy.
    3. Pillar 3: Disclosure of risk management information to the market.
  • SCR can be calculated using the standardized approach or internal models involving Value-at-Risk (VaR) with a one-year time horizon and 99.5% confidence limit.
  • SCR includes charges for investment risk (market and credit), underwriting risk (life, non-life, health), and operational risk.
  • Capital adequacy tests include various adverse event scenarios (e.g., 32% stock market decrease, 20% real estate price decrease).
  • Internal models must pass three tests: statistical quality, calibration, and use.
  • Types of capital:
    1. Tier 1: Equity capital, retained earnings.
    2. Tier 2: Liabilities subordinated to policyholders, available in wind-down scenarios.
    3. Tier 3: Liabilities subordinated to policyholders, not meeting Tier 2 criteria.
  • Capital composition rules are specified for Tier 1, Tier 1 + Tier 2, and Tier 1 + Tier 2 + Tier 3.

12.2 Basel II.5, Basel III, and Other Post-Crisis Changes

  • Basel II’s implementation roughly coincided with the onset of the Global Financial Crisis.
  • Critics argue Basel II encouraged self-regulation, allowing banks to use their own model inputs for regulatory capital.
  • Basel II may not be solely to blame:
    • The origins of the crisis predate Basel II.
    • U.S. was late in implementing Basel II, affecting capital levels.
  • Basel II.5 was introduced post-crisis to amend market risk capital calculations.
    • Implementation date: December 31, 2011.
  • Basel III was a more comprehensive post-crisis regulatory overhaul.
    • First set of regulations: December 2010.
    • Finalized: December 2017.
    • Implementation phased over several years.
  • Other post-2008 regulations:
    • Dodd–Frank Act in the U.S., signed July 21, 2010.
    • New rules in the European Union and the United Kingdom.

12.2.1 Basel II.5

  • Implemented on December 31, 2011, it introduced changes to the calculation of market risk capital.
  • Three Main Changes:
    1. Stressed Value-at-Risk (VaR)
    2. Incremental Risk Charge (IRC)
    3. Comprehensive Risk Measure (CRM)
  • Impact: Significantly increased the capital large banks need to hold for market risk.

12.2.1.1 Details:

  • Stressed VaR:
    • Originally based on 10-day, 99% VaR.
    • Introduces a stressed VaR, based on 250-day stressed market conditions. \[ \text{Total Capital Charge} = \max(VaR_{t-1}, m_c \times VaR_{\text{avg}}) + \max(sVaR_{t-1}, m_s \times sVaR_{\text{avg}}) \]
    • \(VaR_{t-1}\) and \(sVaR_{t-1}\) are the Value-at-Risk and stressed Value-at-Risk calculated on the previous day with a 10-day time horizon and a 99% confidence level.
    • \(VaR_{\text{avg}}\) and \(sVaR_{\text{avg}}\) are the averages of Value-at-Risk and stressed Value-at-Risk calculated over the previous 60 days with the same confidence level and time horizon.
    • \(m_c\) and \(m_s\) are multiplicative factors determined by bank supervisors, with a minimum value of three.
    • Effect: At least doubles the previous capital requirement.
  • Incremental Risk Charge (IRC):
    • Addresses discrepancy between trading and banking book capital requirements.
    • Calculates a one-year 99.9% VaR, considering both credit rating changes and defaults.
    • Includes a “liquidity horizon” for each instrument.
    • Employs the “constant level of risk assumption.”
  • Comprehensive Risk Measure (CRM):
    • Focuses on correlation-dependent instruments like ABSs and CDOs.
    • Replaces the Incremental Risk Charge and specific risk charge for these instruments.
    • Includes both standardized and internal model-based approaches.

12.2.2 Basel III

  • Introduced post-2007–2009 Global Financial Crisis to overhaul Basel II.
  • First set of regulations published in December 2010, finalized in 2017.
  • Implementation delayed to January 2023.
  • Six Key Components:
    1. Capital Definition and Requirements
    2. Capital Conservation Buffer
    3. Countercyclical Buffer
    4. Leverage Ratio
    5. Liquidity Risk
    6. Counterparty Credit Risk

12.2.2.1 Capital Definition and Requirements

  • Consists of Tier 1 equity capital, Additional Tier 1 capital, and Tier 2 capital. No Tier 3 capital.
  • Each tier has different characteristics and is intended to absorb losses to varying degrees, thereby contributing to a bank’s overall financial stability.
  1. Tier 1 Equity Capital (Common Equity Tier 1 or CET1)
    • Includes share capital and retained earnings.
    • Excludes goodwill, deferred tax assets, and defined benefit pension plan deficits.
  2. Additional Tier 1 (AT1) Capital
    • Items like non-cumulative preferred stock.
    • Previously considered Tier 1 but not common equity.
  3. Tier 2 Capital
    • Includes subordinated debt with an original maturity of at least five years.
  • Capital Requirements:
    • Tier 1 equity capital: At least 4.5% of risk-weighted assets.
    • Total Tier 1 capital: At least 6% of risk-weighted assets.
    • Total capital (Tier 1 + Tier 2): At least 8% of risk-weighted assets.

12.2.2.2 Capital Conservation Buffer

  • Further CET1 capital equal to 2.5% of risk-weighted assets.

  • Dividend restrictions based on capital ratios.

  • To ensure banks accumulate additional common equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital during normal economic conditions.

  • This buffer serves as a reserve for absorbing losses during financial stress.

  • Banks must maintain a CET1 capital conservation buffer equal to 2.5% of risk-weighted assets.

  • Dividend Restrictions: If the buffer is partially or wholly used, dividend payouts are constrained until the capital is replenished.

12.2.2.3 Countercyclical Buffer

  • To counteract the cyclicality of bank earnings and protect the financial system during economic downturns.
  • The buffer can range from 0% to 2.5% of a bank’s total risk-weighted assets and must be met with Tier 1 equity capital (CET1).
  • The implementation level is determined by each country’s regulatory authorities, allowing for regional economic conditions.

12.2.2.4 Leverage Ratio

  • Purpose: To limit banks’ discretion in calculating risk-weighted assets and to provide additional information to regulators.
  • Potential Issue: Could encourage banks to hold riskier assets if this becomes the constraining ratio.
  • The leverage ratio is the ratio of Tier 1 capital to the exposure measure.
  • Basel III specifies a minimum leverage ratio of 3%.
  • Capital Measure: Total Tier 1 capital.
  • Exposure Measure: Sum of (a) on-balance-sheet exposures, (b) derivatives exposures, (c) securities financing transaction exposures, and (d) off-balance-sheet items.
  • No risk-weighting adjustments are made.
  • Some countries have proposed or implemented higher minimums.

12.2.2.5 Liquidity Risk

  • Basel III introduces the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and the Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR).
  • LCR: Focuses on a bank’s ability to survive a 30-day liquidity disruption. \[ \text{LCR} = \frac{\text{High-Quality Liquid Assets}}{\text{Net Cash Outflows in a 30-Day Period}} \]
  • NSFR: Focuses on liquidity management over one year. \[ \text{NSFR} = \frac{\text{Amount of Stable Funding}}{\text{Required Amount of Stable Funding}} \]
  • Liquidity Management: Designed to ensure that banks can survive liquidity pressures.

12.2.2.6 Counterparty Credit Risk

  • Banks calculate a Credit Value Adjustment (CVA) for each derivatives counterparty.
  • CVA measures expected loss from counterparty default.
  • Changes in CVA can be due to shifts in risk factors or credit spreads.
  • Basel III includes CVA risk in market risk capital requirements.

12.2.2.7 G-SIBs, SIFIs, and D-SIBs

  • Regulators aim to ensure that large, systemically important institutions hold sufficient capital.
  • G-SIBs: Global Systemically Important Banks
  • SIFIs: Systemically Important Financial Institutions (includes both banks and non-banks).
  • Importance is determined through scoring methodologies and other approaches like network theory.
  • G-SIBs required to hold extra Tier 1 equity capital, based on categorization.
  • Financial Stability Board publishes annual G-SIB list.
  • Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity (TLAC) rules have been set for G-SIBs.
  • Non-compliance with TLAC can limit a bank’s dividend issuance.
  • Some countries impose stricter capital requirements (e.g., Switzerland).
  • Domestic Systemically Important Banks (D-SIBs) have their own sets of rules and capital requirements.

12.2.3 Contingent Convertible Bonds (CoCos)

  • CoCos are bonds that automatically convert into equity under certain conditions, generally when a bank faces financial difficulties.
  • Offer higher interest rates than regular debt.
  • Benefits for banks: High return on equity in normal times, and an equity cushion during financial stress.
  • Benefits for regulators: Reduces the need for public bailouts.
  • Specifying the trigger for conversion, often based on Tier 1 equity capital ratios.

12.2.4 Dodd–Frank Act

  • The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act was signed into U.S. law in July 2010.
  • Aimed to prevent future bailouts and protect consumers.
  • Its primary objectives are to:
    • Enhance regulation and oversight of financial markets and institutions.
    • Reduce systemic risks in the financial sector.
    • Protect consumers through the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).
    • Increase transparency in financial products and services.

12.3 Fundamental Review of the Trading Book

  • FRTB was introduced by the Basel Committee in May 2012, final rules published in January 2019, implementation slated for January 2023, but delays are expected.
  • Previous Approaches:
    • Basel I used Value at Risk (VaR) with a 99% confidence level.
    • Basel II.5 added “stressed VaR” based on historical periods of financial stress.
  • Key Features of FRTB:
  • Shifts from VaR to Expected Shortfall (ES) with a 97.5% confidence level (and stressed ES).
  • Liquidity horizons introduced, varying from 10 to 120 days, based on the risk factor considered.
  • Stricter definitions to counter regulatory arbitrage between the trading book and the banking book.
  • The Basel Committee outlines two approaches for banks: Standardized Approach and Internal Models Approach.
  • Banks approved for Internal Models Approach must also implement the Standardized Approach.
  • Standardized Approach calculates regulatory capital using delta, vega, and gamma exposures in the trading book.
  • Internal Models Approach uses stressed expected shortfall for regulatory capital calculation.
  • Calculations are performed separately for each trading desk.

12.3.1 Standardized Approach

  • Capital requirement is a sum of risk charge, default risk charge, and residual risk add-on.
  • Risk Charge:
    • Seven Risk Classes: General interest rate, foreign exchange, commodity, equity, and three credit spread risks.
    • Types of Risk Charges: Delta, Vega, and Curvature.
  • Default Risk Charge:
    • Calculated by Loss Given Default (LGD) and a default risk weight.
  • Residual Risk Add-On:
    • For risks not captured by delta/vega/curvature approach, e.g., exotic options.
  • Simplified Approach:
    • For smaller banks; excludes vega and gamma risks.

12.3.2 Trading Book vs. Banking Book

  • Trading Book: Contains financial instruments intended for trading. Subject to market risk capital.
  • Banking Book: Consists of instruments intended to be held to maturity. Subject to credit risk capital.
  • Regulatory Arbitrage: Past practices allowed banks to choose book placement based on advantageous capital requirements.
  • FRTB aims to clarify and standardize criteria for book classification.
  • ‘Intent to trade’ insufficient; must be manageable on a trading desk and pose risks to solvency.
  • Instruments are initially assigned to a specific book with limited ability to transfer later.
  • Allow for book transfers, but any capital benefits from such moves are disallowed.

12.4 Economic Capital and RAROC

  • Economic Capital: Internal estimate of the capital a financial institution requires for its risk exposure.
  • It varies from regulatory capital, which follows standard rules.
  • Acts as a “currency” for risk-taking within an institution.

12.4.1 Definition of Economic Capital

  • Definition: Amount of capital needed to absorb losses over one year at a specific confidence level, tailored to maintain credit ratings like AA or BBB.
  • Economic capital fills the gap between expected loss and unexpected loss.
  • Case Example: An AA-rated bank with expected annual losses of 1% and a one-year 99.98% VaR of 5% would require $4 in economic capital per $100 of loans.
  • Approaches to Measure Economic Capital:
    • Top-Down: Uses asset volatility to estimate risk, often using Merton’s model.
    • Bottom-Up: Aggregates loss distributions by risk types and business units.
  • Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital (RAROC):
    • Measures profitability relative to allocated economic capital.
    • Used for assessing past and forecasting future performance, also aids in capital allocation decisions.
  • Types of Risks Considered:
    • Regulatory Capital covers Credit, Market, and Operational risks.
    • Some institutions also consider Business risk, which is not mandated for regulatory capital.

12.4.2 Components of Economic Capital

  • Market Risk Economic Capital:
    • Time horizon is usually one year; confidence level can go up to 99.98%.
    • Standard deviation for one-year loss or gain derived from annualized daily metrics.
  • Credit Risk Economic Capital:
    • Banks have freedom to choose their credit correlation models.
    • Popular approaches include CreditMetrics and Credit Risk Plus.
    • Can be conditional (cycle-specific) or unconditional (cycle-neutral) based on economic conditions.
  • Operational Risk Economic Capital:
    • Regulatory procedure often adopted; some banks use more advanced models.
    • Aims to create a complete distribution of losses.
  • Business Risk Economic Capital:
    • Involves strategic and reputational risks.
    • Difficult to quantify, largely subjective.
    • Quantification can inform the impact of new strategic initiatives on total risk.
  • Characteristics of Loss Distributions for Different Risk Types
Standard Deviation Skewness Kurtosis
Market risk High Zero Low
Credit risk Moderate Moderate Moderate
Operational risk Low High High

12.4.3 Aggregating Economic Capital

  • To calculate the total economic capital for a financial institution by aggregating various types of risks such as market, credit, and operational risks.
  1. Simple Summation (Basel Committee Method)
    • Formula: \(E_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E_i\)
    • Assumes perfect correlation between risks.
    • Overestimates capital requirement by approx. 40%.
  2. Normal Distributions
    • Formula: \(\sigma_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij}}\)
    • Underestimates capital due to disregard for skewness and kurtosis.
  3. Using Copulas
    • Maps loss distributions to a standard distribution.
    • Gaussian copula as the simplest case.
  4. Hybrid Approach
    • Formula: \(E_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} E_i E_j \rho_{ij}}\)
    • Offers a balance between underestimation and overestimation of capital.

12.4.4 RAROC

  • Risk-adjusted performance measurement (RAPM) is crucial for assessing business units.
  • RAROC (Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital) is a widely-used approach.
  • Compares expected return with economic capital, adjusted for risk.

\[ \text{RAROC} = \frac{\text{Revenues} - \text{Costs} - \text{Expected losses}}{\text{Economic capital}} \]

  • Numerator can be pre-tax or post-tax.
  • An alternative includes adding a risk-free rate of return to the numerator.
  • Ex-ante calculations: For business expansion/contraction decisions.
  • Ex-post calculations: For performance evaluation and bonuses.
  • Decisions should not solely be based on ex-post results; consider long-term expectations.

12.5 Practice Questions and Problems

12.5.1 Basel I, Basel II, and Solvency II

  1. “When a steel company goes bankrupt, other companies in the same industry benefit because they have one less competitor. But when a bank goes bankrupt other banks do not necessarily benefit.” Explain this statement.
  2. “The existence of deposit insurance makes it particularly important for there to be regulations on the amount of capital banks hold.” Explain this statement.
  3. An interest rate swap involves the exchange of a fixed rate of interest for a floating rate of interest with both being applied to the same principal. The principals are not exchanged. What is the nature of the credit risk for a bank when it enters into a five-year interest rate swap with a notional principal of $100 million? Assume the swap is worth zero initially.
  4. In a currency swap, interest on a principal in one currency is exchanged for interest on a principal in another currency. The principals in the two currencies are exchanged at the end of the life of the swap. Why is the credit risk on a currency swap greater than that on an interest rate swap?
  5. A four-year interest rate swap currently has a negative value to a financial institution. Is the financial institution exposed to credit risk on the transaction? Explain your answer.
  6. All the derivatives transactions a bank has with a corporate client have a positive value to the bank. What is the value to the bank of netting provisions in its master agreement with the client?
  7. What is the difference between the trading book and the banking book for a bank? A bank currently has a loan of $10 million to a corporate client. At the end of the life of the loan, the client would like to sell debt securities to the bank instead of borrowing. How does this potentially affect the nature of the bank’s regulatory capital calculations?
  8. Under Basel I, banks do not like lending to highly creditworthy companies and prefer to help them issue debt securities. Why is this? Do you think this changed as a result of Basel II?
  9. Banks sometimes use what is referred to as regulatory arbitrage to reduce their capital. What do you think this means?

12.5.2 Basel II.5, Basel III, and Oter Post-Crisis Changes

  1. What are the three major components of Basel II.5?
  2. What are the six major components of Basel III?
  3. What is the difference between VaR as it has been traditionally measured and stressed VaR?
  4. Explain how the incremental risk charge is calculated. Why was it introduced by the Basel Committee?
  5. Explain how CoCo bonds work. Why are they attractive to (a) banks and (b) regulators?

12.5.3 Fundamental Review of the Trading Book

  1. Outline the differences between the way market risk capital is calculated in (a) Basel I, (b) Basel II.5, and (c) the FRTB.
  2. What are the advantages of expected shortfall over value at risk?
  3. What is the difference between the trading book and the banking book? Why are regulators concerned about specifying whether an instrument should be one or the other in the FRTB?

12.5.4 Economic Capital and RAROC

  1. What is the difference between economic capital and regulatory capital?
  2. What determines the confidence level used by a AA-rated financial institution in its economic capital calculations?
  3. What types of risk are included in business risk?
  4. A bank is considering expanding its asset management operations. The main risk is operational risk. It estimates the expected operational risk loss from the new venture in one year to be $2 million and the 99.97% VaR (arising from a small chance of an large investor lawsuit) to be $40 million. The expected fees it will receive from investors for the funds under administration are $12 million per year, and administrative costs are expected to be $3 million per year. Estimate the before-tax RAROC.
  5. RAROC can be used in two different ways. What are they?
