12.1 Basel I, Basel II, and Solvency II

The Basel Accord of 1988 marked the beginning of standardized international bank regulation. Subsequent frameworks, such as Basel II and Solvency II, have built on earlier rules, refining approaches to address evolving financial challenges. Understanding the historical evolution of these regulations is key to comprehending the current landscape. This chapter examines Basel I, netting provisions, the 1996 Amendment, and the transformational changes introduced by Basel II and Solvency II. Basel II, introduced in 2007, redefined the regulatory framework for banks, while Solvency II, implemented in the EU in 2016, extended similar principles to insurance companies.

12.1.1 Why Regulate Banks?

Bank regulation ensures that financial institutions maintain adequate capital to manage risks, safeguarding economic stability. Governments regulate banks to reduce the likelihood of default, as self-regulation has historically failed to maintain sufficient risk discipline. While deposit insurance protects individual depositors, it introduces moral hazard by potentially encouraging excessive risk-taking. Systemic risk—where the failure of one large institution can destabilize the entire financial system—is a critical concern. Governments must balance preventing systemic collapses with avoiding the unintended consequences of bailouts. The 2008 financial crisis highlighted this tension, with actions ranging from Lehman Brothers’ collapse to significant bailouts to stabilize markets.

12.1.2 Bank Regulation Before 1988

Before 1988, bank regulation primarily relied on country-specific minimum capital-to-asset ratios, leading to inconsistencies across nations. These variations created competitive imbalances and failed to address the complexity of international loans and emerging instruments like OTC derivatives. The inadequacy of existing standards prompted the creation of the Basel Committee in 1974, tasked with harmonizing regulations. The 1988 Basel Accord, later known as Basel I, was the Committee’s first comprehensive regulatory framework.

12.1.3 The 1988 BIS Accord

The 1988 BIS Accord introduced the first international risk-based standards for capital adequacy, signed by all 12 Basel Committee members. It emphasized improving banks’ risk measurement and management capabilities, laying the foundation for modern practices. Despite its significance, the Accord faced criticism for oversimplification, particularly in its treatment of risk categories.

  • Cooke Ratio: Central to the Accord, focusing on total risk-weighted assets to measure credit risk exposure.
  • Categories of Credit Risk:
    • On-Balance Sheet Assets: Excludes derivatives, e.g., loans and bonds.
    • Off-Balance Sheet Items: Includes instruments like guarantees and loan commitments, excluding derivatives.
    • Over-the-Counter Derivatives: Covers swaps, forwards, and similar instruments.

Key Equations

  1. Risk-Weighted Assets (On-Balance):
    \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i L_i \]

    • \(L_i\): Principal amount
    • \(w_i\): Risk weight

  2. Credit Equivalent Amount (Off-Balance):
    Conversion factors applied to the principal amounts of items like bankers’ acceptances and guarantees.

  3. Credit Equivalent Amount (Derivatives):
    \[ \text{max}(V, 0) + aL \]

    • \(V\): Current value of the derivative
    • \(a\): Add-on factor
    • \(L\): Principal amount

  4. Total Risk-Weighted Assets:
    \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i L_i + \sum_{j=1}^{M} w^*_j C_j \]

    • \(C_j\): Credit equivalent amount for off-balance sheet items
    • \(w^*_j\): Risk weight of counterparties

Capital Requirements

  • Minimum Capital Requirement: Banks must maintain capital equal to at least 8% of total risk-weighted assets.
  • Tier 1 Capital (Core Capital):
    • Comprises equity and noncumulative perpetual preferred stock (minus goodwill).
    • At least 50% of required capital (4% of risk-weighted assets), including 2% as common equity.
  • Tier 2 Capital (Supplementary Capital):
    • Includes cumulative perpetual preferred stock, long-term subordinated debt, and 99-year debentures.
    • Serves as a buffer for depositors during insolvency.

12.1.4 Netting

Netting is a critical risk management tool in OTC derivatives markets, allowing multiple transactions to be treated as a single agreement in case of counterparty default. It reduces credit risk and impacts regulatory capital requirements. The Basel Accords were updated in 1995 to incorporate netting.

  • Without Netting: Credit exposure equals the sum of positive-value transactions.
    • \(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \max(V_i, 0)\)
    • Example: Transactions of +$24M, -$17M, +$8M → Loss = $24M + $8M = $32M.
  • With Netting: Credit exposure equals the net value of all transactions.
    • \(\max(\sum_{i=1}^{N} V_i, 0)\)
    • Example: Loss = ($24M - $17M + $8M) = $15M.

12.1.5 The 1996 Amendment

The 1996 Amendment to the Basel Accord introduced capital charges for market risks in trading activities, requiring fair value (mark-to-market) accounting for the trading book. It introduced two approaches to measure market risk:

  1. Standardized Approach: Predefined regulatory calculations.
  2. Internal Model-Based Approach: Bank-specific Value-at-Risk (VaR) models, validated through back-testing.

Additional features include:

  • Specific Risk Charge (SRC): Captures issuer- and security-specific risks.
  • Total Capital Requirements: Combined credit and market risks.
  • Performance-based multipliers applied to VaR models depending on back-testing results.

12.1.6 Basel II

Basel II addressed the shortcomings of Basel I with a more comprehensive and risk-sensitive framework. The framework evolved through several iterations (1999–2005) and was implemented in 2007.

  • Applies to all banks and securities firms in the EU; in the U.S., it applies to internationally active banks.
  • Introduced the Three Pillars framework:
  1. Pillar 1: Minimum Capital Requirements
    • Improved sensitivity to credit risk and included operational risk.
    • 8% minimum capital requirement for risk-weighted assets (RWA).
    • Formula:
      \[ \text{Total Capital} = 0.08 \times (\text{credit risk RWA} + \text{market risk RWA} + \text{operational risk RWA}) \]
  2. Pillar 2: Supervisory Review
    • Focus on internal risk management, regulatory oversight, and early intervention.
    • Encourages both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
  3. Pillar 3: Market Discipline
    • Enhanced disclosure requirements to promote transparency and accountability in risk management.

12.1.7 Solvency II

Solvency II is the EU’s regulatory framework for insurance companies, introduced in 2016 to replace Solvency I. It accounts for underwriting, investment, and operational risks using a structure similar to Basel II.

  1. Pillar 1: Capital Requirements
    • Minimum Capital Requirement (MCR): Breach triggers supervisory intervention.
    • Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR): Calculated using a standardized approach or internal models (VaR at 99.5% confidence over one year).
    • SCR covers investment, underwriting, and operational risks.
  2. Pillar 2: Supervisory Review
    • Ensures internal capital adequacy through robust oversight.
  3. Pillar 3: Market Discipline
    • Requires detailed disclosures on risk management practices.
  • Capital Composition Rules:
    • Tier 1: Equity capital and retained earnings.
    • Tier 2: Subordinated liabilities available in wind-down scenarios.
    • Tier 3: Subordinated liabilities not qualifying as Tier 2.
  • Capital adequacy tests include stress scenarios (e.g., 32% stock market drop, 20% real estate price decline).
  • Internal models must meet statistical quality, calibration, and usability standards.

12.2 Basel II.5, Basel III, and Other Post-Crisis Changes

The implementation of Basel II coincided with the Global Financial Crisis, leading to criticisms that it promoted self-regulation by allowing banks to use internal models for regulatory capital. However, Basel II is not solely to blame, as the crisis origins predate its adoption, and delayed implementation in the U.S. affected capital levels. In response to the crisis, Basel II.5 and Basel III introduced significant reforms, alongside other post-2008 regulatory changes such as the Dodd–Frank Act in the U.S. and new EU and UK regulations.

12.2.1 Basel II.5: Enhancements to Market Risk Capital

Introduced on December 31, 2011, Basel II.5 aimed to address weaknesses in market risk capital requirements. It made three major adjustments:

  1. Stressed Value-at-Risk (sVaR)
    • Supplements the original 10-day, 99% VaR by incorporating stressed market conditions (250-day period).
      \[ \text{Total Capital Charge} = \max(VaR_{t-1}, m_c \cdot VaR_{\text{avg}}) + \max(sVaR_{t-1}, m_s \cdot sVaR_{\text{avg}}) \]
    • \(VaR_{t-1}\), \(sVaR_{t-1}\): Previous day’s Value-at-Risk and stressed Value-at-Risk (10-day, 99% confidence).
    • \(VaR_{\text{avg}}\), \(sVaR_{\text{avg}}\): Averages over the past 60 days.
    • \(m_c\), \(m_s\): Multipliers set by supervisors (minimum value = 3).
    • Impact: At least doubles market risk capital requirements for most large banks.
  2. Incremental Risk Charge (IRC)
    • Bridges the gap between trading and banking book capital requirements.
    • Measures a one-year, 99.9% VaR, accounting for credit rating changes and defaults.
    • Includes a “liquidity horizon” to reflect instrument-specific liquidity risks.
    • Assumes a “constant level of risk” over the horizon.
  3. Comprehensive Risk Measure (CRM)
    • Targets correlation-dependent instruments like asset-backed securities (ABSs) and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs).
    • Replaces the Incremental Risk Charge and specific risk charge for these instruments.
    • Allows both standardized and internal model-based approaches.

12.2.2 Basel III

Introduced after the 2007–2009 Global Financial Crisis, Basel III sought to address shortcomings in Basel II by implementing a comprehensive overhaul of banking regulations. The initial rules were published in December 2010, finalized in 2017, and implemented in phases, culminating in January 2023.

Key Components of Basel III:

  1. Capital Definition and Requirements
  2. Capital Conservation Buffer
  3. Countercyclical Buffer
  4. Leverage Ratio
  5. Liquidity Risk
  6. Counterparty Credit Risk

Capital Definition and Requirements

Basel III revised the capital framework, redefining the composition of regulatory capital to ensure greater resilience. Tier 3 capital was eliminated, and capital is now categorized into three tiers:

  1. Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1):
    • Includes share capital and retained earnings.
    • Excludes intangible assets like goodwill, deferred tax assets, and pension deficits.
    • CET1 must constitute at least 4.5% of risk-weighted assets.
  2. Additional Tier 1 (AT1) Capital:
    • Includes instruments like non-cumulative preferred stock.
    • Contributes to Tier 1 capital but is not classified as common equity.
  3. Tier 2 Capital:
    • Includes subordinated debt with a minimum original maturity of five years.

Minimum Capital Requirements:

  • Tier 1 equity (CET1): 4.5% of risk-weighted assets (RWA).
  • Total Tier 1 capital (CET1 + AT1): 6% of RWA.
  • Total capital (Tier 1 + Tier 2): 8% of RWA.

Capital Conservation Buffer

  • Banks must maintain an additional 2.5% of CET1 capital as a buffer, bringing the effective CET1 requirement to 7%.
  • The buffer ensures banks can absorb losses during periods of stress while restricting dividend payouts when the buffer is depleted.

Countercyclical Buffer

  • Designed to mitigate the procyclicality of financial systems, this buffer ranges from 0% to 2.5% of RWA and is implemented based on regional economic conditions.
  • It must be met entirely with CET1 capital.

Leverage Ratio

The leverage ratio provides a non-risk-weighted safeguard to limit excessive leverage:

\[ \text{Leverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Tier 1 Capital}}{\text{Exposure Measure}} \]

  • Exposure Measure includes on-balance-sheet exposures, derivative exposures, securities financing transactions, and off-balance-sheet items.
  • Basel III mandates a minimum leverage ratio of 3%, though some countries enforce stricter requirements.
  • The leverage ratio prevents underestimation of risk-weighted assets but may inadvertently encourage riskier asset holdings if it becomes the binding constraint.

Liquidity Risk

Basel III introduced two critical liquidity metrics:

  1. Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR):

    • Ensures banks maintain sufficient high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) to survive a 30-day liquidity stress period.

    \[ \text{LCR} = \frac{\text{High-Quality Liquid Assets}}{\text{Net Cash Outflows Over 30 Days}} \]

  2. Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR):

    • Ensures banks maintain stable funding over a one-year horizon.

    \[ \text{NSFR} = \frac{\text{Available Stable Funding}}{\text{Required Stable Funding}} \]

Counterparty Credit Risk

Basel III enhanced counterparty risk management in derivatives transactions by introducing the Credit Value Adjustment (CVA):

  • CVA accounts for potential losses from counterparty defaults and is included in market risk capital requirements.
  • It measures expected credit losses due to changes in risk factors or credit spreads.

12.2.3 G-SIBs, SIFIs, and D-SIBs

Regulators require systemically important institutions to maintain additional capital to safeguard financial stability.

  1. Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs)

    • G-SIBs are large, interconnected banks whose failure could destabilize the global financial system.
    • Designation is based on scoring methodologies, often incorporating network theory and other systemic importance metrics.
    • G-SIBs must hold extra Tier 1 equity capital, with requirements varying by category.
    • The Financial Stability Board (FSB) publishes an annual list of G-SIBs.

  2. Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFIs)

    • Includes both banks and non-bank financial institutions deemed critical to the financial system.

  3. Domestic Systemically Important Banks (D-SIBs)

    • Focuses on banks with significant importance at a national level.
    • Rules and capital requirements for D-SIBs are determined by individual countries.

Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity (TLAC) applies to G-SIBs, requiring sufficient resources to absorb losses in case of failure. Non-compliance with TLAC requirements can limit dividend payouts. Some jurisdictions, such as Switzerland, impose stricter capital standards.

12.2.4 Contingent Convertible Bonds (CoCos)

CoCos are debt instruments that convert into equity under predefined conditions, providing a capital cushion during financial stress.

  • Higher interest rates compared to traditional debt.

  • Trigger events often linked to Tier 1 equity capital ratios or other financial metrics.

  • Benefits:

    • For banks: Enhances equity during stress while offering higher returns during normal times.
    • For regulators: Reduces reliance on public bailouts by automatically recapitalizing banks.

12.2.5 Dodd–Frank Act

Signed into U.S. law in July 2010, the Dodd-Frank Act aimed to strengthen financial regulation and reduce systemic risks in the wake of the Global Financial Crisis.

  1. Enhanced Oversight: Improves regulation of financial institutions and markets.
  2. Systemic Risk Reduction: Addresses vulnerabilities to prevent future crises.
  3. Consumer Protection: Establishes the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) to safeguard consumers from abusive financial practices.
  4. Transparency: Increases clarity in financial products and services to protect investors and consumers.

12.3 Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB)

The Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB) was introduced by the Basel Committee in May 2012, with final rules published in January 2019. Although initially slated for implementation in January 2023, further delays are anticipated.

  1. Basel I: Introduced Value at Risk (VaR) at a 99% confidence level for market risk.
  2. Basel II.5: Added “stressed VaR,” accounting for market conditions during financial stress.
  3. FRTB: Replaces VaR with Expected Shortfall (ES) at a 97.5% confidence level to better capture tail risk.

12.3.1 Key Features of FRTB

  • Expected Shortfall (ES): A stressed ES model replaces VaR to account for extreme market events.
  • Liquidity Horizons: Varying horizons (10 to 120 days) are applied depending on the risk factor.
  • Stricter Book Definitions: Prevents regulatory arbitrage between the trading book and banking book.
  • Dual Approaches: Banks may use either the Standardized Approach or Internal Models Approach, though approval for the latter requires parallel implementation of the Standardized Approach.
  • Trading Desk-Level Calculations: Regulatory capital is computed separately for each trading desk to enhance granularity.

12.3.2 Standardized Approach

The Standardized Approach aggregates risk into the following components:

  1. Risk Charge: Covers seven risk classes: general interest rate, foreign exchange, commodities, equities, and three credit spread risks. Includes Delta, Vega, and Curvature risk charges.

  2. Default Risk Charge (DRC): Calculated using Loss Given Default (LGD) and a default risk weight.

  3. Residual Risk Add-On (RRAO): Captures risks excluded from standard measures, such as exotic options.

  4. Simplified Approach: A streamlined alternative for smaller banks, excluding Vega and Curvature risks.

12.3.3 Internal Models Approach

  • Uses Stressed Expected Shortfall (ES) to calculate regulatory capital.
  • Approved banks must calculate capital separately for each trading desk.
  • Requires adherence to stringent regulatory and validation standards.

12.3.4 Trading Book vs. Banking Book

FRTB introduces stricter criteria to differentiate between the trading book and the banking book:

  1. Trading Book: Includes instruments actively managed for short-term trading. Subject to market risk capital requirements.

  2. Banking Book: Contains instruments intended to be held to maturity. Subject to credit risk capital requirements.

  3. Addressing Regulatory Arbitrage: Banks previously exploited flexible book classification to minimize capital requirements.

  • Clear intent and evidence of active management for trading book classification.
  • Initial book assignment with restricted transfer options.
  • Any capital benefit from transfers is disallowed.

12.4 Economic Capital and RAROC

Economic capital represents an internal estimate of the capital a financial institution needs to absorb potential losses from its risk exposures. Unlike regulatory capital, which follows standardized rules, economic capital is tailored to an institution’s risk profile and serves as the “currency” for internal risk-taking decisions.

12.4.1 Definition of Economic Capital

Economic capital is the amount required to absorb unexpected losses over a one-year horizon at a chosen confidence level, aligned with maintaining a target credit rating (e.g., AA or BBB).

  • Concept: Fills the gap between expected loss and unexpected loss.
  • Example: An AA-rated bank with expected annual losses of 1% and a 99.98% one-year VaR of 5% requires $4 in economic capital per $100 of loans.
  • Measurement Approaches:
    1. Top-Down: Relies on asset volatility, often using Merton’s structural model.
    2. Bottom-Up: Aggregates loss distributions by risk type and business unit.

12.4.2 Components of Economic Capital

  1. Market Risk:
    • Assessed over a one-year horizon, with confidence levels up to 99.98%.
    • Derived from the annualized standard deviation of daily metrics.
  2. Credit Risk:
    • Models such as CreditMetrics and Credit Risk+ are commonly used.
    • Approaches can be conditional (cycle-specific) or unconditional (cycle-neutral).
  3. Operational Risk:
    • Typically follows regulatory procedures, though some institutions use advanced models.
    • Focuses on modeling a complete loss distribution.
  4. Business Risk:
    • Covers strategic and reputational risks, which are subjective and challenging to quantify.
    • Helps evaluate the impact of strategic initiatives on total risk.

Characteristics of loss distributions for risk types:

Risk Type Standard Deviation Skewness Kurtosis
Market Risk High Zero Low
Credit Risk Moderate Moderate Moderate
Operational Risk Low High High

12.4.3 Aggregating Economic Capital

To calculate total economic capital, institutions aggregate risks using various methods:

  1. Simple Summation:
    • Formula: \(E_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E_i\)
    • Assumes perfect correlation between risks, leading to overestimation (~40%).
  2. Normal Distributions:
    \[ \sigma_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij}} \]
    • Does not account for skewness or kurtosis, underestimating capital requirements.
  3. Copulas:
    • Uses copulas to map loss distributions to a standard distribution.
    • The Gaussian copula is the simplest approach.
  4. Hybrid Approach:
    \[ E_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} E_i E_j \rho_{ij}} \]
    • Balances overestimation and underestimation.

12.4.4 RAROC (Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital)

RAROC measures risk-adjusted profitability, aiding in capital allocation, performance evaluation, and decision-making.

\[ \text{RAROC} = \frac{\text{Revenues} - \text{Costs} - \text{Expected Losses}}{\text{Economic Capital}} \]

  • Numerator: Can be pre-tax or post-tax; adjustments may include adding a risk-free rate of return.
  • Applications:
    • Ex-Ante Calculations: Used for expansion or contraction decisions.
    • Ex-Post Calculations: Evaluates past performance, often linked to compensation and bonuses.
  • Long-term expectations should supplement ex-post results to ensure sound decision-making.

12.5 Practice Questions and Problems

12.5.1 Basel I, Basel II, and Solvency II

  1. “When a steel company goes bankrupt, other companies in the same industry benefit because they have one less competitor. But when a bank goes bankrupt other banks do not necessarily benefit.” Explain this statement.

  2. “The existence of deposit insurance makes it particularly important for there to be regulations on the amount of capital banks hold.” Explain this statement.

  3. An interest rate swap involves the exchange of a fixed rate of interest for a floating rate of interest with both being applied to the same principal. The principals are not exchanged. What is the nature of the credit risk for a bank when it enters into a five-year interest rate swap with a notional principal of $100 million? Assume the swap is worth zero initially.

  4. In a currency swap, interest on a principal in one currency is exchanged for interest on a principal in another currency. The principals in the two currencies are exchanged at the end of the life of the swap. Why is the credit risk on a currency swap greater than that on an interest rate swap?

  5. A four-year interest rate swap currently has a negative value to a financial institution. Is the financial institution exposed to credit risk on the transaction? Explain your answer.

  6. All the derivatives transactions a bank has with a corporate client have a positive value to the bank. What is the value to the bank of netting provisions in its master agreement with the client?

  7. What is the difference between the trading book and the banking book for a bank? A bank currently has a loan of $10 million to a corporate client. At the end of the life of the loan, the client would like to sell debt securities to the bank instead of borrowing. How does this potentially affect the nature of the bank’s regulatory capital calculations?

  8. Under Basel I, banks do not like lending to highly creditworthy companies and prefer to help them issue debt securities. Why is this? Do you think this changed as a result of Basel II?

  9. Banks sometimes use what is referred to as regulatory arbitrage to reduce their capital. What do you think this means?
