9.1 Estimating Default Probabilities

9.1.1 Credit Ratings

Credit Rating Agencies (e.g., Moody’s, S&P, Fitch)

Credit rating agencies assign ratings to corporate bonds, providing an assessment of the issuer’s creditworthiness. These ratings are typically stable and change infrequently, as agencies aim to avoid frequent reversals. They often use a “through-the-cycle” approach, focusing on long-term credit stability and disregarding short-term economic fluctuations.

Market-Based Providers (e.g., Moody’s KMV, Kamakura)

Unlike traditional rating agencies, these companies estimate default probabilities using market data, such as equity prices and other financial variables. Their models are more dynamic and responsive to real-time market changes, prioritizing timely insights over stability.

Internal Credit Ratings in Banks

Banks use internal credit rating systems to evaluate the credit risk of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) that lack publicly traded debt. These ratings incorporate financial ratios, such as:

  • Profitability ratios: e.g., Return on Assets (ROA).
  • Leverage ratios: e.g., Debt-to-Equity Ratio.

Additionally, banks analyze projected cash flow statements to assess a firm’s ability to meet its debt obligations.

Altman’s Z-Score

The Z-Score is a statistical model developed to predict corporate default risk using a combination of accounting ratios. The formula is:

\[ Z = 1.2 X_1 + 1.4 X_2 + 3.3 X_3 + 0.6 X_4 + 0.999 X_5 \]

Where:

  • \(X_1\): Working capital / Total assets
  • \(X_2\): Retained earnings / Total assets
  • \(X_3\): Earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) / Total assets
  • \(X_4\): Market value of equity / Book value of total liabilities
  • \(X_5\): Sales / Total assets

Interpretation of Z-Scores:

  • \(Z > 3.0\): Low probability of default.
  • \(2.7 < Z < 3.0\): Company is on alert.
  • \(1.8 < Z < 2.7\): Moderate risk of default.
  • \(Z < 1.8\): High likelihood of financial distress.

This framework is widely used for early warning signals and credit risk assessment in non-financial firms.

  • A bond initially rated BBB has a 0.16% chance of defaulting in the first year, 0.43% in the second year, etc.
  • Yearly Probability Calculation: The probability of defaulting in a specific year can be calculated by subtraction. E.g., for the second year, \(0.43\% - 0.16\% = 0.27\%\).

Unconditional Default Probability

Definition

Unconditional Default Probabilities: Reflect the likelihood of default during a specific year as measured from the start of the observation period.

The unconditional default probability is the increase in cumulative probability during a specific year.

Example

Unconditional probability of default for CCC/C bonds during third year: \[ 43.42\% - 38.33\% = 5.09\%. \]

Conditional Default Probability

Definition

Conditional Default Probabilities: Indicate the probability of default in a given year, assuming no prior default.

The conditional probability adjusts for survival until a specific time, offering more context for ongoing risk management.

Steps to Calculate:

  1. Calculate the probability of surviving until the end of the previous year.
  2. Divide the yearly default probability by the survival probability.
Example

Conditional probability of default for CCC/C bonds during third year: \[ 100\% - 38.33\% = 61.67\% \] \[ \frac{5.09\%}{61.67\%} = 8.25\%. \]

Applications

  • Unconditional Probabilities: Useful for static, long-term risk assessments.
  • Conditional Probabilities: Crucial for dynamic risk management as they reflect updated information and ongoing contexts.

By analyzing these probabilities, financial practitioners can better understand and manage the risk profiles of bonds, making informed investment and credit decisions.

Hazard Rates

Definition

The hazard rate (\(\lambda(t)\)) represents the intensity of default over a short time period, conditional on the entity’s survival until that time. It bridges the concepts of conditional and unconditional probabilities, offering a dynamic measure of default risk.

  • The hazard rate measures the likelihood of default in a short time interval, given no prior default.
  • It incorporates survival probabilities to provide a nuanced understanding of credit risk over time.
  • \(V(t)\) is the cumulative probability of the company surviving to time \(t\).

Conditional Probability of Default:
\[ \frac{V(t) - V(t + \Delta t)}{V(t)} = \lambda(t) \Delta t \] Cumulative Survival Probability:
\[ V(t) = e^{-\int_{0}^{t} \lambda(\tau)d\tau} \] Cumulative Default Probability:
\[ Q(t) = 1 - e^{-\int_{0}^{t} \lambda(\tau)d\tau} = 1 - e^{-\lambda(t) t} \]

Example Calculations with Hazard Rates

Assume a constant hazard rate of \(1.5\%\) per year. The default probabilities for each year are computed as follows:

\[ Q(1) = 1 - e^{-0.015 \times 1} = 0.0149 \] \[ Q(2) = 1 - e^{-0.015 \times 2} = 0.0296 \] \[ Q(3) = 1 - e^{-0.015 \times 3} = 0.0440 \] \[ Q(4) = 1 - e^{-0.015 \times 4} = 0.0582 \] \[ Q(5) = 1 - e^{-0.015 \times 5} = 0.0723 \]

  • Unconditional Default Probability: The probability of default in the 4th year is the difference between cumulative probabilities of the 4th and 3rd years:
    \[ Q(4) - Q(3) = 0.0582 - 0.0440 = 0.0142 \]

  • Conditional Default Probability: The probability of default in the 4th year, assuming survival until the end of the 3rd year:
    \[ \frac{0.0142}{1 - Q(3)} = \frac{0.0142}{1 - 0.0440} = 0.0149 \]

  • Hazard rates are essential for modeling time-dependent default risk in credit risk management.

  • They provide a more granular and dynamic perspective compared to static probabilities, aiding in real-time risk assessments and pricing of credit derivatives.

9.1.3 Recovery Rates

Definition

The recovery rate of a bond is the percentage of the principal amount recovered after default, typically based on the bond’s trading price about one month post-default. This metric provides critical insights into creditor losses and varies based on the bond’s seniority and prevailing economic conditions.

  • The recovery rate represents the value creditors reclaim through partial payments or asset liquidation.
  • Recovery rates differ significantly based on bond category:
    • First Lien Bonds: 54.6% recovery.
    • Junior Subordinated Bonds: 22.3% recovery.
  • Recovery rates are inversely related to default rates:
    • Good economic conditions: Higher recovery rates.
    • Poor economic conditions: Lower recovery rates.
  • In challenging economic periods, the combination of high default rates and low recovery rates exacerbates creditor losses.
  • Understanding recovery rate trends is crucial for credit risk assessment, pricing of distressed debt, and strategic risk management.

9.1.4 Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

Definition

A Credit Default Swap (CDS) is a financial contract that acts as a form of insurance against the risk of credit default.

  • It involves two parties: the protection buyer, who pays periodic premiums (typically quarterly), and the protection seller, who compensates the buyer if a specified credit event, such as a default, occurs.
  • CDS contracts can be linked to corporate bonds, government bonds, or other credit instruments, providing the buyer with protection against the risk of the issuer defaulting on the underlying instrument.

The pricing of a CDS is determined by several factors, including the creditworthiness of the reference entity, current market conditions, and the maturity of the swap. CDS contracts play a crucial role in price discovery for credit risk and significantly influence overall market liquidity and stability.

CDS Spread

  • The CDS spread is the annual premium, usually expressed in basis points (bps), that the protection buyer pays to the protection seller over the duration of the contract.
  • The spread is calculated as a percentage of the notional amount of the underlying credit instrument.
  • A wider CDS spread typically indicates a higher perceived risk of default for the underlying entity, whereas a narrower spread suggests lower perceived risk.

For example, if the CDS spread for a particular corporate bond is 200 bps, the buyer pays the seller 2% of the notional amount annually for the protection. The CDS spread serves as a valuable indicator of credit risk and market sentiment concerning the referenced entity.

Credit Indices

  • Credit indices are benchmarks that track the performance of a basket of credit instruments, such as bonds or credit default swaps.
  • These indices often include a diversified portfolio of credit instruments representing various sectors, credit ratings, or geographic regions.
  • Credit indices are used to gauge overall market health, providing insights into credit risk, liquidity, and investor sentiment.

Popular credit indices include the Markit iTraxx in Europe and the CDX in North America, which track CDS spreads across different categories of issuers. These indices enhance liquidity in the credit market by creating standardized products that can be traded more easily.

9.1.5 Credit Spreads

Definition

The credit spread is the additional rate of interest per annum that investors require for bearing a particular credit risk.

  • Credit spreads can be measured using CDS spreads or bond yield spreads (calculated as the difference between a bond’s yield and the yield on a risk-free bond).
  • A CDS can effectively hedge a corporate bond, essentially converting it into a risk-free bond.
  • The N-year CDS spread should approximate the yield difference between an N-year corporate bond and an N-year risk-free bond. However, liquidity constraints can limit the effectiveness of arbitrage opportunities between CDS and bonds.
  • CDS contracts provide a direct estimate of the credit spread, while risk-free rates are generally derived from Treasury bonds.
  • Notably, implied risk-free rates are often found to be higher than Treasury rates.
  • Asset swaps also provide direct estimates of excess bond yields over a floating reference rate.

CDS–Bond Basis

The CDS–Bond Basis is the difference between the CDS spread and the bond yield spread:

\[ \text{CDS-Bond Basis} = \text{CDS Spread} - \text{Bond Yield Spread} \]

Various factors, such as counterparty default risk and bond price fluctuations, can cause the CDS–Bond Basis to deviate from zero. Historically, the CDS–Bond Basis was positive before the 2007 financial crisis, but it has since tended to be small and negative.

9.1.6 Estimating Default Probabilities from Credit Spreads

Estimating default probabilities from credit spreads involves using market indicators such as CDS spreads, bond yield spreads, or asset swap spreads to infer the likelihood of default.

Approximate Calculation

This method provides a straightforward way to estimate average default probabilities when the bond price is near par value.

Scenario: Suppose a 5-year credit spread is 240 basis points (or 2.4%), and the recovery rate is 40%.

Formula: The average hazard rate (\(\overline{\lambda}\)) over the time horizon \(T\) is given by: \[ \overline{\lambda} = \frac{s(T)}{1 - R} \]

Where:

  • \(s(T)\): Credit spread for maturity \(T\),
  • \(R\): Recovery rate,
  • \(\overline{\lambda}\): Average hazard rate.

Using the given parameters: \[ \overline{\lambda} = \frac{0.024}{1 - 0.4} = 4\% \]

This method is effective for credit default swaps (CDS), bond yield spreads, and asset swap spreads. It assumes that the bond price is close to its par value.

9.1.7 Comparison of Default Probability Estimates

When estimating default probabilities, it is essential to distinguish between probabilities derived from historical data (real-world) and those inferred from credit spreads (risk-neutral).

  • Real-World Probabilities: Derived from historical default rates, representing actual default frequencies observed in the past.
  • Risk-Neutral Probabilities: Derived from credit spreads and market prices, reflecting the probability of default adjusted for investor risk preferences and market expectations.

Hazard rates estimated from credit spreads are generally higher than those derived from historical data. This disparity grows more pronounced as credit quality declines, and several factors contribute to these differences:

  1. Illiquidity of Corporate Bonds: Corporate bonds often trade in less liquid markets, leading to higher spreads.
  2. Systematic Risk: Defaults are often correlated, increasing perceived risk in credit spreads.
  3. Non-Systematic Risk: Corporate bonds carry firm-specific risks that are harder to diversify compared to equity portfolios.
  • Use risk-neutral default probabilities for:
    • Valuing credit derivatives: Required for pricing instruments like CDS or CDOs.
    • Estimating the impact of default risk: Particularly when using market-consistent measures.
  • Use real-world default probabilities for:
    • Scenario analyses: Assessing potential future losses under various economic conditions.

The Merton (1974) model provides a theoretical foundation for calculating default probabilities. It treats a company’s equity as a call option on its assets, with the strike price equivalent to the debt repayment amount. This approach links default risk to asset value volatility, offering a structural method for default probability estimation.

9.2 xVAs

Over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives are cleared through either Central Clearing Parties (CCPs) or bilateral agreements. Regulatory frameworks now require most standardized derivatives to be cleared via CCPs, significantly reducing credit risk. However, bilaterally cleared transactions necessitate additional collateral to manage credit risk. Despite these safeguards, certain OTC transactions, such as those with non-financial end-users, remain outside the scope of these regulations.

Assessing credit risk in derivatives is more complex than in traditional loans due to the uncertainty of future exposure and the potentially unlimited loss. To manage this complexity, various adjustments, collectively known as xVAs, are used. These include:

  • Credit Value Adjustment (CVA): Quantifies the counterparty credit risk.
  • Debit Value Adjustment (DVA): Reflects the counterparty’s perspective of the dealer’s credit risk.
  • Funding Value Adjustment (FVA), Margin Value Adjustment (MVA), and Capital Value Adjustment (KVA): Capture other costs and risks in derivative transactions.

This discussion focuses on CVA, the primary metric for quantifying a dealer’s credit risk in bilaterally cleared transactions.

9.2.1 Credit Exposure on Derivatives

Credit exposure in derivatives arises from market value fluctuations, unlike loans with fixed exposures. Future exposure is uncertain, and losses can theoretically be unlimited. Consider a single bilaterally cleared transaction with no collateral posted:

  1. Liability for the dealer (e.g., short option): The dealer has no credit exposure.
  2. Asset for the dealer (e.g., long option): The dealer bears the credit exposure.
  3. Variable exposure (e.g., forward or swap): Exposure fluctuates over time, depending on market conditions.

For multiple transactions between a dealer and a counterparty, exposures are often netted. The dealer’s net exposure is given by:
\[ \text{max}(V, 0) \] where \(V\) represents the net market value of all transactions. The dealer must consider both current and potential future exposure when assessing credit risk.

9.2.2 Credit Value Adjustment (CVA)

CVA measures the expected loss from a counterparty’s potential default in OTC derivative transactions. It directly impacts a dealer’s financials:

  • An increase in CVA reduces profits, as it represents higher expected credit losses.
  • CVA must be calculated for each counterparty to incorporate their specific default risk.

CVA is a crucial risk management tool, as it reflects the financial cost of counterparty risk, helping dealers to price derivatives more accurately and align with regulatory and accounting requirements.

Calculations for CVA

To calculate Credit Value Adjustment (CVA), the process involves discretizing the timeline of the longest derivative and estimating the potential losses over each interval.

  • \(T\): The life of the longest derivative.
  • \([t_{i-1}, t_i]\): Intervals dividing \([0, T]\).
  • \(q_i\): Risk-neutral probability of default during the \(i\)th interval.
  • \(v_i\): Present value of net exposure at the midpoint of the \(i\)th interval.
  • \(R\): Estimated recovery rate.
  • \(s_i\): Credit spread for a maturity of \(t_i\).

The total expected loss from counterparty default is calculated as: \[ \text{CVA} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (1 - R) q_i v_i \]

The risk-neutral default probability (\(q_i\)) for the \(i\)th interval is given by: \[ q_i = \exp \left( \frac{- s_{i-1} t_{i-1}}{1 - R} \right) - \exp \left( \frac{- s_i t_i}{1 - R} \right) \]

The exposure (\(v_i\)) is typically estimated using Monte Carlo simulations, which account for the uncertain nature of market values over time.

Collateral and Cure Periods

Collateral agreements influence the calculation of exposures. The exposure, \(E\), is defined as: \[ E = \max(V - C, 0) \]

  • \(V\): Market value of outstanding transactions.
  • \(C\): Collateral posted at the time of default.

The cure period—typically 10 days—is the time between when a counterparty stops posting collateral and the close-out of the transaction. This affects the collateral’s value and must be modeled in the Monte Carlo simulation.

Peak Exposure

  • Calculated at the midpoint of each interval and represents a high percentile (e.g., 97.5%) of potential exposures.
  • Real-world simulations should ideally be used for this calculation, but for short time periods, risk-neutral simulations yield similar results.

Downgrade Triggers

  • Downgrade triggers in ISDA agreements require increased collateral or termination of transactions when a party’s credit rating falls below a threshold.
  • Overuse of these triggers can lead to liquidity crises, as seen in cases such as AIG, Enron, Xerox, and Ambac.
  • While they provide some protection, they are limited in addressing sudden and severe credit rating declines.

9.2.3 The Impact of a New Transaction

The addition of a new transaction to an existing portfolio can impact the Credit Value Adjustment (CVA) depending on its correlation with the existing transactions:

  • Positive correlation with existing transactions increases CVA, as it raises the potential exposure to the counterparty.
  • Negative correlation reduces CVA, as the exposure to the counterparty becomes less correlated with existing positions, mitigating overall risk.

Companies may receive more favorable quotes from dealers with whom they already have existing transactions, due to netting effects and CVA considerations. However, recalculating CVA for each new transaction can be computationally intensive. Instead, stored values from previous Monte Carlo simulations can be leveraged to efficiently update CVA incrementally without starting from scratch.

9.2.4 CVA Risk and Wrong-Way Risk

CVA can be thought of as a complex derivative position, and its management requires the same hedging techniques used for other derivatives. Since a rise in CVA results in a loss for the dealer, hedging CVA becomes essential.

Computing Greek letters (such as delta, gamma, etc.) for CVA is computationally intensive, yet necessary for risk management. Regulators require that changes in credit spreads be incorporated into market risk capital calculations to reflect CVA risk.

  • Wrong-way risk occurs when the probability of default is positively correlated with exposure, increasing risk for the dealer.
  • Right-way risk occurs when the default probability is negatively correlated with exposure, reducing risk for the dealer.

Evaluating these types of risk requires an understanding of the counterparty’s business and the full scope of their other transactions. To adjust for wrong-way risk, regulators require a CVA adjustment using an alpha multiplier of at least 1.2.

9.2.5 Debit Value Adjustment (DVA)

Debit Value Adjustment (DVA) reflects the counterparty’s potential cost due to the dealer’s default. It is essentially the counterpart to CVA, representing the cost to the counterparty if the dealer defaults.

  • DVA benefits the dealer, effectively offsetting the dealer’s CVA, which aligns with the zero-sum nature of derivatives markets.
  • The book value of derivatives is calculated as: \[ f_{nd} - \text{CVA} + \text{DVA} \]

where \(f_{nd}\) is the value of the derivative assuming no defaults.

An increase in the dealer’s credit spread results in an increase in DVA, which can boost reported profits. However, because this effect could artificially inflate profits, regulators exclude DVA gains and losses from the definition of common equity in regulatory capital calculations. This exclusion aims to prevent misrepresentation of financial stability.

9.3 Credit Value at Risk (Credit VaR)

Credit VaR is a critical tool for regulatory capital determination and internal credit risk management. It measures the potential credit loss over a specified time horizon that will not be exceeded at a given confidence level.

  • Can consider only default losses or include losses from downgrades and credit spread changes.
  • Banks use Credit VaR for regulatory capital (to comply with Basel requirements) and economic capital.
  • Credit VaR typically uses a longer horizon (e.g., one year) than market risk VaR (e.g., one day).
  • Credit risk models are more elaborate than those for market risk due to the need to capture interdependent defaults and potential downgrades.

A critical variable in credit risk modeling is credit correlation, which accounts for the interdependence of defaults among companies in a portfolio. During economic downturns, increased credit correlation magnifies the risk for institutions, emphasizing its importance in stress testing and capital adequacy assessments.

9.3.2 Methods for Calculating Credit VaR

  1. Vasicek’s Model:
    • Based on a one-factor Gaussian copula model.
    • Focuses on the time to default for portfolio companies.
    • Widely used for regulatory capital calculations.
  2. Credit Risk Plus:
    • Introduced by Credit Suisse in 1997.
    • Inspired by insurance models, focusing on default probabilities and loss rates.
    • Incorporates uncertainty in default probabilities to calculate loss distributions.
  3. CreditMetrics:
    • Developed by JPMorgan in 1997.
    • Combines Gaussian copula models with ratings transition matrices.
    • Measures both default and downgrade risks, producing a more comprehensive loss estimate.
  • CreditMetrics and Credit Risk Plus, both aim to estimate long-term loss distributions.
  • Differences lie in timing of loss recognition:
    • Credit Risk Plus recognizes losses only upon default.
    • CreditMetrics considers revaluation losses for downgrades over time.
  • Example: If a loan is downgraded in Years 1 and 2 before defaulting in Year 3:
    • Credit Risk Plus calculates the loss only in Year 3.
    • CreditMetrics includes incremental revaluation losses in Years 1 and 2.

9.3.3 Credit Spread Risk

Credit spread risk reflects changes in credit spreads, which can significantly impact the value of credit-sensitive products such as bonds and derivatives.

  1. Historical Simulation:
    • Uses historical data (e.g., the last 500 days of credit spread changes).
    • Limitation: Assumes no default probability and may lack sufficient data quality.
  2. CreditMetrics:
    • Combines rating transition matrices and Monte Carlo simulations.
    • Accounts for the distribution of credit spreads over the simulation horizon.
