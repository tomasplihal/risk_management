9.1 Estimating Default Probabilities

9.1.1 Credit Ratings

  • Rating Agencies (e.g., Moody’s, S&P, Fitch):

    • Provide credit ratings for corporate bonds, indicating creditworthiness.
    • Focus on stability, so ratings change infrequently to avoid frequent reversals.
    • May “rate through the cycle,” ignoring short-term economic changes.

  • Other Companies (e.g., Moody’s KMV, Kamakura):

    • Provide estimates of default probabilities based on equity price and other variables.
    • More responsive to market information, not focusing on stability.

  • Internal Credit Ratings in Banks:

    • Used for small and medium-sized companies without publicly traded debt.
    • Utilize profitability ratios and balance sheet ratios, like return on assets and debt to equity.
    • Estimate cash flow statements to analyze a company’s ability to service its debt.

  • Altman’s Z-Score:

    • A statistical technique to predict default using accounting ratios.

    \[ Z = 1.2 X_1 + 1.4 X_2 + 3.3 X_3 + 0.6 X_4 + 0.999 X_5 \]

    • Variables:
      • \(X_1\): Working capital/Total assets
      • \(X_2\): Retained earnings/Total assets
      • \(X_3\): Earnings before interest and taxes/Total assets
      • \(X_4\): Market value of equity/Book value of total liabilities
      • \(X_5\): Sales/Total assets
    • Interpretation:
      • \(Z > 3.0\): Unlikely to default
      • \(2.7 < Z < 3.0\): On alert
      • \(1.8 < Z < 2.7\): Good chance of default
      • \(Z < 1.8\): Very high probability of financial embarrassment.

9.1.2 Historical Default Probabilities

  • Ratings agencies produce data to show default experience over time based on credit ratings.
  • For investment-grade bonds (BBB and above), default probability tends to increase over time. For poor-rated bonds, it may decrease.
  • Both unconditional and conditional default probabilities can be computed from the data.

  • Unconditional Default Probability:
    • The probability of default during a specific year as seen at the initial time (time zero).
    • The unconditional default probability of a CCC/C bond during the third year is calculated as \(43.42\% - 38.33\% = 5.09\%\).
  • Conditional Default Probability:
    • The probability that a bond will default during a specific year, given that no earlier default has occurred.
    • Calculation:
      1. Probability of surviving until the end of year two: \(100\% - 38.33\% = 61.67\%\).
      2. Conditional probability of default during the third year: \(\frac{5.09\%}{61.67\%} = 8.25\%\).
    • The conditional default probability considers the probability of survival until a specific point, then calculates the probability of default from that point onwards.
  • Conditional probabilities are often more relevant for ongoing risk management as they reflect updated information and context, whereas unconditional probabilities provide a broader, initial view of risk.

9.1.2.1 Hazard Rates

  • Hazard Rate (λ(t)) is a measure of default intensity over a short time period, conditional on no prior default.
  • The hazard rate is a useful tool for understanding the risk of default over time, taking into consideration both conditional and unconditional probabilities.
  • It accounts for the possibility of survival and provides insights into the company’s creditworthiness.
  • Formula:
    • Conditional Probability: \[ \frac{V(t) - V(t + \Delta t)}{V(t)} = \lambda(t) \Delta t\]
    • Cumulative Survival: \[V(t) = e^{-\int_{0}^{t} \lambda(\tau)d\tau}\]
    • Cumulative Default Probability: \[Q(t) = 1 - e^{-\int_{0}^{t} \lambda(\tau)d\tau} = 1 - e^{-\lambda(t) t}\]
  • Example:
    • Constant hazard rate 1.5% per year.
    • Probabilities:
      • 1st Year: \(1 - e^{-0.015 \times 1} = 0.0149\)
      • 2nd Year: \(1 - e^{-0.015 \times 2} = 0.0296\)
      • 3rd Year: 0.0440
      • 4th Year: 0.0582
      • 5th Year: 0.0723
    • Unconditional Probability in 4th Year: \(0.0582 - 0.0440 = 0.0142\)
    • Conditional Probability in 4th Year: \(\frac{0.0142}{1 - 0.0440} = 0.0149\)

9.1.3 Recovery Rates

  • Recovery rate for a bond refers to the price at which it trades approximately one month after default as a percentage of its principal amount.
  • Creditors may agree to partial payments, or assets are sold to meet claims. Some claims have priorities over others.
  • Recovery rates vary among different bond categories, ranging from 54.6% for first lien bonds to 22.3% for junior subordinated bonds.
  • The average recovery rate is negatively related to the default rate.
  • Higher recovery rates during good economic conditions, and lower during poor conditions.
  • A bad year for defaults can lead to both higher defaults and lower recovery rates, intensifying the negative impact for lenders.

9.1.4 Credit Default Swaps

  • A CDS is a financial contract that functions as a form of insurance against the risk of credit default.
  • Involves two parties - the buyer pays periodic premiums to the seller (often quarterly), and the seller agrees to compensate the buyer if a specified credit event, such as default, occurs.
  • Can be linked to corporate bonds, government bonds, or other credit instruments.
  • Provides the buyer with protection against the risk that the issuer of the underlying credit instrument might default.
  • Pricing is determined by factors such as the creditworthiness of the reference entity, market conditions, and the maturity of the swap.
  • Plays a role in price discovery for credit risk and can influence overall market liquidity and stability.

9.1.4.1 CDS Spread

  • The CDS spread is the annual amount, usually expressed in basis points (bps), that the protection buyer pays to the protection seller over the length of the contract.
  • Typically calculated as a percentage of the notional amount of the underlying credit instrument paid per year.
  • A wider spread often indicates a higher perceived risk of default for the underlying credit entity, whereas a narrower spread suggests a lower perceived risk.
  • Example: If the CDS spread for a particular corporate bond is 200 bps, the buyer would pay the seller 2% of the notional amount annually for the protection.
  • The CDS spread serves as a valuable indicator of credit risk and market sentiment for the referenced credit entity.
  • Credit indices are benchmarks that track the performance of a basket of credit instruments such as bonds or credit default swaps.
  • They include a diversified portfolio of credit instruments, often representing various sectors, credit ratings, or geographic regions.
  • Used to gauge the overall health and trends in the credit market, providing insights into credit risk, liquidity, and investor sentiment.
  • Examples: Popular credit indices include the Markit iTraxx in Europe, and the CDX in North America, tracking credit default swap spreads across various categories of issuers.
  • They often enhance liquidity in the credit market by creating standardized products that can be traded more easily.

9.1.5 Credit Spreads

  • The credit spread is the extra rate of interest per annum required by investors for bearing a particular credit risk.
  • It can be measured by CDS spreads or bond yield spreads (Bond yield - risk-free bond).
  • A CDS can hedge a corporate bond, essentially converting it into a risk-free bond.
  • N-year CDS spread should approximate the yield difference between an N-year corporate bond and an N-year risk-free bond.
  • Liquidity constraints can limit the effectiveness of arbitrage between CDS and bonds.
  • CDSs provide a direct estimate of credit spread.
  • Risk-free rates are generally derived from Treasury bonds.
  • Implied risk-free rates often found to be higher than Treasury rates.
  • Asset swaps give direct estimates of excess bond yields over a floating reference rate.

9.1.5.1 CDS–Bond Basis

  • The excess of the CDS spread over the bond yield spread.

\[ \text{CDS-Bond Basis} = \text{CDS Spread} - \text{Bond Yield Spread} \]

  • Various factors like counterparty default risk and bond prices can cause deviations from zero.
  • The CDS-bond basis was positive before the 2007 market turmoil but has since usually been small and negative.

9.1.6 Estimating Default Probabilities from Credit Spreads

  • Objective is to estimate default probabilities using credit spreads like CDS spread, bond yield spread, or asset swap spread.

9.1.6.1 Approximate Calculation

  • Scenario: 5-year credit spread is 240 basis points; recovery rate is 40%.

  • Formula: \[ \overline{\lambda} = \frac{s(T)}{1-R} \]

  • where \(s(T)\) is the credit spread for a maturity \(T\), \(R\) is the recovery rate, and \(\overline{\lambda}\) is the average hazard rate between time zero and time \(T\).

    \[ \overline{\lambda} = \frac{0.024}{1 - 0.4} = 4\% \]

  • Works well for CDS, bond yield, and asset swap spreads.

  • Assumes bond price close to par value.

9.1.6.2 A More Exact Calculation

  • Suitable for bonds with prices significantly deviating from par value.
  • General Idea: Incorporates bond price, coupon rate, and yield for more precise estimates.
  • Calculation Steps:
    • Compare prices of corporate and similar risk-free bonds.
    • Calculate expected loss from default.
    • Equate this to present value of future default losses.
    • Solve for default probability \(Q\).
  • Advantages: Higher degree of accuracy; accounts for complex market conditions.

9.1.7 Comparison of Default Probability Estimates

  • Real world (historical data) vs. risk-neutral world (credit spreads).
  • Hazard rates from credit spreads are generally higher than those from historical data.
  • Differences in rates increase as credit quality declines.
  • Multiple reasons for these differences:
    • Illiquidity of corporate bonds.
    • Systematic risk due to correlated defaults.
    • Non-systematic risk harder to diversify in bonds.
  • When valuing credit derivatives or estimating the impact of default risk on the pricing of instruments, we should use risk-neutral default probabilities.
  • When carrying out scenario analyses to calculate potential future losses from defaults, we should use real-world default probabilities.
  • Merton 1974, model for calculating default probabilities.
  • Equity of a company is a call option on the value of the assets of the company with a strike price equal to the repayment required on the debt.

9.2 xVAs

  • Over-the-counter derivatives are cleared either through Central Clearing Parties (CCPs) or bilateral agreements.
  • Regulatory changes mandate standardized derivatives to be cleared via CCPs.
  • Bilaterally cleared transactions involve more collateral to mitigate credit risk.
  • In both cases, there is very little credit risk.
  • However, there are transactions to which regulations do not apply (e.g., OTC transactions with non-financial end users).
  • Credit risk assessment for derivatives is more complicated than for loans.
  • Unlike fixed loan exposures, the future exposure in derivatives is uncertain, and the potential loss could be theoretically infinite.
  • Credit Value Adjustment (CVA) and Debit Value Adjustment (DVA) are risk management tools used in the valuation of counterparty risk.
  • xVAs: Credit Value Adjustment (CVA) and Debit Value Adjustment (DVA) are key metrics among xVAs, which also include Funding Value Adjustment (FVA), Margin Value Adjustment (MVA), and Capital Value Adjustment (KVA). These quantify different aspects of credit risk costs or benefits in derivatives.
  • The chapter concentrates on CVA as a primary tool for quantifying the dealer’s credit risk in bilaterally cleared transactions.

9.2.1 Credit Exposure on Derivatives

  • Example: one transaction, cleared bilaterally and no collateral is posted.
  • Three Credit Exposure Scenarios:
    1. The derivative is a liability (e.g., short option): No credit exposure for the dealer.
    2. The derivative is an asset (e.g., long option): Dealer has credit exposure.
    3. The derivative can be either(e.g., forward, swap): Exposure varies over time.
  • In multiple transactions between a dealer and a counterparty, exposures are netted.
  • The dealer’s net exposure is \(\text{max}(V, 0)\), where \(V\) is the net market value of all transactions.
  • In calculating its credit risk, the dealer is interested in both its credit exposure today and what that exposure might be in the future.

9.2.2 Credit Value Adjustment (CVA)

  • Credit Value Adjustment (CVA) estimates expected loss from counterparty default in OTC derivatives.
  • CVA impacts balance sheet and profits (increase CVA = decrease profits).
  • Dealer calculates it for each counterparty.

9.2.2.1 Calculations

  • Time \(T\) is the life of the longest derivative.
  • Divide \([0, T]\) into intervals \([t_{i-1}, t_i]\).
  • Variables:
    • \(q_i\): Risk-neutral probability of default during \(i\)th interval.
    • \(v_i\): Present value of net exposure at \(i\)th interval’s midpoint.
    • \(R\): Estimated recovery rate.
    • \(s_i\): An estimate of the counterparty’s credit spread for a maturity of \(t_i\).
  • CVA Equation, total expected loss from a default: \[ \text{CVA} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (1 - R) q_i v_i \]

\[ q_i = \exp \left( \frac{ - s_{i-1} t_{i-1}}{1 - R} \right) - \exp \left( \frac{- s_i t_i}{1 - R} \right) \]

  • \(v_i\) usually calculated using Monte Carlo simulation.

9.2.2.2 Collateral and Cure Periods

  • Collateral agreements are integrated into the calculation of \(v_i\).
  • Exposure, \(E\): Defined as \(E = \max(V - C, 0)\), where \(V\) is the market value of outstanding transactions and \(C\) is the collateral posted at the time of default.
  • Cure Period: Usually 10 days. Time lapse between a counterparty ceasing to post collateral and the transaction close-out. Affects the collateral value at the time of default.
  • It must be taken into account in the Monte Carlo simulation.

9.2.2.3 Peak Exposure and Downgrade Triggers

  • Peak Exposure: - Calculated at the midpoint of each interval, representing a high percentile (e.g., 97.5%) of exposures in Monte Carlo simulations.
  • It should use real-world, not risk-neutral world, simulations, but for short time periods the difference is small.
  • Downgrade Triggers: Clauses in ISDA master agreements that require more collateral or termination if one party’s credit rating falls below a specified level.
  • Downgrade triggers can lead to significant liquidity crises if a company cannot meet collateral demands, potentially resulting in bankruptcy (e.g., if it is overused).
  • They offer limited protection against drastic changes in credit rating.
  • Examples include AIG, Enron, Xerox, and Ambac.

9.2.3 The Impact of a New Transaction

  • Positive correlation with existing transactions increases CVA; negative correlation reduces it.
  • Companies may get favorable quotes from dealers with whom they already have transactions due to CVA/netting considerations.
  • Directly recalculating CVA for each new transaction is impractical due to computational intensity.
  • Stored values from previous Monte Carlo simulations can be used for efficient incremental CVA calculation.

9.2.4 CVA Risk and Wrong-Way Risk

  • CVA can be considered as a complex derivative.
  • A rise in CVA decreases dealer’s income, hence the need to hedge CVAs in the same way as other derivatives (using Greek letters).
  • Computing Greek letters for CVA is computationally intensive.
  • Incorporating risks from changes in credit spreads into market risk capital calculations is a required by regulators.
  • Wrong-way risk arises when probability of default and exposure are positively correlated.
  • Right-way risk is the opposite.
  • Evaluating wrong-way or right-way risk requires insights into counterparty’s business and their other transactions.
  • Regulatory mechanism to adjust CVA for wrong-way risk using alpha multiplier (min. 1.2).

9.2.5 Debit Value Adjustment (DVA)

  • Debit Value Adjustment (DVA) is the counterparty’s expected cost due to the dealer’s possible default.
  • It is essentially the counterparty’s CVA.
  • DVA is a benefit to the dealer, offsetting the dealer’s own CVA. This is in line with the zero-sum nature of derivatives.
  • The book value of derivatives is calculated as \(f_{nd} - CVA + DVA\), where \(f_{nd}\) is the value of the derivatives assuming neither side will default.
  • An increase in a dealer’s credit spread leads to an increase in DVA and consequently boosts reported profits.
  • Due to the potential for inflated profits, regulators have excluded DVA gains and losses from the definition of common equity in regulatory capital calculations.

9.3 Credit Value at Risk

  • Value at Risk (VaR) is crucial for regulatory capital determination and internal credit risk management in both financial and nonfinancial firms.
  • Credit risk VaR is the potential credit loss over a specific time, not to be exceeded at a given confidence level.
  • It may consider default losses alone or include downgrade and credit spread changes.
  • Banks use Credit VaR for both regulatory and economic capital.
  • Longer horizon for credit risk VaR (often one year) compared to market risk VaR (usually one day).
  • Credit risk VaR requires more elaborate models than market risk VaR, which often uses historical simulation.
  • Credit Correlation: A key variable that accounts for the interdependence of defaults among different companies, important especially in economic downturns.
  • Increased credit correlation during stressed conditions raises the risk for financial institutions with credit portfolios.

9.3.1 Ratings Transition Matrices

  • Tools showing the likelihood of a company moving from one credit rating to another over a specified time, based on historical data.
  • Example: A firm with an ‘A’ rating has a 92.61% chance of maintaining that rating after one year, according to S&P’s one-year transition matrix.
  • One-year matrices can be extended to longer periods by matrix multiplication (e.g., for five-year horizon take fifth power of the one-year matrix).
  • The model assumes rating changes are independent across periods, though this is not entirely accurate.
  • Recent downgrades make further downgrades more likely.

9.3.2 Methods for Calculating Credit VaR:

  • Vasicek’s Model:
    • Utilizes a one-factor Gaussian copula model.
    • Focuses on time to default for companies in a portfolio.
    • Commonly used for regulatory capital determination.
  • Credit Risk Plus:
    • Proposed by Credit Suisse in 1997.
    • Similar to insurance industry approaches.
    • Calculates default losses based on probabilities of individual company defaults.
    • Incorporates uncertainty in default rates.
  • CreditMetrics:
    • Proposed by JPMorgan in 1997.
    • Uses a Gaussian copula model with a ratings transition matrix.
    • Considers losses from both defaults and credit rating downgrades.
  • CreditMetrics vs. Credit Risk Plus:
    • Should predict the same long-term loss distribution if based on identical assumptions.
    • Differences arise in the timing, not the magnitude, of predicted losses.
    • Example Scenario: Loan downgraded from A to BBB in Year 1, BBB to B in Year 2, and defaults in Year 3.
    • Credit Risk Plus Approach: Assumes no losses in Year 1 and Year 2; calculates the loss only in Year 3.
    • CreditMetrics Approach: Calculates separate revaluation losses in each of the three years.
    • The total losses calculated using the CreditMetrics approach should theoretically equal those calculated using the Credit Risk Plus approach.

9.3.3 Credit Spread Risk

  • Value of credit-sensitive products highly dependent on credit spreads.
  • VaR or ES calculated considering potential credit spread changes.
  • Historical Simulation Approach:
    • Collect last 500 days (or other period) of credit spread changes.
    • Limitations: Assumes no probability of default, may lack quality data.
  • Alternative: CreditMetrics Approach:
    • Utilizes 10-day rating transitions matrix and Monte Carlo simulation.
    • Samples from matrix and credit spread probability distribution.
