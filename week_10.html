10.1 Operational Risk

Operational risk refers to the potential for losses arising from failed internal processes, human errors, system failures, or external events. Unlike credit or market risk, operational risk is inherently more challenging to quantify and manage, as it spans diverse and unpredictable events. Regulatory bodies began addressing operational risk formally in 1999 by introducing capital requirements for its management.

One prominent and growing subtype of operational risk is cyber risk, which necessitates robust security measures and continuous employee education. Unlike insurance companies, which can adjust premiums to offset large losses, banks operate in competitive environments that limit their ability to pass such costs to clients, making effective operational risk management critical.

Operational risk not only affects a bank’s costs and revenues but also interacts with credit and market risks, amplifying their impact.

Definition

The Basel Committee defined operational risk in 2001 as:

“The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems, or from external events.”

  • Inclusions: Legal risk, covering lawsuits and regulatory penalties.
  • Exclusions: Reputation risk and risks arising from strategic decisions.

Operational risks can be classified into several distinct types:

  1. Internal Fraud
    Losses due to intentional acts by employees to deceive or circumvent laws.
    Examples: Insider trading, rogue trading, and embezzlement.

  2. External Fraud
    Fraudulent activities by third parties, often targeting a bank’s systems or assets.
    Examples: Robbery, computer hacking, and phishing attacks.

  3. Employment Practices and Workplace Safety
    Violations related to employment laws, workplace health, and safety.
    Examples: Discrimination lawsuits, claims for wrongful termination, and workplace injury cases.

  4. Clients, Products, and Business Practices
    Failures to meet professional standards or obligations, misuse of customer information, or ethical lapses.
    Examples: Fiduciary breaches, customer data leaks, and money laundering.

  5. Damage to Physical Assets
    Losses stemming from external events that damage tangible assets.
    Examples: Fires, earthquakes, floods, and vandalism.

  6. Business Disruption and System Failures
    Issues that disrupt business operations, often due to technological or utility failures.
    Examples: Hardware malfunctions, server outages, and power failures.

  7. Execution, Delivery, and Process Management
    Failures in transaction processing, documentation, or vendor management.
    Examples: Settlement errors, missed deadlines, and supply chain disputes.

10.1.1 Loss Severity and Loss Frequency

Effective management of operational risk requires understanding two key statistical distributions: loss frequency and loss severity. These distributions provide a framework for estimating the potential magnitude of operational risk losses.

Loss Frequency

Definition

The distribution of the number of loss events occurring within a given time period, typically one year.

  • Commonly modeled using the Poisson distribution:
    \[ P(n) = e^{-\lambda T} \frac{(\lambda T)^n}{n!} \]

where:

  • \(P(n)\): Probability of observing \(n\) losses in a year.
  • \(\lambda\): Average annual frequency of losses.
  • \(T\): Time period (usually \(T = 1\)).

Loss Severity

Definition

The distribution of the magnitude of losses, given that a loss occurs.

  • Often represented by the lognormal distribution, as it accounts for the typically skewed nature of loss sizes, with many small losses and a few large ones.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation combines the loss frequency and severity distributions to estimate the total loss distribution. This process involves:

  1. Simulating the number of loss events from the loss frequency distribution.
  2. Simulating the magnitude of each loss event from the loss severity distribution.
  3. Aggregating simulated losses to determine the total potential loss.

Banks often face challenges due to limited internal data on operational losses. To address this:

  • External data, such as vendor or consortium data, may be used but must be adjusted to reflect the institution’s size and characteristics.
  • Publicly available loss data often overrepresents large losses, introducing biases.

Scenario Analysis

  • Used to estimate risks for rare or unobserved events.
  • Relies on expert judgment to assess potential loss frequency and severity.
  • Encourages proactive planning for adverse events and their mitigation.

10.1.2 The Standardized Measurement Approach (SMA)

Definition

The Standardized Measurement Approach was introduced by regulators in December 2017 to provide a consistent framework for calculating operational risk capital across banks. Its implementation began in January 2023 (source).

Key Components of the SMA

  1. Business Indicator (BI)
    • Measures a bank’s size and potential exposure to operational risk.
    • Components:
      • Interest, Leases, and Dividend Component (ILDC)
      • Services Component (SC)
      • Financial Component (FC)
    • Formula:
      \[ \text{BI} = \text{ILDC} + \text{SC} + \text{FC} \]
  2. Business Indicator Component (BIC)
    • Converts the BI into a base measure of operational risk capital.
    • Formulas:
      • For small banks (\(\text{BI} < 1\) billion euros):
        \[ BIC = 0.12 \times BI \]
      • For \(1 \leq \text{BI} < 30\) billion euros:
        \[ BIC = 0.12 + 0.15 \times (\text{BI} - 1) \]
      • For ( ) billion euros:
        \[ BIC = 4.47 + 0.18 \times (\text{BI} - 30) \]
  3. Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM)
    • Adjusts the BIC based on historical operational risk losses.
    • Formula:
      \[ ILM = \ln \left[ e - 1 + \left( \frac{LC}{BIC} \right)^{0.8} \right] \]
      where:
      • \(LC\): Loss Component, equal to 15 times the average annual operational risk losses over the previous 10 years.
  4. Operational Risk Capital Calculation
    • For small banks (\(\text{BI} < 1\) billion euros):
      \[ \text{Capital} = \text{BIC} \]
    • For other banks:
      \[ \text{Capital} = \text{BIC} \times \text{ILM} \]
  • When \(LC = BIC\): \(ILM = 1\), and the required capital equals the BIC.
  • Banks with higher-than-average operational losses have \(ILM > 1\), requiring additional capital.
  • Conversely, banks with lower-than-average losses have \(ILM < 1\), reducing their capital requirement.
  • Supervisors may apply discretion to equalize capital requirements across banks or adjust them for smaller institutions.

10.1.3 Preventing Operational Risk Losses

Operational risk managers work proactively to mitigate losses by analyzing failures at other institutions and identifying patterns and causal relationships. This analysis helps design preventive measures such as improving employee qualifications, upgrading systems, or enhancing internal processes.

  • Risk Identification:
    Business unit managers play a key role in identifying risks through tools like questionnaires and scorecards in a process known as Risk Control and Self-Assessment (RCSA).

  • Key Risk Indicators (KRIs):
    KRIs serve as early warning signals, allowing managers to monitor and address potential vulnerabilities before they lead to losses. Examples include:

    • Staff turnover rates.
    • Number of failed transactions.
    • Percentage of temporary staff.
    • Supervisor-to-staff ratio.
    • Number of unfilled positions.
    • Percentage of employees not taking at least 10 consecutive leave days annually (an indicator of potential fraud or operational strain).

10.1.4 Allocation of Operational Risk Capital

Operational risk capital must be allocated strategically to encourage effective risk management across business units.

  • Incentivization: Reducing operational risk should decrease the allocated capital, thereby improving the unit’s return on capital.
  • Cost-Benefit Consideration: In some cases, the costs of mitigating certain risks may outweigh the potential benefits, requiring careful evaluation of risk-reduction strategies.
  • Governance: Active support and oversight by senior management and the board of directors are essential. The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision recommends periodic board reviews of risk management programs to ensure their effectiveness.

10.1.5 Insurance in Operational Risk Management

Insurance can play a role in managing operational risk, but it introduces its own challenges:

  • Moral Hazard: Insuring against operational risks may lead to complacency in risk management, as employees and managers might rely on insurance rather than prevention.
  • Adverse Selection: Insurance providers must carefully assess potential clients, as higher-risk institutions are more likely to seek coverage.
  • Risk Mitigation by Insurers: To counter these challenges, insurers use mechanisms like:
    • Deductibles: Ensuring that the insured institution shares part of the loss.
    • Coinsurance: Requiring the insured to cover a percentage of the loss.
    • Policy Limits: Capping the maximum amount payable by the insurer for a claim.

10.2 Liquidity Risk

Liquidity risk gained prominence during the Global Financial Crisis of 2007, highlighting its critical role in financial stability.

Definition
  • Solvency: A firm is solvent if its assets exceed its liabilities, ensuring positive equity value.
  • Liquidity: Refers to the firm’s ability to meet its cash payment obligations as they come due.

Even solvent financial institutions can fail due to liquidity problems, underscoring the distinction between solvency and liquidity. Liquidity risk is divided into two key categories:

  1. Liquidity Funding Risk: The inability to meet short-term obligations.
  2. Liquidity Trading Risk: Challenges related to marketability of assets and associated transaction costs, such as widening bid-ask spreads.

Regulatory liquidity requirements aim to ensure institutions can survive worst-case scenarios, such as sudden market shocks or systemic events. The concept of “liquidity black holes” describes situations where market liquidity vanishes, often triggered by widespread, simultaneous attempts to liquidate positions.

10.2.1 Liquidity Trading Risk

Liquidity trading risk involves challenges in converting assets to cash without significant losses. Several factors affect an asset’s liquidity:

  • Asset Type: Market liquidity varies greatly by asset. For example, IBM shares are typically more liquid than non-investment-grade bonds.
  • Trade Size: Larger trades can strain market liquidity and widen bid-ask spreads.
  • Speed of Liquidation: Quickly liquidating positions often requires accepting lower prices or higher costs.
  • Economic Environment: Adverse economic conditions exacerbate liquidity risks by reducing market participants’ willingness to trade.

Bid-Ask Spread

The bid-ask spread is a primary measure of liquidity and can be expressed in two forms:

  • Cash Bid-Ask Spread:
    \[ p = \text{Ask price} - \text{Bid price} \]

  • Proportional Bid-Ask Spread:
    \[ s = \frac{\text{Ask price} - \text{Bid price}}{\text{Mid-market price}} \]

Cost of Liquidation

The cost of liquidation reflects the expense of unwinding a portfolio under different market conditions:

  • Normal Market Conditions:
    \[ \text{Cost} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{s_i \alpha_i}{2} \]

    • \(s_i\): Proportional bid-ask spread for the \(i\)-th asset.
    • \(\alpha_i\): Value of the position in the \(i\)-th asset.

  • Stressed Market Conditions:
    \[ \text{Cost} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{(\mu_i + \lambda \sigma_i) \alpha_i}{2} \]

    • \(\mu_i\): Mean of the proportional bid-ask spread for the \(i\)-th asset.
    • \(\sigma_i\): Standard deviation of the proportional bid-ask spread for the \(i\)-th asset.
    • \(\lambda\): Multiplier for the desired confidence level.

Key Considerations in Liquidity Management

  • Diversification alone does not guarantee a reduction in liquidity risk.
  • Liquidity monitoring is sensitive to the assumed liquidation timeframe.
  • Holding multiple small positions generally reduces liquidity risk compared to a few large positions.
  • Limiting the size of any single position is a practical method for managing liquidity trading risk.

Liquidity-Adjusted Value at Risk (L-VaR)

Liquidity-Adjusted VaR extends traditional VaR by incorporating liquidity risk. It combines market risk with the cost of liquidation, providing a comprehensive risk metric. L-VaR can be calculated for both normal and stressed market conditions, enhancing its applicability during periods of market stress.

Optimal Position Unwinding

Unwinding large positions presents a tradeoff between:

  1. Quick Liquidation: Minimizes market risk but incurs higher bid-ask spreads.
  2. Slow Liquidation: Reduces bid-ask spreads but increases exposure to market risk.

Almgren and Chriss (2001) proposed a mathematical framework to optimize this tradeoff, balancing execution cost and risk exposure.

Additional Measures of Market Liquidity

  • Trading Volume and Price Impact: High trading volumes indicate better liquidity, while large price changes for small trades reflect poor liquidity.

  • Amihud’s Illiquidity Measure: Proposed by Amihud (2002), this metric links daily returns to trading volume:
    \[ \text{Amihud's Measure} = \frac{\text{Absolute daily return}}{\text{Daily dollar volume}} \]

    • Lower liquidity results in higher values of this measure.
    • Empirical evidence shows that assets with lower liquidity require higher expected returns as compensation for liquidity risk.

10.2.2 Liquidity Funding Risk

Liquidity funding risk pertains to a financial institution’s ability to meet its cash needs as they arise. Even solvent institutions can fail if they encounter liquidity constraints. The primary causes of liquidity funding problems are:

  1. Economic Liquidity Stresses: A lack of willingness among investors to fund credit-risk-bearing activities, often observed during financial crises.
  2. Aggressive Funding Decisions: Relying on short-term funding to meet long-term needs, creating a mismatch in liquidity horizons.
  3. Poor Financial Performance: Weak performance can lead to a loss of market confidence, triggering deposit withdrawals and funding difficulties.

In severe cases, all these factors may converge, exacerbating liquidity issues. Effective management involves predicting cash needs—some are predictable (e.g., bond coupon payments), while others, such as deposit withdrawals or margin calls, are not. Complex financial instruments add further unpredictability, particularly in scenarios involving downgrade triggers or counterparty defaults.

The ultimate goal is to ensure that institutions can meet cash demands even during adverse scenarios.

Sources of Liquidity

Institutions rely on multiple liquidity sources, each with unique characteristics and challenges:

  1. Cash and Treasury Securities: Highly liquid and reliable but expensive to maintain in large quantities.

  2. Liquidating Trading Book: Requires monitoring under both normal and stressed market conditions to assess liquidity accurately.

  3. Ability to Borrow: Borrowing is accessible during stable periods but becomes challenging during times of market stress.

  4. Retail and Wholesale Deposits: Highly volatile and susceptible to market-wide conditions, making them an unreliable liquidity source in crises.

  5. Securitization: Once a dependable source of liquidity, its risks became evident after the 2007 financial crisis.

  6. Central Bank Borrowing: Serves as a lender of last resort but is subject to collateral requirements and potential haircuts.

Key Considerations

  • Stressed vs. Normal Conditions: Liquidity sources behave differently in stress scenarios; stressed conditions often constrain borrowing capacity or require rapid liquidation of assets.
  • Collateral and Haircuts: Borrowing from central banks depends on the quality and value of collateral, which may be discounted (haircut).
  • Hedging Liquidity Risk: Derivative hedging may create additional liquidity pressures through margin requirements.

10.2.3 Reserve Requirements and Regulation

Liquidity management is tightly regulated to ensure institutions can withstand short-term and long-term liquidity challenges:

  1. Reserve Requirements: Banks must hold a fixed percentage of their deposits in reserve, limiting their ability to lend but ensuring liquidity. Requirements vary by country and influence the broader money supply.

  2. Basel III Liquidity Risk Requirements:

    • Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): Ensures that banks hold enough high-quality liquid assets to cover net cash outflows for a 30-day stress scenario.
      \[ \text{LCR} \geq 100\% \]
    • Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): Promotes stability by requiring sufficient stable funding to meet long-term funding needs.
      \[ \text{NSFR} \geq 100\% \]

10.2.4 Liquidity Black Holes

A liquidity black hole occurs when market liquidity evaporates, often during periods of extreme stress. This phenomenon arises when most market participants simultaneously seek to sell (or buy) assets, with few or no counterparties willing to trade. While transactions may still occur, they do so at significantly widened bid-ask spreads, reflecting the market’s illiquidity.

  1. Negative Feedback Traders: These traders stabilize markets by buying when prices fall and selling when prices rise.
  2. Positive Feedback Traders: These traders destabilize markets by buying when prices rise and selling when prices fall, amplifying price movements and contributing to liquidity black holes.

The dominance of positive feedback traders creates unstable markets prone to sudden liquidity crises.

Reasons for Positive Feedback Trading

  1. Trend Trading: Traders buy or sell based on observed price trends, reinforcing upward or downward movements.
  2. Stop-Loss Rules: Automatic sell orders triggered when prices fall below a predetermined level can accelerate price declines.
  3. Dynamic Hedging: Adjusting hedge positions for short options increases selling pressure in falling markets, reducing liquidity.
  4. Synthetic Options: Commercial programs that mimic put options can cause large-scale selling during market downturns.
  5. Margin Calls: Sharp market moves trigger margin requirements, forcing leveraged traders to sell assets, further depressing prices.
  6. Predatory Trading: Opportunistic traders exploit large forced sales by driving prices down further.
  7. Historical Example – LTCM Failure: The collapse of Long-Term Capital Management illustrates how excessive leverage and margin calls can destabilize markets.

Leveraging and Deleveraging Cycles

  • Leveraging: During periods of abundant liquidity, credit availability increases, fueling asset price growth.
  • Deleveraging: When liquidity contracts, credit availability tightens, driving asset prices downward.

Hedge funds are especially vulnerable to these cycles due to their reliance on leverage. The transition from leveraging to deleveraging often triggers liquidity black holes, as asset sales cascade.

Irrational Exuberance

The term “irrational exuberance”, coined by Alan Greenspan in 1996, describes overvaluation driven by speculative behavior and short-term profit-seeking.
- Overvalued markets can create self-reinforcing cycles of buying and overconfidence.
- These cycles often end in sharp corrections or crashes, accompanied by liquidity black holes.

The Role of Regulation

  1. Uniform Regulations and Synchronous Behavior: A one-size-fits-all regulatory framework can lead to synchronized actions among institutions, amplifying market stress during crises.
  2. Need for Differentiated Capital Requirements: Financial institutions experience different cycles, and uniform capital requirements may exacerbate systemic risks. Tailored regulations can mitigate these issues.

The Importance of Market Diversity

A lack of diversity in market behavior is a key contributor to liquidity black holes. When participants act homogeneously, markets become fragile and prone to crises.

  1. Regulatory Differentiation: Different types of institutions should face varied regulatory requirements to promote heterogeneity in market behavior.
  2. Contrarian Investment Strategies: Contrarian strategies, which involve acting against prevailing market trends, can stabilize markets. However, they are difficult to implement under short-term, VaR-based risk management frameworks, which discourage deviations from the norm.

Promoting diversity in market participants and strategies is crucial to mitigating liquidity black holes and fostering resilient financial systems.

10.3 Model Risk Management

Models have become indispensable tools in modern finance, supporting tasks such as credit decisions, liquidity management, and risk assessment. Advances in analytics, particularly machine learning, have automated tasks traditionally performed by humans. However, the increasing reliance on models brings with it significant model risk, a subset of operational risk, necessitating dedicated model risk management functions in large institutions.

Models are approximations of real-world phenomena, relying on assumptions and simplifications. Understanding these assumptions, their limitations, and their potential implications is essential. Regulatory systems require that banks develop, validate, and use models responsibly to minimize the risk of adverse outcomes.

10.3.1 Regulatory Guidance

One key regulatory framework is SR 11-7 (Federal Reserve SR 11-7 Guidance), which establishes principles for effective model risk management.

Definition

Model risk arises primarily from:
1. Fundamental Errors: Mistakes in data, calculations, or design.
2. Improper Usage: Misapplication or misunderstanding of a model’s assumptions and limitations.

Key Components of Model Risk Management

  1. Model Development:
    • Thorough documentation is essential for continuity, regulatory compliance, and validation.
    • Testing should be rigorous, including:
      • Out-of-Sample Testing: Evaluates performance on unseen data.
      • Stress Testing: Assesses performance under extreme or unlikely scenarios.
  2. Model Validation:
    • Validation should be conducted by independent groups, fostering objectivity and critical evaluation.
    • Core elements of validation:
      • Conceptual Soundness: Ensures the model is theoretically robust.
      • Ongoing Monitoring: Tracks model performance over time to detect deviations or failures.
      • Outcomes Analysis: Compares predicted outcomes with actual results to assess accuracy.
    • Corporate culture should support independence and transparency in validation.
  3. Vendor Models:
    • Vendor-supplied models must undergo the same validation rigor as internally developed models.
    • Institutions should demand thorough documentation and continuous monitoring from vendors.
    • Contingency plans should be in place for vendor unavailability to ensure business continuity.

Models in Physics vs. Finance

While models in physics describe fundamental and consistent natural laws, financial models are approximations influenced by human behavior and market dynamics. This distinction creates unique challenges in finance:

  • Stability vs. Volatility: Parameters like gravitational pull in physics are constant, but financial parameters (e.g., volatility) fluctuate frequently.
  • Precision vs. Approximation: Physical models achieve high precision; financial models often introduce significant errors due to their reliance on assumptions and approximations.
  • Behavioral Impact: Financial models are sensitive to human decisions, market sentiment, and external shocks.

Incorrect assumptions or inadequate validation in financial models can lead to substantial errors, resulting in significant financial losses. Institutions must actively monitor model performance, trading patterns, and competitive positioning across products to detect and mitigate potential risks early.

10.3.2 Models for Pricing Actively Traded Products

In the over-the-counter (OTC) market, financial instruments often require valuation using models because they are not identical to actively traded products, even if they are standard in nature (e.g., options).

  • Market-Derived Prices: Actively traded instruments have transparent, market-determined prices.
  • Model Use: Models like the Black–Scholes–Merton framework ensure pricing consistency for products that are similar but not identical to market-traded instruments.
  • Role of Models: Primarily serve as interpolation tools, meaning they help fill in gaps for non-standard instruments without significantly altering prices under normal conditions.
  • Model Risk: Becomes critical during extreme market conditions, requiring careful validation to ensure reliability.
  • Hedging and Risks:
    • Hedging strategies account for both variables within and outside the model’s scope.
    • Portfolio value fluctuations may arise due to unhedged risks, model imperfections, or new trades.
  • Risk Diversification: Large institutions often diversify risks across portfolios, mitigating the effects of inaccurate hedging strategies.

10.3.3 Models for Less Actively Traded Products

For less actively traded products, valuation and risk management pose greater challenges:

  • Calibration for Traded Products: Actively traded products benefit from calibration to observed market prices, minimizing model risk.
  • Valuation Challenges for Non-Traded Products: Model choice plays a significant role in pricing and hedging, as direct market calibration is not possible.
  • Relative Valuation Approach: Institutions focus on relative valuation, referencing similar market-traded instruments rather than absolute valuation.
  • Multiple Model Usage: Using several models helps quantify model risk by providing a range of valuations.
    • Institutions should price conservatively at the higher end of this range.
    • Conservative profit recognition avoids overestimating gains from illiquid or complex instruments.
  • Accounting Standards: Valuations are categorized into:
    • Level 1: Prices from active markets.
    • Level 2: Prices based on observable inputs but requiring some modeling.
    • Level 3: Prices based on unobservable inputs, heavily reliant on assumptions.

10.3.4 Successful Pricing Models and Common Pitfalls

The aim of a pricing model is to provide insights into market behavior and support decision-making while avoiding unnecessary complexity.

Characteristics of Successful Pricing Models

  1. Consistency: Aligns with observed market data, facilitating reliable interpolation.
  2. Communication: Produces stable and interpretable metrics, such as implied volatility, which are easier for traders and stakeholders to understand.
  3. Simplicity: Offers intuitive frameworks that traders can grasp and apply effectively.

Model-Building Best Practices

  1. Avoid Overfitting:
    • Complex models may fit historical data well but fail in new or changing market conditions.
    • Overfitting leads to unrealistic predictions and poor decision-making.
  2. Limit Over-Parameterization:
    • Excessive parameters increase model complexity without adding significant value.
    • Simplified models are more robust across different market regimes.
  3. Prioritize Simplicity:
    • Simple models are easier to explain, validate, and maintain.
    • They reduce reliance on “black box” mechanisms, which can obscure risks and undermine trust.

10.4 Practice Questions and Problems

10.4.1 Operational Risk

  1. What risks are included by regulators in their definition of operational risks? What risks are not included?
  2. Discuss how moral hazard and adverse selection are handled in car insurance.
  3. When is a trading loss classified as a market risk and when is it classified as an operational risk?
  4. Discuss whether there is (a) moral hazard and (b) adverse selection in life insurance contracts.
  5. Give two examples of key risk indicators that might be monitored by a central operational risk management group within a bank.
  6. Consider two banks, each with a BIC of 200 million euros. Bank A has suffered 10 operational risk losses of 20 million euros in the past 10 years. Bank B has suffered one 200 million euro loss in the past 10 years. What is the operational risk regulatory capital for each bank under SMA?

10.4.2 Liquidity Risk

  1. An asset is quoted bid 50, offer 55. What does this mean? What is the proportional bid–offer spread?
  2. Suppose that an investor has shorted shares worth $5,000 of Company A and bought shares worth $3,000 of Company B. The proportional bid–offer spread for Company A is 0.01 and the proportional bid–offer spread for Company B is 0.02. What does it cost the investor to unwind the portfolio?
  3. Suppose that in Problem 2. the bid–offer spreads for the two companies are normally distributed. For Company A the bid–offer spread has a mean of 0.01 and a standard deviation of 0.01. For Company B the bid–offer spread has a mean of 0.02 and a standard deviation of 0.03. What is the cost of unwinding that the investor is 95% confident will not be exceeded?
  4. Explain the difference between the liquidity coverage ratio (LCR) and the net stable funding ratio (NSFR).
  5. Why is it risky to rely on wholesale deposits for funding?
  6. What is meant by (a) positive feedback trading and (b) negative feedback trading? Which is liable to lead to liquidity problems?
  7. What is meant by liquidity-adjusted VaR?
  8. Explain how liquidity black holes occur. How can regulation lead to liquidity black holes?
  9. Why is it beneficial to the liquidity of markets for traders to follow diverse trading strategies?

10.4.3 Model Risk Management

  1. How is a financial institution likely to find that it is using a model different from its competitors for valuing a particular type of derivatives product?
  2. Distinguish between within-model and outside-model hedging.
  3. What is meant by P&L decomposition?
  4. What is meant by over-fitting and over-parameterization in model building?
  5. “For structured products, traders mark to model. They do not mark to market.” Explain this remark.
