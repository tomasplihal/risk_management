10  Operational Risk, Liquidity Risk, and Model Risk

Learning outcomes:

  • Recognize the evolution of capital charge for operational risk.
  • Comprehend the need for key risk indicators in operational risk management.
  • Learn how to allocate operational risk capital to business units.
  • Be aware of insurance options for mitigating operational risk and their limitations.
  • Acknowledge the evolving nature of operational risk management procedures.
  • Understand two types of liquidity risk: trading and funding.
  • Recognize factors affecting liquidity trading risk.
  • Know how to measure liquidity using bid–ask spread methods.
  • Understand the importance of forecasting cash needs in normal and stressed conditions.
  • Identify “liquidity black holes” and the need for diverse trading strategies.
  • Understand model risk as a form of operational risk.
  • Identify the unique challenges posed by financial models compared to physical science models.

10.1 Operational Risk

  • Regulators introduced operational risk capital requirements in 1999.
  • Cyber risk is a growing form of operational risk, necessitating advanced security measures and ongoing education.
  • Operational risk is harder to quantify and manage compared to credit or market risk.
  • Unlike insurance companies that can raise premiums to cover large losses, banks do not have this option as they operate in a competitive environment.

10.1.1 Defining Operational Risk

  • Basel Committee’s 2001 definition: “The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems or from external events.”
  • Legal risk is included, but reputation and strategic decision risks are excluded.
  • Operational risks affect bank costs and revenue, and can interact with credit and market risks.
  • E.g., natural disasters, political and regulatory risk, security breaches, and so on.

10.1.2 Types of Operational Risk

  • Internal Fraud: Intentional acts to defraud or circumvent law by internal parties. Examples include insider trading and rogue trading.
  • External Fraud: Fraudulent acts by third parties such as robbery and computer hacking.
  • Employment Practices and Workplace Safety: Violations related to employment laws, health and safety, including discrimination claims.
  • Clients, Products, and Business Practices: Failures to meet professional obligations or misuse of customer information. Examples include fiduciary breaches and money laundering.
  • Damage to Physical Assets: Losses from natural disasters or vandalism. Examples include fires and earthquakes.
  • Business Disruption and System Failures: Issues like hardware failures and utility outages disrupting business.
  • Execution, Delivery, and Process Management: Transaction failures, documentation issues, and vendor disputes.

10.1.3 Loss Severity and Loss Frequency

  • Loss frequency and loss severity are two fundamental distributions to estimate potential size of operational risks.

  • Loss Frequency: Distribution of the number of losses observed in one year. Commonly modeled using the Poisson distribution. \[ P(n) = e^{-\lambda T} \frac{(\lambda T)^n}{n!} \]

  • Where \(\lambda\) is the average number of losses per year.

  • Loss Severity: Distribution of the size of a loss, given that a loss occurs. Often modeled using the lognormal distribution.

  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Used to combine loss frequency and severity to determine a total loss distribution.

  • Banks often have limited internal data but may utilize data from other banks or vendors.

  • Losses from other banks must be adjusted to reflect size of the institution.

  • Publicly reported data, like that from vendors, is often skewed towards larger losses.

  • Scenario analysis is used to assess operational risks that have not yet been observed.

  • Committee estimates for loss frequency and severity are subjective.

  • Scenarios can be developed to think actively about potential adverse events and formulate responses.

10.1.4 The Standardized Measurement Approach

  • Defined by regulators in December 2017, see www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d424.pdf.

  • Implementation date: January 2023

  • Its purpose was to standardize the calculation of capital for operational risk in banks.

  • Business Indicator (BI) to measures the bank’s size and therefore its potential operational risk exposure:

    • Interest Leases and Dividend Component (ILDC)
    • Services Component (SC)
    • Financial Component (FC)
    • Formula: \(\text{BI} = \text{ILDC} + \text{SC} + \text{FC}\)

  • Business Indicator Component (BIC):

    • Small Banks: \(BIC = 0.12 \times BI\)
    • BI between 1 and 30 billion euros: \(BIC = 0.12 + 0.15 \times (BI-1)\)
    • BI above 30 billion euros**: \(BIC = 4.47 + 0.18 \times (BI-30)\)

  • Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM):

    • Loss Component (LC) is equal to 15 times average annual operational risk losses over previous 10 years. \[ ILM = \ln \left[ e - 1 + \left( \frac{LC}{BIC} \right)^{0.8} \right] \]

  • Operational Risk Capital:

  • For Small Banks (BI < 1 billion euros): Equal to BIC.

  • For Other Banks: Equal to \(BIC \times ILM\).

  • National Supervisors’ Discretion: May set capital equal to BIC for all banks or use loss data for small banks.

  • If \(LC = BIC\), then \(ILM = 1\) and required capital is BIC.

  • Banks with operational risk losses higher than average will have \(ILM > 1\).

  • Banks with losses lower than average will have \(ILM < 1\).

10.1.5 Preventing Operational Risk Losses

  • Operational risk managers aim to prevent losses by studying failures at other banks.
  • Managers explore causal relationships between decisions and operational risk to identify preventive measures, such as modifying employee qualifications or upgrading systems.
  • Business unit managers identify operational risks, often using questionnaires and scorecards (risk control and self-assesment).
  • Key risk indicators act as early warning systems, tracking metrics like:
    1. Staff turnover
    2. Number of failed transactions
    3. Number of positions filled by temps
    4. Ratio of supervisors to staff
    5. Number of open positions
    6. Percentage of staff who did not take 10 days consecutive leave in the last 12 months

10.1.6 Allocation of Operational Risk Capital

  • Capital should be allocated in a way that motivates business units to manage operational risks effectively.
  • Reduction in operational risk should lead to less allocated capital, thereby increasing return on capital for the unit.
  • However, costs of reducing certain risks may outweigh the benefits.
  • Support from senior management and board oversight is critical.
  • Basel Committee on Banking Supervision recommends regular board reviews of risk management programs.

10.1.7 Insurance

  • Moral Hazard: Insuring against operational risks may encourage lax behavior in risk management.
  • Adverse Selection: Insurance companies may attract higher-risk clients, requiring rigorous assessment of bank controls.
  • Deductibles, coinsurance, and policy limits are used to manage these insurance risks.

10.2 Liquidity Risk

  • Elevated by the Global Financial Crisis of 2007.
  • Solvency refers to a company having more assets than liabilities, so that the value of its equity is positive.
  • Liquidity refers to the ability of a company to make cash payments as they become due.
  • Financial institutions that are solvent can fail because of liquidity problems.
  • Liquidity Funding Risk: Risk associated with inability to meet short-term obligations.
  • Liquidity Trading Risk: Concerns bid-ask spreads and asset marketability.
  • Regulatory liquidity requirements aim to ensure worst-case scenario survival.
  • “Liquidity Black Holes”: Extreme situations where market liquidity evaporates suddenly.

10.2.1 Liquidity Trading Risk

  • Factors Affecting Liquidity:
    • Asset type (e.g., IBM shares vs. non-investment-grade bonds)
    • Trade size
    • Speed of liquidation
    • Economic environment
  • Market makers may widen bid-ask spreads for large trades.
  • Without market makers, implicit bid-ask spreads exist, varying from 0.01% to 10% of mid-market price.
  • Market participants may anticipate large position unwinds, affecting liquidity.
  • Importance of maintaining confidentiality to avoid predatory trading.
  • Lack of transparency can lead to illiquid markets.
  • During the Global Financial Crisis, complex derivatives became illiquid due to lack of understanding.
  • “Liquidity Black Hole”: liquidity evaporates when multiple institutions try to unwind similar positions simultaneously.

10.2.2 Measuring Market Liquidity

  • Bid-Ask Spread: Measures liquidity either as a cash amount or as a proportion of the asset price.

  • Cash Bid-Ask Spread: \(p = \text{Ask price} - \text{Bid price}\)

  • Proportional Bid-Ask Spread: \(s = \frac{\text{Ask price} - \text{Bid price}}{\text{Mid-market price}}\)

  • One measure of the liquidity of a book is how much it would cost to liquidate the book in normal market conditions within a certain time.

  • Cost of Liquidation: Estimates the cost to liquidate an asset or book of assets.

  • Cost of Liquidation (Normal Market):
    \[ \text{Cost} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{s_i \alpha_i}{2} \]

  • where \(s_i\) is the proportional bid-ask spread and \(\alpha_i\) is the value of the position.

  • Cost of Liquidation (Stressed Market):
    \[ \text{Cost} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{(\mu_i + \lambda \sigma_i) \alpha_i}{2} \]

  • \(\mu_i\) is the mean of the proportional bid-offer spread for the \(i\)th financial instrument.

  • \(\sigma_i\) is standard deviation of the proportional bid-offer spread for the \(i\)th financial instrument.

  • \(\lambda\) gives the required confidence level for the spread.

  • Diversification doesn’t necessarily reduce liquidity risk.

  • Monitoring liquidity regularly is crucial.

  • The measure is sensitive to the time period assumed for liquidation.

  • Holding many small positions rather than a few large positions therefore tends to entail less liquidity risk.

  • Setting limits to the size of any one position can therefore be one way of reducing liquidity trading risk.

10.2.2.1 Additional Notes

  • Liquidity-Adjusted VaR:
    • It incorporates both market risk and liquidity risk into a single metric (VaR plus Cost of liquidation).
    • It can be calculated using either normal market conditions or stressed market conditions.
  • Unwinding a Position Optimally:
    • A dilemma exists between liquidating quickly (higher bid-ask spread, less market risk) and liquidating slowly (lower bid-ask spread, higher market risk).
    • Almgren and Chriss (2001) provide a mathematical approach to optimize this process.
  • Other Measures of Market Liquidity:
    • Besides bid-ask spread, trading volume and price impact are also considered.
    • A metric proposed by Amihud relates daily return to daily dollar volume, suggesting that higher expected returns compensate for lower liquidity.
    • Amihud’s Measure: Average of \[ \frac{\text{Absolute value of daily return}}{\text{Daily dollar volume}} \]
    • This is a simpler but similar measure to price impact.
    • Amihud (2002) demonstrates that as liquidity decreases, an asset’s expected return increases, implying that investors are compensated for taking on liquidity risk.

10.2.3 Liquidity Funding Risk

  • Focuses on a financial institution’s capacity to meet cash needs.
  • Institutions can be solvent yet fail due to liquidity issues.
  • Causes of Liquidity Funding Problems:
    1. Economic Liquidity Stresses: Reluctance from investors to fund any credit-risk bearing activities.
    2. Aggressive Funding Decisions: Tendency to fund long-term needs with short-term instruments, causing liquidity mismatch.
    3. Poor Financial Performance: Leads to loss of confidence, deposit withdrawal, and funding difficulties.
  • Often, all three causes may contribute to severe liquidity problems.
  • Managing Risk:
    1. Predicting Cash Needs: Some are predictable (e.g., bond coupons), others less so (e.g., deposit withdrawals).
    2. Complex Financial Instruments: Increase unpredictability of cash needs due to factors like downgrade triggers and counterparty defaults.
  • The key is to ensure cash needs can be met even in adverse scenarios.

10.2.3.1 Sources of Liquidity

  1. Cash and Treasury Securities: Highly liquid but expensive, limiting the amount held.
  2. Liquidating Trading Book: Requires monitoring liquidity under stressed market conditions.
  3. Ability to Borrow: Easy during unstressed periods but difficult during market stress.
  4. Retail and Wholesale Deposits: Volatile and dependent on market-wide conditions.
  5. Securitization: Used to be reliable but became risky after 2007.
  6. Central Bank Borrowing: Lender of last resort, subject to collateral and haircut.
  • Additional Considerations:
    1. Stressed vs Normal Conditions: Different liquidity sources function differently under stress.
    2. Collateral and Haircut: Important when borrowing from central banks.
    3. Hedging Issues: May result in liquidity problems due to margin requirements.

10.2.4 Reserve Requirements and Regulation

  • A set percentage of a bank’s deposits must be kept in reserve.
  • Affects the money supply; varies by country.
  • Basel III Liquidity Risk Requirements:
    • LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio): High-quality liquid assets to net cash outflows in a 30-day stress period should be \(\geq\) 100%.
    • NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio): Amount of stable funding to required stable funding should be \(\geq\) 100%.

10.2.5 Liquidity Black Holes

  • A situation where market liquidity dries up due to everyone wanting to sell and no one wanting to buy, or vice versa.
  • It is still possible to buy/sell assets but the bid-ask spread is huge.
  • Negative Feedback Traders: Buy when prices fall, sell when prices rise.
  • Positive Feedback Traders: Do the opposite, exacerbating price movements.
  • Dominance of positive feedback traders leads to unstable, illiquid markets.
  • Reasons for positive feedback trading:
    1. Trend Trading: Buy or sell based on asset price trends.
    2. Stop-Loss Rules: Automatic selling below certain price levels.
    3. Dynamic Hedging: Can reduce market liquidity when large short option positions are hedged.
    4. Synthetic Options: Commercial programs for synthetic put options can cause liquidity black holes.
    5. Margins: Big market moves lead to margin calls, further destabilizing prices.
    6. Predatory Trading: Knowing an investor must sell large quantities can drive prices further down.
    7. LTCM Failure: Example of how leveraging and margin calls can exacerbate liquidity issues.
  • Leveraging and Deleveraging:
    • Banks awash with liquidity make credit easily available, leading to increased asset prices (“leveraging”).
    • Deleveraging is the opposite: reduced liquidity in banks results in tightened credit and decreased asset prices.
    • Hedge funds are particularly affected by leveraging-deleveraging cycles.
  • Irrational Exuberance:
    • Term coined by Alan Greenspan in 1996 to describe overvaluation of asset prices.
    • Often self-reinforcing due to high short-term profits.
    • Leads to liquidity black holes and market crashes.
  • The Impact of Regulation:
    • Uniform regulatory environment can lead to synchronous behavior among banks, exacerbating market issues.
    • Different financial cycles necessitate different capital requirements; uniformity can be problematic.
  • The Importance of Diversity:
    • Market participants often do not act independently, causing liquidity black holes.
    • Different types of financial institutions should be regulated differently to introduce market diversity.
    • Contrarian investment strategies may offer benefits but are hard to implement under short-term VaR-based risk management.

10.3 Model Risk Management

  • Models increasingly used for various applications like credit decisions, liquidity management, and risk assessment.
  • Advanced analytic techniques, including machine learning, automate tasks previously done by humans.
  • Large institutions require a model risk management function for appropriate use and validation.
  • Models are approximations, dependent on assumptions about the phenomena they represent.
  • Importance of understanding these assumptions and their limitations.
  • Model risk is considered a component of operational risk.
  • Banks must develop, validate, and use models in accordance with regulatory systems.

10.3.1 Regulatory Guidance

  • SR 11-7 by the U.S. Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System outlines principles for effective model risk management.
  • Model risk defined as adverse consequences from incorrect or misused models.
  • Model risk occurs primarily for two reasons:
    1. The model may have fundamental errors (data, calculation)
    2. The model may be used incorrectly or inappropriately (limitations and assumptions of the model)
  • Model Development:
    • Importance of thorough documentation for model continuity, regulatory scrutiny, and validation.
    • Testing should be rigorous, including out-of-sample tests and performance under extreme conditions.
  • Model Validation:
    • Model validation groups provide an independent check.
    • Three core elements: conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, and outcomes analysis.
    • Importance of independence and corporate culture.
  • Vendor Models:
    • Vendor models should undergo similar validation processes.
    • Require documentation and ongoing monitoring from vendors.
    • Contingency plans for vendor unavailability.

10.3.2 Models in Physics and Finance

  • Physical models are precise because they describe fundamental laws of nature.
  • Financial models are approximate, influenced by human behavior.
  • Parameters like gravitational pull in physics are constant.
  • Financial parameters like volatility change frequently.
  • Financial models can result in significant errors and losses.
  • Incorrect assumptions and lack of validation can be costly.
  • To manage model risk, monitoring trading patterns and competitiveness in various products is crucial.

10.3.3 Models for Pricing Actively Traded Products

  • A situation that is common in the over-the-counter market is one where a financial instrument that has to be valued is a standard product such as an option, but not exactly the same as one that trades in the market.
  • Actively traded financial instruments have market-determined prices.
  • Models like Black–Scholes–Merton are used for similar but non-standard products to ensure pricing consistency.
  • Models are primarily interpolation tools; changes in model usually don’t affect prices significantly.
  • Model risk exists in extreme market conditions and must be validated.
  • Hedging focuses on both within-model and outside-model variables.
  • Portfolio value changes can be attributed to unhedged risks, model imperfections, and new trades.
  • Risk is often diversified away in large financial institutions, minimizing the impact of inaccurate hedging.

10.3.4 Models for Less Actively Traded Products

  • For actively traded products, calibrating models to market prices minimizes model risk.
  • For less-traded products, model choice significantly affects valuation and hedging.
  • Institutions often aim for relative, not absolute, valuation based on similar market-traded instruments.
  • Multiple models should be used for non-standard products to understand model risk.
  • Using multiple models can provide a range of possible prices.
  • Institutions should charge at the higher end of this range and be conservative about recognizing profits.
  • Accounting standards categorize valuations as Level 1, 2, or 3 based on reliability and need for modeling assumptions.

10.3.5 Successful Pricing Models and Common Mistakes

  • The goal is to create models that aid in market understanding and decision-making without unnecessary complexity.

  • Successful Pricing Models:

    1. Consistency: Aligns with observed market prices, useful for interpolation.
    2. Communication: Facilitates stable, easier-to-understand metrics like implied volatilities.
    3. Simplicity: Intuitive enough for traders to develop a nuanced understanding.

  • Model-Building Considerations:

    1. Avoid Over-fitting: Complex models may capture data well but lack utility and might produce unrealistic outcomes.
    2. Caution on Over-parameterization: Adding too many parameters complicates the model and could fail during market regime changes.
    3. Preference for Simplicity: Simple models are often preferred as they are easier to understand and less of a ‘black box.’

10.4 Practice Questions and Problems

10.4.1 Operational Risk

  1. What risks are included by regulators in their definition of operational risks? What risks are not included?
  2. Discuss how moral hazard and adverse selection are handled in car insurance.
  3. When is a trading loss classified as a market risk and when is it classified as an operational risk?
  4. Discuss whether there is (a) moral hazard and (b) adverse selection in life insurance contracts.
  5. Give two examples of key risk indicators that might be monitored by a central operational risk management group within a bank.
  6. Consider two banks, each with a BIC of 200 million euros. Bank A has suffered 10 operational risk losses of 20 million euros in the past 10 years. Bank B has suffered one 200 million euro loss in the past 10 years. What is the operational risk regulatory capital for each bank under SMA?

10.4.2 Liquidity Risk

  1. An asset is quoted bid 50, offer 55. What does this mean? What is the proportional bid–offer spread?
  2. Suppose that an investor has shorted shares worth $5,000 of Company A and bought shares worth $3,000 of Company B. The proportional bid–offer spread for Company A is 0.01 and the proportional bid–offer spread for Company B is 0.02. What does it cost the investor to unwind the portfolio?
  3. Suppose that in Problem 2. the bid–offer spreads for the two companies are normally distributed. For Company A the bid–offer spread has a mean of 0.01 and a standard deviation of 0.01. For Company B the bid–offer spread has a mean of 0.02 and a standard deviation of 0.03. What is the cost of unwinding that the investor is 95% confident will not be exceeded?
  4. Explain the difference between the liquidity coverage ratio (LCR) and the net stable funding ratio (NSFR).
  5. Why is it risky to rely on wholesale deposits for funding?
  6. What is meant by (a) positive feedback trading and (b) negative feedback trading? Which is liable to lead to liquidity problems?
  7. What is meant by liquidity-adjusted VaR?
  8. Explain how liquidity black holes occur. How can regulation lead to liquidity black holes?
  9. Why is it beneficial to the liquidity of markets for traders to follow diverse trading strategies?

10.4.3 Model Risk Management

  1. How is a financial institution likely to find that it is using a model different from its competitors for valuing a particular type of derivatives product?
  2. Distinguish between within-model and outside-model hedging.
  3. What is meant by P&L decomposition?
  4. What is meant by over-fitting and over-parameterization in model building?
  5. “For structured products, traders mark to model. They do not mark to market.” Explain this remark.
