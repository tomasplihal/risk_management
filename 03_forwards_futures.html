2.1 Introduction

  • Forward and futures contracts are commitments for one party to buy and the other to sell an underlying instrument at a future date at a pre-agreed price. Forward contracts are customized agreements, while futures contracts are standardized, created on a futures exchange, and subject to federal regulation.
  • The material provides scenarios where these contracts are used for risk management.
  • Arbitrage strategies, which profit from price discrepancies, are not discussed further as they were covered previously.
  • Businesses manage a variety of risks, often through hedging, which involves taking a market position to protect against unfavorable outcomes.
  • Instead of just hedging, the broader concept of ‘risk management’ will be emphasized.

2.2 Strategies and Applications for Managing Interest Rate Risk

  • Interest rate risk is a concern for financial institutions.

  • It arises from the possibility of changes in interest rates affecting the profitability and financial stability of the institution.

  • Financial institutions, such as banks, are exposed to interest rate risk due to their lending and borrowing activities.

  • Interest rate risk can impact the institution’s net interest income, asset values, and overall balance sheet.

  • Effective risk management is crucial for financial institutions to mitigate potential losses and maintain stability.

  • Other companies also plan their borrowing based on future cash needs and the rates they will pay.

  • Even with fixed-rate loans, there is still interest rate risk. Interest rate risk exists when interest rates increase between the decision to borrow and actually taking the loan.

  • Managing interest rate risk is crucial for effective planning and avoiding unexpected cash demands.

2.2.1 Managing the Interest Rate Risk of a Loan Using an FRA

2.2.1.1 Single-Payment Loan

XYZ Corporation is a leading manufacturer in the electronics industry. The company is planning to expand its operations and needs to borrow $10 million in six months’ time for a period of one year to finance this expansion.

The CFO of XYZ Corporation is concerned about the potential increase in interest rates over the next six months, which would result in a higher cost of borrowing. Currently, the 1-year interest rate stands at 5%, but financial forecasts indicate that it might rise to 7% in six months.

Hedging Strategy Using FRA:

To hedge this interest rate risk, XYZ Corporation enters into a 6x18 FRA with a bank. The terms of this FRA are as follows:

  • Notional principal: $10 million
  • FRA rate: 5.5% (the current market forward rate for loans commencing in 6 months for a period of 1 year)

Outcome Scenario:

  1. Rates Rise (Hedge is Beneficial):

Suppose the CFO’s fears materialize, and the 1-year interest rate indeed rises to 7% in six months. XYZ Corporation will borrow $10 million from its lender at the then-prevailing rate of 7%.

However, because of the FRA contract, the bank will pay XYZ Corporation the difference in interest between the agreed-upon FRA rate and the actual market rate, based on the notional principal. The cash settlement from the bank helps offset the increased cost of borrowing.

  1. Rates Fall (Hedge has a Cost):

Conversely, if the interest rate falls to 4% in six months, XYZ Corporation would have been better off not entering into the FRA. They still borrow at the lower 4% rate, but now they have to pay the bank the difference between the agreed FRA rate and the actual market rate, based on the notional principal.

Conclusion:

By entering into an FRA, XYZ Corporation has effectively locked in a maximum interest rate for its future loan, thus hedging against the risk of rising interest rates. This strategy illustrates how FRAs can provide businesses with certainty about future interest costs, enabling them to plan their finances more effectively. However, it’s crucial to remember that if rates fall, hedging can result in missed opportunities to benefit from lower borrowing costs.

The payoff of a Forward Rate Agreement (FRA) at maturity can be calculated as follows:

\[ \text{{FRA Payoff}} = \frac{{\text{{Notional Principal}} \times [(\text{{Rate at Maturity}} - \text{{FRA Rate}}) \times (\text{{Days}}/360)]}}{{1 + (\text{{Rate at Maturity}} \times (\text{{Days}}/360))}} \]

Where:

  • Notional Principal is the hypothetical amount of money that the FRA is based on.
  • Rate at Maturity is the actual market interest rate when the FRA matures.
  • FRA Rate is the interest rate agreed upon in the FRA contract.
  • Days is the number of days for which the contract is in effect.

Note that this payoff is calculated assuming a 360-day year, which is a common convention for FRAs. The formula is based on the difference between the FRA Rate and the Rate at Maturity.

Let’s consider the scenario for XYZ Corporation mentioned previously. The company has entered into a 6x18 FRA with the following details:

  • Notional Principal: $10 million
  • FRA Rate: 5.5%
  • The actual market interest rate (Rate at Maturity) when the FRA matures: 7%
  • Days (the contract is for a one-year loan starting in six months): 360

Plugging these numbers into the FRA payoff formula, we get:

\[ \text{{FRA Payoff}} = \frac{{\$10,000,000 \times [(0.07 - 0.055) \times (360/360)]}}{{1 + (0.07 \times (360/360))}} \]

Solving the above expression will give us the payoff from the FRA, which represents the amount that the FRA seller (the bank in this case) has to pay XYZ Corporation because the Rate at Maturity is higher than the FRA Rate. The calculation can be performed similarly if the Rate at Maturity is lower than the FRA Rate.

It will pay back ($10 million - result from previous example) * (1+0.07*360/360).

It effectively pays a rate of ((payback / $10 mil ) -1)*(360/360)

Tip

An FRA or Forward Rate Agreement is a financial contract between two parties to exchange interest payments for a notional principal amount on a future, pre-agreed date. The two numbers, such as “6x18” in a 6x18 FRA, denote the agreement period.

  • The first number, “6”, is the amount of time (in months) from now until the contract’s effective date, which is when the first party borrows or lends the money. In this case, the effective date is 6 months from now.
  • The second number, “18”, is the amount of time (in months) from now until the end of the contract, when the borrowed money would be repaid.

Therefore, a “6x18” FRA means the agreement is for a loan that will start 6 months from now and will end 18 months from now, or the loan period itself is for (18-6) 12 months or one year after a period of six months.

A note for lenders:

  • Lenders can secure a rate on future loans by shorting a Forward Rate Agreement (FRA).
  • However, lenders are often hesitant to use FRAs due to the unpredictability of their customers’ future borrowing needs.
  • Banks that offer floating-rate credit lines may consider using FRAs to secure lending rates.
  • There’s a significant risk for lenders using FRAs because the decision to borrow lies with the borrower, not the lender. If the borrower decides not to take the loan, the lender could face losses.
  • To mitigate this risk, lenders may choose to use options instead of FRAs. The worst-case scenario in using options is the loss of the option premium.

2.2.1.2 Floating-Rate Loan

  • Floating-rate loans reset their interest rates multiple times during the loan term, posing a potential risk for the borrower.
  • To manage rising interest rate risk, a borrower can use a series of Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs), each set to expire on a loan’s rate reset date.
  • An alternative is futures contracts, but they often have specific expirations that may not match the loan’s rate reset dates.
  • For example, Eurodollar futures contracts expire on set dates, and are based on the 90-day Eurodollar rate, which may not match the loan’s 180-day reset period.
  • Although financial institutions often use sophisticated techniques to manage the volatility of futures contracts, these methods may not be suitable for regular borrowers.
  • Swaps might be a more feasible solution for regular borrowers needing to align their interest rate reset dates with derivative expiry dates. Understanding FRAs can aid in grasping the application of swaps in managing interest rate risk.

2.3 Strategies and Applications for Managing Equity Market Risk

  • Stock market volatility poses greater risk compared to bond market volatility.
  • The stock market is generally more liquid than the bond market.
  • Futures contracts can help manage the risk associated with stock market volatility.
  • Futures contracts are typically based on stock market indices, rather than individual stocks.
  • Diversified investors manage risk at the portfolio level and prefer futures on broad-based indices.

2.3.1 Measuring and Managing the Risk of Equities 387

Beta is a measure of a stock’s or a portfolio’s volatility in relation to the market. In other words, it represents the tendency of a security’s returns to respond to swings in the market.

The formula for Beta (β) of a stock or portfolio is:

\[ \beta_{i} = \frac {Cov(R_{i}, R_{m})} {Var(R_{m})} \]

Where:

  • \(\beta_{i}\) is the beta of the stock or portfolio (i)
  • \(Cov(R_{i}, R_{m})\) is the covariance between the return of the stock/portfolio and the return of the market
  • \(Var(R_{m})\) is the variance of the return of the market

In the formula above, ( R_{i} ) represents the return on the stock or portfolio, and ( R_{m} ) is the return on the market.

If Beta is:

  • 1, the security’s price will move with the market
  • less than 1, the security will be less volatile than the market
  • greater than 1, the security’s price will be more volatile than the market.

Beta is used in the capital asset pricing model (CAPM), which calculates the expected return of an asset based on its beta and expected market returns.

To adjust the beta of a portfolio using futures contracts, you need to determine the number of futures contracts to buy or sell. Beta is a measure of the portfolio’s sensitivity to market movements.

The general formula to calculate the number of futures contracts (N) needed is:

\[ N = [(Target Beta - Current Portfolio Beta) * Portfolio Value] / [Futures Price * Futures Beta] \]

Let’s break down the elements of this formula:

  • Target Beta: The desired beta of the portfolio after hedging.
  • Current Portfolio Beta: The beta of the portfolio before the adjustment.
  • Portfolio Value: The total value of the portfolio that you want to hedge.
  • Futures Price: The price of one futures contract.
  • Futures Beta: The beta of the futures contract relative to the market.

Please note that if the result of this formula is positive, it means you should buy futures contracts. Conversely, if the result is negative, it means you should sell futures contracts.

The calculation assumes that the futures contract and the portfolio are based on the same underlying asset (or at least closely correlated assets), so that changes in the price of the portfolio and the futures contract are related. This is why the beta of the futures contract is important – it represents how the futures contract’s price will change relative to the market.

2.3.2 Managing the Risk of an Equity Portfolio 389

Let’s take an example where we have the following data:

  • Current Portfolio Beta: 1.2
  • Target Beta: 0.8
  • Portfolio Value: $1,000,000
  • Futures Price: $50,000
  • Futures Beta: 1.0 (not necessarily 0)

We use the following formula to calculate the number of futures contracts to manage the beta of the portfolio:

\[ N = \frac {(Target Beta - Current Portfolio Beta) * Portfolio Value} {Futures Price * Futures Beta} \]

Substitute the given values into the formula:

\[ N = \frac {(0.8 - 1.2) * 1,000,000} {50,000 * 1.0} \]

Perform the calculations:

\[ N = \frac {-0.4 * 1,000,000} {50,000} = -8 \]

Here, the result of N is -8. This means we need to short 8 futures contracts in order to adjust the portfolio beta to the target of 0.8. A negative value indicates shorting futures contracts because we want to decrease the portfolio’s market risk (beta).

2.3.3 Creating Equity out of Cash 392

  • Stock index futures help create synthetic equity positions, reducing transaction costs and conserving liquidity.
  • A relationship between futures/forwards and the asset is established using a formula connecting the risk-free interest rate and dividends.
  • Synthetic replication of the asset is beneficial in creating a synthetic stock index fund or converting cash into equity while retaining liquidity.

  • It is created by combining risk-free bonds and futures on the desired index.

Here’s a concise version of the scenario, using different values:

On 1st January, an investment firm wants to construct a synthetic index fund of $200 million invested in US stock. The index is the S&P 500, with a dividend yield of 1.5%. A futures contract on the S&P 500 is priced at $3,000 with a multiplier of $50. The position will be held for three months, after which it will be renewed. The US risk-free rate is 2%, and both the risk-free rate and dividend yield are compounded annually.

To calculate the number of futures contracts, we use:

\[ N = \frac{V(1 + r)^t}{qf} = \frac{\$200,000,000(1.02)^\frac{1}{4}}{\$50 \times \$3,000} = 2704.12 \] We can only buy whole futures contracts, so we round up to 2705 contracts. We’re effectively investing $202,345,618.22 in the stock, and we put this much money into risk-free bonds.

If the stock were actually purchased, dividends would be reinvested into additional shares, so the number of shares would grow to:

\[ N' = \frac{N \times q}{(1 + \delta)^t} = \frac{2705 \times \$50}{(1.015)^\frac{1}{4}} = 134,351.32 \] So, the number of shares would grow to 134,559.74.

When the futures contract expires, the futures contracts will have a payoff of $134,559.74 s_t - $202,345,618.22, where \(s_t\) is the index level at expiration.

To settle the futures contract and obtain the market value of the index units, the fund will pay $202,345,618.22, which is the grown value of the risk-free bonds. Therefore, the investment firm ends up with 134,559.74 units of the index, the desired market position.
