6.1 Introduction

Definition

Market risk refers to the risk associated with fluctuations in stock prices, interest rates, exchange rates, and commodity prices.

Managing this risk involves identifying and measuring potential exposures and ensuring they align with an institution’s risk tolerance. Effective management of market risk requires sound judgment, experience, and an understanding of the limitations and strengths of various risk models.

Compared to other types of financial risk, market risk is generally easier to analyze due to the abundance of data and accumulated industry expertise. However, estimating potential losses remains challenging because they rely on historical data and probabilistic assessments of future conditions. Risk management models incorporate historical information alongside forward-looking judgments, establishing a framework to assess and test risk exposure.

Key metrics such as Value at Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES) are commonly used to quantify market risk in a single, interpretable measure. VaR, originally introduced by JPMorgan, has become a standard tool among corporate treasurers, fund managers, and financial institutions. Although regulators have traditionally relied on VaR for capital requirement calculations, recent shifts have seen a preference for ES in certain areas of market risk evaluation.

6.2 Understanding Value at Risk (VaR)

Definition

Value at Risk (VaR) is a statistical measure that estimates the minimum expected loss of a portfolio over a specified time period and confidence level, under normal market conditions.

VaR has become a critical risk measure in global financial markets, offering a quantifiable estimate of potential losses over a defined time horizon. It can be expressed in currency units or as a percentage of portfolio value. Importantly, VaR reflects a minimum expected loss, not the maximum potential loss of the portfolio.

Example

A portfolio with a 5% VaR of $2.2 million over one day implies:

  • A minimum loss of $2.2 million would be expected 5% of the time.
  • Losses would be at least $2.2 million in 5% of cases.
  • 95% of the time, the loss would not exceed $2.2 million.

A typical VaR statement specifies both the frequency of losses and the time period over which they might occur, with a 5% VaR implying a 95% confidence level. This means that in 95% of scenarios, losses should not surpass the VaR amount. While often illustrated with a normal distribution, VaR calculations may use other distributions better suited to the unique characteristics of financial market returns.

Code 
import numpy as np
import plotly.graph_objects as go
from scipy.stats import norm

# Parameters
mu = 0  # Mean of the distribution
sigma = 1  # Standard deviation of the distribution
confidence_level = 0.95  # 95% confidence level
x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 1000)

# Calculate VaR at 5% level (inverse CDF for the normal distribution)
VaR = norm.ppf(1 - confidence_level, mu, sigma)

# Probability density function values
y = norm.pdf(x, mu, sigma)

# Plot the normal distribution and VaR threshold in Plotly
fig = go.Figure()

# Normal distribution curve
fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(x=x, y=y, mode='lines', name='Normal Distribution', line=dict(color='blue')))

# Highlighting the VaR area (left tail of the distribution at 5% level)
x_fill = np.linspace(-4, VaR, 1000)
y_fill = norm.pdf(x_fill, mu, sigma)

fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(
    x=np.append(x_fill, VaR), y=np.append(y_fill, 0),
    mode='lines', name='VaR (95% confidence)', fill='tozeroy',
    line=dict(color='blue'), fillcolor='rgba(0,0,255,0.3)'
))

# Update layout for the graph
fig.update_layout(
    title="Value at Risk (VaR) on a Normal Distribution",
    xaxis_title="Value",
    yaxis_title="Probability Density",
    showlegend=True,
    hovermode="x unified",
    width=745,
    height=600
)

fig.show()

6.3 Advantages and Limitations of VaR

6.3.1 Advantages of VaR

  • Simple and intuitive: VaR offers an easily understood concept that answers the essential question, “How bad can things get?” making it accessible for decision-makers without technical expertise.
  • Clear communication tool: By consolidating complex risk metrics into a single, interpretable number, VaR simplifies the communication of risk to stakeholders.
  • Risk comparability: VaR enables the comparison of risks across various asset classes, portfolios, and trading units, fostering informed decision-making across diverse financial contexts.
  • Supports capital allocation: VaR serves as a benchmark for allocating capital efficiently across different trading units or portfolio positions.
  • Performance evaluation: VaR facilitates risk-adjusted performance measurement, offering a more comprehensive understanding of profitability by adjusting for the inherent risk in portfolios.
  • Backtesting capability: The reliability of VaR can be validated through backtesting, allowing firms to assess the accuracy of their risk estimates based on historical data.
  • Regulatory acceptance: Widely endorsed by regulators, such as the SEC, VaR is frequently featured in financial institutions’ risk disclosures, fostering industry consistency and compliance.

6.3.2 Limitations of VaR

  • Subjectivity in estimation: Estimating VaR involves subjective choices, such as selecting the confidence level, time horizon, and estimation method, which can lead to inconsistency.
  • Underestimates extreme events: VaR may overlook the likelihood of severe adverse events or “left-tail events,” often underrepresenting risk during extreme market downturns.
  • Ignores liquidity risk: VaR may understate risk for illiquid assets, particularly during market stress when these assets are challenging to sell without substantial losses.
  • Sensitivity to correlation shifts: VaR assumes stable correlations among assets, which can be misleading as correlations often spike during market crises, compounding risk.
  • Prone to regime shifts: VaR may underestimate losses during low-volatility periods or trending markets, as it fails to account for shifts in volatility regimes.
  • Misinterpreted as a worst-case measure: VaR does not reflect the worst possible loss and can give a false sense of security, as losses beyond VaR are possible and potentially severe.
  • Oversimplifies risk profiles: While straightforward to communicate, VaR can obscure nuances of the underlying risk structure, providing an incomplete picture of potential outcomes.
  • Neglects right-tail events: VaR emphasizes losses (left-tail events) and disregards potential gains (right-tail events), missing opportunities for a full distributional view of outcomes.
  • Risk of misleading compliance: Traders can meet VaR limits without truly minimizing risk, as skewed strategies can fit within set limits but harbor large, unaccounted losses.
Example

A trader could structure a portfolio with a 99.1% chance of a loss under $10 million but a 0.9% chance of a $500 million loss, meeting a bank’s one-day 99% VaR limit of $10 million while masking potential catastrophic outcomes.

  • Encourages high-risk strategies: Some trading strategies, such as writing out-of-the-money options, offer high returns but carry a low probability of large losses, which can appear benign within VaR limits but introduce hidden risks.
  • Incentive for risk-seeking behavior: Traders motivated by returns may seek ways to engage in high-risk strategies that technically stay within VaR limits, thus exposing firms to heightened risk without breaching formal boundaries.

6.3.3 Extensions of VaR

No single risk model can address all of a risk manager’s concerns, so several extensions of VaR provide additional insights into portfolio risk.

  • Conditional VaR (CVaR, or Expected Shortfall): CVaR estimates the average loss given that the loss has exceeded the VaR threshold, offering a more complete picture of potential extreme losses.
  • Incremental VaR (IVaR): This measures the change in the portfolio’s VaR resulting from adjusting the size of a particular position, helping managers understand how modifications impact overall risk.
  • Marginal VaR (MVaR): MVaR evaluates the effect of a small position change on total portfolio VaR, providing insights into each asset’s contribution to overall risk within diversified portfolios.
  • Relative VaR (or Ex Ante Tracking Error): This extension quantifies the potential deviation of a portfolio’s performance from its benchmark, allowing managers to gauge risk relative to specific performance targets.

IVaR and MVaR are particularly valuable for assessing potential trades before execution, helping managers make proactive adjustments to maintain desired risk levels.

6.4 Expected Shortfall

Definition

Expected Shortfall (ES) is a risk measure that calculates the average loss in scenarios where losses exceed the Value at Risk (VaR) threshold, providing a more comprehensive view of potential extreme losses beyond the VaR estimate.

Expected Shortfall (ES), also known as conditional Value at Risk, conditional tail expectation, or expected tail loss, is an alternative risk measure to Value at Risk (VaR) that offers improved incentives for traders by focusing on the severity of extreme losses. Unlike VaR, which estimates the maximum loss at a specified confidence level, ES provides a more comprehensive risk assessment by averaging losses that exceed the VaR threshold. This makes ES particularly valuable for understanding the risk of rare but significant losses.

To compute ES, it is necessary to first determine VaR, as ES is the expected loss over a time horizon \(T\) given that the loss exceeds the VaR. ES depends on two parameters: the time horizon \(T\) and the confidence level \(X\). For example, if \(X = 99\%\), \(T\) is 10 days, and the VaR is $64 million, then ES represents the average loss over those 10 days when losses exceed $64 million.

Setting ES limits can reduce excessive risk-taking, as ES better captures the tail risks in a portfolio. It also has desirable mathematical properties, such as always acknowledging the benefits of diversification. However, ES can be more challenging to understand and implement compared to VaR, and back-testing procedures for ES are also more complex.

Alternative risk measures, such as the exponential spectral risk measure, adjust ES by using different weightings rather than equal weights across all extreme losses.

6.5 Coherent Risk Measures

Coherent risk measures offer a consistent and reliable framework for assessing risk by incorporating the benefits of diversification and ensuring that scaling a portfolio proportionally adjusts the risk measure. Expected Shortfall (ES) is an example of a coherent risk measure, while Value at Risk (VaR) is not coherent as it does not satisfy all coherence properties, specifically subadditivity.

A coherent risk measure is characterized by four fundamental axioms:

  1. Monotonicity: If portfolio \(A\) consistently outperforms portfolio \(B\), the risk measure for \(A\) should be less than or equal to that of \(B\).
  2. Subadditivity: The risk measure of a combined portfolio \(A + B\) should be less than or equal to the sum of the individual risk measures of \(A\) and \(B\), rewarding diversification.
  3. Positive Homogeneity: Scaling a portfolio by a factor \(\lambda\) proportionally scales the risk measure. Thus, the risk measure of \(\lambda A\) is \(\lambda\) times that of \(A\) for any \(\lambda > 0\).
  4. Translation Invariance: Adding a risk-free asset with return \(r\) to a portfolio should decrease the risk measure by \(r\). For portfolio \(A\), the risk measure of \(A + r\) is the risk measure of \(A\) minus \(r\).

These axioms ensure that coherent risk measures, such as ES, provide a robust assessment of risk, particularly in recognizing the advantages of diversification.

6.5.1 Subadditivity Example

Consider two independent projects, each with a 2% probability of incurring a $10 million loss and a 98% probability of a $1 million loss within one year.

  • The one-year 97.5% VaR (Value at Risk) for each project is $1 million.

When these projects are combined into a portfolio, the loss distribution changes as follows:

  • A 0.04% probability of a $20 million loss,
  • A 3.92% probability of an $11 million loss,
  • A 96.04% probability of a $2 million loss.

In this combined portfolio, the one-year 97.5% VaR is $11 million, exceeding the sum of the individual VaRs ($2 million) by $9 million, thereby violating the subadditivity property.

For Expected Shortfall (ES), each project’s 97.5% ES considers a 2% chance of a $10 million loss and a 0.5% chance of a $1 million loss, leading to an expected loss of $8.2 million per project. For the combined portfolio, the ES considers:

  • A 0.04% chance of a $20 million loss,
  • A 2.46% chance of an $11 million loss.

This results in a portfolio ES of $11.144 million. Here, the sum of the individual project ES values ($16.4 million) is greater than the portfolio ES ($11.144 million), thereby satisfying the subadditivity property.

6.6 Choice of Parameters for VaR and ES

For both Value at Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES), users must specify two key parameters: the time horizon and the confidence level. Typically, it is assumed that changes in portfolio value at the time horizon follow a normal distribution, though this assumption may not always hold.

When the portfolio loss distribution has a mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), VaR at confidence level \(X\) is calculated as:

\[ \text{VaR} = \mu + \sigma N^{-1}(X) \]

where \(N^{-1}(\cdot)\) denotes the inverse cumulative normal distribution. For short time horizons, \(\mu\) is often assumed to be zero, simplifying VaR to be directly proportional to \(\sigma\).

Similarly, if the portfolio loss is normally distributed with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), ES at confidence level \(X\) is given by:

\[ \text{ES} = \mu + \sigma \frac{e^{-(Y^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} (1 - X)} \]

where \(Y\) is the \(X\)th percentile of the standard normal distribution.

Example

Assume the loss from a portfolio over a 10-day horizon is normally distributed with a mean of zero and a standard deviation of $20 million. The calculations for 10-day 99% VaR and ES are:

\[ \text{VaR} = 20 \times N^{-1}(0.99) = 20 \times 2.326 = \$46.5 \text{ million} \]

\[ \text{ES} = 20 \times \frac{e^{-(2.326^2/2)}}{\sqrt{2\pi} \times 0.01} = \$53.3 \text{ million} \]

These values provide a clearer assessment of potential losses, with VaR representing the threshold loss and ES giving the expected average loss in extreme scenarios.

6.6.1 The Time Horizon

The choice of time horizon for VaR and ES depends on the liquidity and trading frequency of the assets in the portfolio. Liquid, actively traded positions typically use shorter time horizons, while less liquid portfolios may require longer horizons. For portfolios with assets of varying liquidity, analysts may apply tailored risk measures to each asset.

A common starting point is a 1-day time horizon, from which T-day VaR and T-day ES can be calculated using the following formulas:

\[ \text{T-day VaR} = \text{1-day VaR} \times \sqrt{T} \]

\[ \text{T-day ES} = \text{1-day ES} \times \sqrt{T} \]

These formulas hold precisely when daily portfolio value changes are independently and identically normally distributed with a mean of zero. In other cases, these calculations serve as approximations.

6.6.2 Impact of Autocorrelation

Portfolio value changes are not always independent. When portfolio returns exhibit higher autocorrelation, VaR and ES calculations may underestimate risk. To adjust for autocorrelation, the standard deviation \(\sigma\) can be modified when calculating VaR and ES. The adjusted standard deviation for the sum of \(T\) daily changes, considering autocorrelation, is given by:

\[ \sigma \sqrt{T + 2(T - 1)\rho^1 + 2(T - 2)\rho^2 + \dots + 2\rho^{T-1}} \]

6.6.3 Confidence Level

The confidence level for VaR and ES depends on risk tolerance and objectives, such as maintaining a particular credit rating. For example, a bank aiming for an AA rating might select a 99.98% confidence level to match a 0.02% annual default probability. Estimating VaR directly at very high confidence levels can be challenging, but extreme value theory offers methods to improve accuracy in such cases.

6.6.4 Back-Testing

Back-testing assesses the accuracy of VaR models by comparing model predictions with actual historical losses. This process is straightforward for VaR, as it relies on tracking “exceptions” where observed losses exceed the predicted VaR. A deviation from expected exception rates may reveal model weaknesses. Statistical tests help determine if a model should be adjusted or rejected based on these exceptions, and regulators may require increased market risk capital if back-testing results over a 250-day period are unsatisfactory.

6.7 Additional Topics

6.7.1 Interest Rate Risk

Financial institutions often have exposure to multiple yield curves, necessitating the calculation of VaR and ES to manage this risk. Two common approaches include treating each interest rate as a separate risk factor or applying principal components analysis (PCA). The bank’s net interest margin, which measures the difference between earned and paid interest, is a key focus of asset/liability management to maintain stability.

Duration is a crucial measure, assessing portfolio sensitivity to shifts in the zero-coupon yield curve, while convexity adjustments add precision for large parallel shifts (but less so for nonparallel shifts). Multiple duration measures (or “deltas”) capture different potential yield curve changes. PCA can serve as an alternative to multiple deltas, effectively hedging against common yield curve shifts.

6.7.2 Derivatives Risk

In managing derivatives risk, traders monitor Greek letters such as delta, gamma, and vega to stay within risk limits. Delta (\(\Delta\)) represents the rate of change in portfolio value relative to changes in the underlying asset’s price. Delta hedging, a fundamental practice, involves creating a delta-neutral position by taking an offsetting position of \(-\Delta\) in the underlying asset. Complex portfolios require frequent rebalancing to maintain delta neutrality.

Beyond delta, traders examine gamma (the rate of change of delta) and vega (sensitivity to volatility), which can be adjusted through options trading. Daily rebalancing is typical for delta neutrality, though maintaining gamma and vega neutrality is less feasible and is managed through corrective actions if these measures exceed limits.

6.7.3 Scenario Analysis and Stress Testing

Stress testing plays a critical role in risk management by evaluating the impacts of extreme scenarios typically beyond the scope of VaR and ES analysis. This approach can reveal potential systemic issues and aid in risk mitigation. Common scenario generation methods include:

  • Testing the impact of extreme movements in a single market variable,
  • Using historical data from periods of extreme market shocks,
  • Constructing plausible extremes through a committee of senior management and economists,
  • Reverse stress testing with algorithms to uncover large-loss scenarios.

Scenario analysis also considers knock-on effects, such as market turmoil leading to a liquidity shortage or flight to quality. Regulators in the US, EU, and UK develop standardized stress scenarios for large financial institutions, which may prompt capital-raising measures. Integrating stress testing with VaR through subjective probability weighting remains largely unexplored by institutions and regulators.

6.8 Practice Questions and Problems

  1. What is the difference between expected shortfall and VaR? What is the theoretical advantage of expected shortfall over VaR?

  2. A fund manager announces that the fund’s one-month 95% VaR is 6% of the size of the portfolio being managed. You have an investment of $100,000 in the fund. How do you interpret the portfolio manager’s announcement?

  3. A fund manager announces that the fund’s one-month 95% expected shortfall is 6% of the size of the portfolio being managed. You have an investment of $100,000 in the fund. How do you interpret the portfolio manager’s announcement?

  4. Suppose that each of two investments has a 0.9% chance of a loss of $10 million and a 99.1% chance of a loss of $1 million. The investments are independent of each other.

    • What is the VaR for one of the investments when the confidence level is 99%?
    • What is the expected shortfall for one of the investments when the confidence level is 99%?
    • What is the VaR for a portfolio consisting of the two investments when the confidence level is 99%?
    • What is the expected shortfall for a portfolio consisting of the two investments when the confidence level is 99%?
    • Show that in this example VaR does not satisfy the subadditivity condition, whereas expected shortfall does.

  5. Suppose that the change in the value of a portfolio over a one-day time period is normal with a mean of zero and a standard deviation of $2 million; what is (a) the one-day 97.5% VaR, (b) the five-day 97.5% VaR, and (c) the five-day 99% VaR?

  6. What difference does it make to your answer to Problem 5 if there is first-order daily autocorrelation with a correlation parameter equal to 0.16?

  7. The change in the value of a portfolio in one month is normally distributed with a mean of zero and a standard deviation of $2 million. Calculate the VaR and ES for a confidence level of 98% and a time horizon of three months.

  8. Suppose that each of two investments has a 4% chance of a loss of $10 million, a 2% chance of a loss of $1 million, and a 94% chance of a profit of $1 million. They are independent of each other.

    • What is the VaR for one of the investments when the confidence level is 95%?
    • What is the expected shortfall when the confidence level is 95%?
    • What is the VaR for a portfolio consisting of the two investments when the confidence level is 95%?
    • What is the expected shortfall for a portfolio consisting of the two investments when the confidence level is 95%?
    • Show that, in this example, VaR does not satisfy the subadditivity condition, whereas expected shortfall does.

  9. Suppose the first-order autocorrelation for daily changes in the value of a portfolio is 0.12. The 10-day VaR, calculated by multiplying the one-day VaR by \(\sqrt{10}\), is $2 million. What is a better estimate of the VaR that takes account of autocorrelation?

  10. The change in the value of a portfolio in three months is normally distributed with a mean of $500,000 and a standard deviation of $3 million. Calculate the VaR and ES for a confidence level of 99.5% and a time horizon of three months.
